Core Principles

Consistency Beats Heroics

The rider who logs steady weeks for months out-performs the one who crams big rides before an event and arrives cooked.

Why It Works

Aerobic adaptations — capillaries, mitochondria, blood volume — build slowly and reward repeated stimulus. Cramming mostly produces fatigue and saddle sores.

  • Frequency first: 3–5 rides/week, even short ones, beat one weekend epic.
  • Ride through life: a 45-minute spin on a busy day keeps fitness ticking.
  • Think in blocks: real gains show across 8–16 week build phases.
Progressive Overload

The body adapts to stress plus recovery. Raise volume and intensity gradually so adaptation outpaces breakdown.

How To Apply
  • ~10% rule: grow weekly hours/distance modestly, not in leaps.
  • One lever at a time: add volume or intensity, rarely both at once.
  • Recovery weeks: every 3–4 weeks, cut volume ~30% to absorb the work.
Polarize Your Intensity

Most riding should be genuinely easy; a small slice should be genuinely hard. The "medium" zone feels productive but builds fatigue without much fitness.

The 80/20 Split
  • ~80% easy: conversational pace, you could chat in full sentences.
  • ~20% hard: threshold and VO2 efforts that actually hurt.
  • Avoid the gray zone: "always kind-of-hard" is the classic amateur trap.
Specificity Closer to the Event

General fitness early; event-like riding in the final 6–8 weeks. Train the demands you'll actually face.

What That Means
  • TT prep: sustained efforts at goal power in the aero position.
  • Fondo prep: long rides with climbs and event-day fueling rehearsed.
  • Course study: know the terrain, wind, and feed-zone spacing.
Recovery Is Where You Improve

Training is the stimulus; rest is the adaptation. Without it you accumulate fatigue, not fitness.

Non-Negotiables
  • Sleep 7–9 hr: the single most powerful recovery tool.
  • Easy days easy: resist turning recovery spins into races.
  • Taper before events: cut volume, keep a little intensity, arrive fresh.
Enjoyment Is a Training Metric

The plan you look forward to is the plan you'll complete. Sustainable beats optimal-on-paper.

Keep It Fun
  • Mix routes: new roads, group rides, and cafe stops fight staleness.
  • Chase process goals: "ride 4×/week" beats only chasing finish times.
  • Listen to motivation: a dip in desire often signals a needed rest.

Bike Fit & Setup

Why Fit Comes First

A bike that fits lets you ride longer, more comfortably, and with more power. Comfort beats aero for almost every amateur.

Payoff
  • Comfort: fewer numb hands, sore necks, and aching knees.
  • Power: good leg extension lets you push efficiently for hours.
  • Worth it: a professional fit is the best money most riders spend.
Saddle Height & Position

The saddle sets your leg extension — the foundation of an efficient, injury-free pedal stroke.

Starting Points
  • Height: slight knee bend (~25–35°) at the bottom of the stroke; hips shouldn't rock.
  • Fore/aft: knee roughly over the pedal axle with the crank horizontal.
  • Adjust small: move 2–3 mm at a time and test over several rides.
  • Knee pain at the front often means saddle too low; back of the knee, too high.
Reach & Contact Points

Bars, saddle, and pedals are your three contact points. Each one, dialed in, removes a source of pain.

Tune These
  • Reach: a relaxed bend in the elbows; not stretched flat or cramped up.
  • Bar height: raise it for comfort — slammed stems wreck amateur backs and necks.
  • Saddle choice: width matched to your sit bones matters more than padding.
  • Cleats: set for a neutral foot; misalignment causes knee and hotfoot pain.

Intensity & Training Zones

FTP — Your Anchor Number

Functional Threshold Power is roughly the power you can hold for an hour. Nearly all power zones are a percentage of it.

Working With FTP
  • Test it: a 20-minute all-out effort × 0.95 is the common estimate.
  • Retest: every 6–8 weeks of training; it moves as you improve.
  • W/kg matters: watts per kilogram predicts climbing better than raw watts.
  • No meter? use heart rate and perceived effort — see the next cards.
Z1–Z2 · Endurance

The aerobic base. ~55–75% FTP, ~60–75% max HR. Conversational — the bulk of your riding lives here.

What It Builds
  • Fat burning: trains the body to spare glycogen on long efforts.
  • Durability: the engine behind every fondo and century.
  • Check: if you can't talk in sentences, you're going too hard.
Z3 · Tempo

"Comfortably hard." ~76–90% FTP. Useful but easy to overuse — this is the gray zone to ration.

Use With Care
  • Good for: fondo-pace rehearsal and sustained climbing feel.
  • The trap: drifting every ride into tempo blurs the 80/20 split.
  • Talk test: short phrases only, not full conversation.
Z4 · Threshold

Right around FTP, ~91–105%. The time-trial zone — sustainable for 10–40 min when fit, and deeply specific to TT performance.

Why Train It
  • Raises the ceiling: pushes up the power you can hold for an hour.
  • TT-specific: the exact demand of a 20–40 km time trial.
  • Sample set: 3 × 10 min at threshold, 5 min easy between.
Z5+ · VO2 & Anaerobic

Above ~106% FTP. Genuinely hard — 30 sec to 5 min efforts that build top-end power and raise your aerobic ceiling.

Handle With Respect
  • Big stimulus: a little goes a long way; 1–2 sessions/week max.
  • Sample set: 5 × 3 min hard, 3 min easy spinning between.
  • Recover fully: these need fresh legs and real rest after.
Heart Rate vs. Power

Power shows what you're doing now; heart rate shows how your body is responding. Best riders use both.

Know The Quirks
  • HR lags: it takes 1–2 min to catch up to a hard effort.
  • HR drifts: heat, fatigue, and caffeine all push it up at the same power.
  • Cardiac drift: HR rising on a steady ride signals dehydration or fatigue.
  • No gadgets? perceived effort (RPE 1–10) still trains you well.

Workout Types

Endurance / Base Rides

Long, steady Z2 rides. The backbone of every training week and the single biggest predictor of event success.

How To Do It
  • Duration: 1.5–5 hr depending on your event and fitness.
  • Discipline: stay in zone even when you feel good — that's the point.
  • Build the long ride: grow it gradually toward ~⅔ of event distance.
Threshold Intervals

Sustained efforts at or near FTP. The most TT-specific session and the fastest way to raise your hour power.

How To Do It
  • Sets: 2–4 × 8–20 min at Z4, with 4–6 min easy between.
  • Aero practice: hold your TT position during the work intervals.
  • Pacing: finish each interval able to do one more — don't blow up early.
VO2 Max Intervals

Short, very hard efforts that lift your ceiling — making every lower zone feel easier.

How To Do It
  • Sets: 4–6 × 3–5 min hard, equal easy recovery.
  • Effort: hard but evenly paced — not a sprint that fades.
  • Frequency: once a week in a build block; skip if legs are flat.
Hill Repeats

Climbing builds strength and threshold together, and rehearses the terrain most rallies throw at you.

How To Do It
  • Sets: 4–8 × 3–6 min climbs at threshold to VO2, easy descent recovery.
  • Cadence: practice both seated grinding and standing — you'll need both.
  • Form: stay relaxed in the upper body; let the legs do the work.
Cadence & Spin-Ups

Drills that smooth your pedal stroke and widen the cadence range you can ride efficiently.

How To Do It
  • High-cadence: 1–2 min at 100–110 rpm, easy gear, no bouncing.
  • Low-cadence strength: 5–10 min at 50–60 rpm, moderate effort, seated.
  • Single-leg drills: expose dead spots in the stroke (use a trainer).
Recovery Spins

Very easy, short rides that promote blood flow without adding training stress.

How To Do It
  • Effort: Z1, embarrassingly easy — ego is the enemy here.
  • Duration: 30–60 min, flat, small gears, high-ish cadence.
  • Or just rest: a true day off is a valid recovery "workout."

Event Distances

Time Trial · 10–40 km

"The race of truth" — you, the clock, and a sustained threshold effort. No drafting, no hiding.

The Demand
  • Energy: steady Z4; 15 min to ~1 hr depending on distance.
  • Key skill: even pacing and holding an aero position under load.
  • Trains best with: threshold intervals — see the Time Trial Focus below.
Club Rally / Charity Ride · 30–60 mi

Social, supported, not a race. The most common amateur event — a good day out with feed zones and friends.

The Demand
  • Energy: mostly Z2 endurance with surges on hills and into wind.
  • Key skill: group riding, drafting etiquette, and steady fueling.
  • Prep: a few long rides near distance; rehearse eating on the bike.
Metric Century · 100 km / 62 mi

A genuine endurance challenge that's achievable for most with a sensible build. A great stepping-stone goal.

The Demand
  • Energy: 3–4+ hr of Z2 — durability and fueling decide your day.
  • Key skill: pacing the first half conservatively; nutrition discipline.
  • Prep: longest training ride of ~40–50 mi before event day.
Gran Fondo · 60–120 mi

A mass-participation ride, often timed on key climbs. Festive atmosphere, real distance, and serious elevation.

The Demand
  • Energy: long Z2 with repeated threshold efforts on the climbs.
  • Key skill: climbing pacing, descending confidence, fuel logistics.
  • Trains best with: see the Rally & Gran Fondo Focus below.
Century · 100 mi

The classic bucket-list ride. 5–7 hr in the saddle — as much a fueling and comfort test as a fitness one.

The Demand
  • Energy: patient Z2; the back half is won by who fueled and paced best.
  • Key skill: on-bike eating, bike-fit comfort, and mental pacing.
  • Prep: build the long ride to ~70–80 mi; train your gut to absorb carbs.
Distance Reference

A rough sense of duration and weekly volume typical amateurs need to finish comfortably.

Ballpark Targets
  • 40 km TT: ~60–80 min effort · 4–6 hr/week training.
  • Metric century: ~3.5–5 hr · 5–7 hr/week, long ride ~3 hr.
  • Century / fondo: ~5–8 hr · 7–10 hr/week, long ride 4–5 hr.
  • Highly individual — terrain, wind, and fitness shift these a lot.

Time Trial Focus Event Plan

The Core Challenge

A TT is a pacing and position puzzle. The fitness matters, but most amateurs lose time by going out too hard and sitting up in the wind.

Win The Margins
  • Pacing: a few watts too hot in the first 5 min costs minutes later.
  • Aerodynamics: at TT speeds, ~70–80% of your effort fights air.
  • Free speed: position and a tucked, still upper body beat new gear.
8-Week Build Sketch

Assumes a base of ~3–4 rides/week. Adjust to your life — consistency over perfection.

The Arc
  • Wk 1–3: build threshold volume — 3×10 → 3×15 min at Z4.
  • Wk 4: recovery week, light, retest FTP.
  • Wk 5–7: race-specific — 2×20 min at goal power, in aero.
  • Wk 8: taper — short, sharp openers; arrive rested.
Race-Day Pacing

The discipline is starting slower than feels right. Adrenaline makes goal power feel easy for the first 10 minutes — it isn't.

The Plan
  • First ¼: hold ~goal power even though you could do more.
  • Middle ½: settle into goal power, stay aero, relax the face and hands.
  • Last ¼: lift gradually; empty the tank only in the final km.
  • Climbs/headwind: push slightly above; descents/tailwind, ease slightly.
The Warm-Up

A short TT needs a real warm-up — you must be ready to hit threshold from the start line, not 10 minutes in.

~20–30 min Routine
  • Ease in: 10 min easy spinning to open the legs.
  • Build: 5 min rising from Z2 to Z3.
  • Primers: 2–3 × 1 min at race pace, full recovery between.
  • Settle: easy spin until your start, stay warm.
Position & Equipment

You don't need a TT bike. Clip-on aero bars and a dialed position deliver most of the gain for little money.

Priority Order
  • 1. Position: low and narrow, but only as aggressive as you can hold and breathe.
  • 2. Clothing: a snug skinsuit beats a flapping jersey.
  • 3. Helmet: an aero or well-vented road helmet, head still and low.
  • 4. Wheels last: deeper wheels help, but they're the smallest lever.
Common TT Mistakes

Most lost time comes from a handful of avoidable errors, not a lack of fitness.

Avoid These
  • Too hot a start: the number-one amateur TT error.
  • Sitting up: every time you break position you bleed speed.
  • Coasting corners: brake late, carry speed, get back on the gas fast.
  • No warm-up: the first 5 min are then wasted just getting going.

Rally & Gran Fondo Focus

The Core Challenge

A long ride is an endurance and logistics test. The fittest rider doesn't win the day — the best-fueled, best-paced one does.

What Decides Your Day
  • Durability: the ability to still ride well in hour four.
  • Fueling: bonking ends days; steady carbs prevent it.
  • Pacing: riding the first half too hard guarantees a grim second half.
10–12 Week Build Sketch

The goal is a long ride that grows steadily toward ⅔–¾ of event distance.

The Arc
  • Early: build weekly volume and the long ride; mostly Z2.
  • Middle: add tempo and hill repeats; long rides on event-like terrain.
  • Late: a few "dress rehearsal" rides with full event-day fueling.
  • Final 1–2 wk: taper volume, keep some intensity, rest up.
Ride-Day Pacing

Ride the first half like you're holding back. You should feel almost too fresh at the midpoint.

The Plan
  • First half: easy Z2; let fast groups go if they're over your pace.
  • Climbs: steady tempo, not heroics — never redline early.
  • Second half: if you fueled right, you can lift the pace here.
  • Feed zones: stop briefly, top off bottles, eat — don't skip them.
The Fueling Plan

Eat early and often, before you feel you need to. Once you're hungry on the bike, you're already behind.

The Rules
  • Carbs: 60–90 g/hour from food, gels, or drink mix.
  • Start at 20 min: begin fueling before the first climb.
  • Fluid: ~1 bottle/hour, more in heat; add electrolytes.
  • Rehearse the exact plan in training — never try new fuel on event day.
Climbing & Descending

Fondos are decided on the hills — climb within yourself and descend with calm confidence.

On The Gradients
  • Climb seated mostly: it's more efficient; stand to stretch or surge.
  • Gear low enough: spin the climbs; grinding cooks your legs.
  • Descend relaxed: look through the corner, brake before it, not in it.
  • Recover on descents: drink, eat, and let the HR drop.
Logistics & Self-Sufficiency

Support exists, but be able to handle a flat and a bad patch on your own.

Be Ready
  • Carry: 2 tubes/plugs, levers, mini-pump or CO₂, multitool, ID, cash/card.
  • Know the route: climbs, feed zones, and cutoff times in advance.
  • Pre-ride check: tires, brakes, drivetrain — fix issues days before, not at the start.
  • Bad patch plan: eat, drink, ease off — most rough spells pass in 20 min.

Pacing & Strategy

Even Effort, Not Even Speed

Pace by effort and power, not the speedometer. Speed swings with wind and gradient; your effort shouldn't.

How To Ride It
  • Uphill/headwind: let speed drop, hold power steady.
  • Downhill/tailwind: keep pedaling, but don't chase a number.
  • Result: smoother energy use and a stronger finish.
Negative Splits

Ride the second half as fast or faster than the first. It's the most reliable way to a good time and a good day.

Why It Works
  • Banks energy: a controlled start leaves something for the finish.
  • Feels better: passing riders late beats being passed.
  • Discipline: the hard part is going easy enough early on.
Read the Wind & Terrain

Wind is the invisible hill. Plan your effort and your group position around it.

Tactics
  • Headwind out: a route that's into the wind early gives a tailwind home.
  • Crosswinds: shelter behind and slightly to the side of other riders.
  • Save matches: spend effort into wind and uphill, recover with it behind you.
Manage Your Matches

Every hard surge is a "match" you can't relight. You have a limited book — spend them deliberately.

Spend Wisely
  • Worth it: bridging to a good group, a timed climb, the final push.
  • Not worth it: chasing every accelerating rider, surging out of every corner.
  • Smooth is fast: steady riders beat surgers over distance.
Have a Plan A, B, and C

Decide your goals before the start so a tough day doesn't become a disaster.

Three Tiers
  • Plan A: the ideal — your target time or pace.
  • Plan B: still a good day if conditions or legs aren't perfect.
  • Plan C: just finish safely — always a valid, successful outcome.
Rehearse Everything

Event day should hold no surprises. Practice the kit, the fuel, and the routine in training.

Dress Rehearsal
  • Nothing new on the day: not food, not shorts, not a saddle.
  • Practice logistics: early starts, pre-ride meal, warm-up timing.
  • Trust the prep: a rehearsed plan calms event-morning nerves.

Skills & Cadence

Find Your Cadence

Most riders are efficient at 85–95 rpm. Higher spins the cardio system; lower loads the legs.

Use It
  • Default: hold a smooth, light pedal stroke around 90 rpm.
  • Climbs: shift down to keep cadence up — don't let it crawl below 60.
  • Train the range: drills make both high and low cadence feel natural.
Smart Gear Use

Gears exist to keep cadence steady as the terrain changes. Anticipate, don't react.

Shifting Habits
  • Shift early: drop gears before the hill, not halfway up under load.
  • Ease the pedals when shifting: a soft pedal stroke saves your drivetrain.
  • Gear for the course: a wide-range cassette is no shame — it's smart.
Cornering Confidence

Smooth cornering is free speed and safety. Most amateurs brake too late and too much.

The Technique
  • Brake before: set your speed on the straight, then release the brakes.
  • Look through: eyes to the exit; the bike follows your gaze.
  • Outside pedal down: weight it, inside knee out, stay relaxed.
Descending

Descend relaxed, not rigid. Tension makes you slow and unsafe; calm makes you smooth.

Stay Composed
  • Hands in the drops: better control and braking leverage.
  • Loose grip, low body: let the bike move beneath you.
  • Brake straight, not in the turn: and feather both brakes evenly.
  • Within your limits: never ride a descent faster than you can see.
Bike Handling Basics

Confident handling lets you ride safely in groups and react calmly to surprises.

Skills To Drill
  • Look back without swerving: practice on quiet roads.
  • Eat & drink one-handed: essential for long events.
  • Hold a line: ride predictably and straight, especially in a bunch.
  • Emergency stops: know how hard you can brake before the rear lifts.
Smooth Pedaling

Aim for round, quiet pedaling — no stomping, no dead spots, no bouncing in the saddle.

Cues
  • Relax the upper body: tension in the shoulders wastes energy.
  • Pedal in circles: apply light pressure through the whole stroke.
  • Quiet hips: rocking means the saddle is too high.

Gear & Bike

Spend Priorities

For amateurs, the value order is fit, tires, comfort, then aero — not the other way around.

Where Money Works Hardest
  • 1. Bike fit: comfort and power for the price of one component.
  • 2. Good tires: the cheapest meaningful speed and grip upgrade.
  • 3. Contact points: saddle, shorts, shoes that actually suit you.
  • 4. Aero gear last: wheels and frames are the smallest real-world gain.
Tires & Pressure

Tires are your only contact with the road — and most riders run them too hard.

Get It Right
  • Go wider: 28–32 mm is fast, comfortable, and grippy on real roads.
  • Lower pressure: rougher roads and lighter riders want less psi than the sidewall max.
  • Check before every ride: tires lose pressure overnight.
Clothing & Layers

Dress for 10 minutes into the ride, not the cold start. Good kit is comfort, not vanity.

The Essentials
  • Quality shorts: a good chamois is the difference on long rides — and go commando.
  • Layer up top: a base layer, jersey, and packable wind/rain shell.
  • Extremities: gloves, and toe covers / cap when it's cold.
  • Be seen: bright colours by day; reflective detail by night.
Always Carry

A small repair kit turns a ride-ending flat into a five-minute stop.

Saddle Bag Checklist
  • Flat kit: 1–2 tubes (or plugs for tubeless), levers, patches.
  • Inflation: mini-pump or CO₂ with a spare cartridge.
  • Tools: a multitool with the sizes your bike uses.
  • Just in case: ID, a card or cash, phone, a spare quick link.
Basic Maintenance

A little upkeep keeps the bike fast, quiet, and reliable — and prevents event-day disasters.

The Routine
  • Clean & lube the chain: a dry, clean drivetrain is faster and lasts longer.
  • "ABC" pre-ride check: Air, Brakes, Chain — every time.
  • Listen: new creaks and clicks are the bike asking for attention.
  • Pre-event service: shop visit a week out, not the night before.
The Helmet & Glasses

Non-negotiable kit: a well-fitted helmet and eye protection on every ride.

Fit & Care
  • Helmet fit: level, snug, two fingers above the brows; replace after any crash.
  • Glasses: shield eyes from grit, bugs, sun, and wind-induced tearing.
  • Don't cheap out on fit: a helmet you leave loose protects nothing.

Tech & Data

Power Meter

A power meter shows your true effort, instantly — the best tool for pacing and structured training.

What It Gives You
  • Honest pacing: can't fake watts on a climb or into wind.
  • Precise intervals: hit exact target zones in workouts.
  • Worth it for: riders chasing TT times or following a real plan.
Heart Rate Monitor

The affordable training tool — shows how hard your body is actually working.

Use It Well
  • Chest strap: more accurate than wrist optical for intervals.
  • Set zones: from a real max-HR or threshold test, not a formula.
  • Watch trends: a high morning HR can flag under-recovery.
GPS Computer

A bike computer handles navigation and data so you can focus on riding.

Why It Helps
  • Routing: follow event courses without stopping to check a phone.
  • Live data: speed, distance, power, HR, gradient at a glance.
  • Records the ride: review and learn from your pacing afterward.
Indoor Trainer

A smart trainer makes every workout count — no traffic, no coasting, no weather excuses.

Best Used For
  • Precise intervals: structured efforts with zero interruptions.
  • Bad-weather base: keeps consistency through winter and storms.
  • Time-crunched: a focused 60 min indoors beats a soft 90 outside.
  • Indoor riding runs hot — use a fan and drink more than you think.
Don't Drown in Data

Numbers serve the riding, not the other way around. A few metrics, used well, beat dashboards you ignore.

Keep Perspective
  • Track the trend: weekly hours and freshness matter more than any single ride.
  • Feel still counts: RPE and how the legs feel are real data too.
  • Avoid the spiral: don't let a "bad" file ruin a perfectly good ride.
Recovery Metrics

Simple readiness signals help you decide when to push and when to back off.

Worth Watching
  • Resting HR: elevated by 5+ bpm can mean fatigue or illness brewing.
  • Sleep: the most predictive recovery metric there is.
  • Mood & motivation: a persistent dip is a signal, not a weakness.

Fueling & Hydration

Before the Ride

Top off the tank with a carb-focused meal 2–3 hours before a long ride or event.

Pre-Ride Plan
  • 2–3 hr out: familiar carbs, moderate protein, low fat and fiber.
  • Short rides: under ~90 min you can ride well fasted or on a small snack.
  • Hydrate early: arrive at the start already topped up, not chugging at the line.
During — Carbs

For rides over ~90 min, eat 60–90 g of carbs per hour, starting early before you feel low.

How To Hit It
  • Mix sources: drink mix, gels, chews, bananas, rice cakes, real food.
  • Start at ~20 min: don't wait for hunger — by then you're behind.
  • Train your gut: practice high carb intake in training; it's a trainable skill.
  • Bonking — sudden empty-tank exhaustion — is a fueling failure, not weakness.
During — Fluids & Salt

Aim for roughly one bottle per hour, more in heat, with electrolytes on long or hot rides.

Hydration Rules
  • Sip steadily: small, regular drinks beat occasional big gulps.
  • Add sodium: electrolyte mix or tabs for rides over ~2 hr or in heat.
  • Watch the heat: needs can double when it's hot — plan refill stops.
  • Don't overdo it: drowning yourself in plain water dilutes your sodium.
After the Ride

Recovery starts with carbs, protein, and fluids in the hours after a hard or long ride.

Refuel Well
  • Soon-ish: a carb + protein snack within an hour or two helps you recover.
  • Then a real meal: the day's total intake matters more than perfect timing.
  • Rehydrate: replace fluids with some sodium, especially after sweaty rides.
Caffeine

A modest, well-timed dose of caffeine is one of the few genuinely useful performance aids.

Use It Smartly
  • Timing: before a key effort or for a late-ride lift.
  • Moderate dose: more isn't better — jitters and gut upset await.
  • Test in training: know how your body and stomach respond first.
Don't Experiment on Event Day

The golden rule: nothing new on event day. Your gut needs to know every gel and bar already.

Why It Matters
  • GI distress: the most common reason for a ruined long ride.
  • Rehearse fully: use long training rides to lock in your fuel plan.
  • Feed-zone caution: unfamiliar event food is a gamble — carry your own.

Group Riding

The Draft Advantage

Sitting behind another rider can cut your effort by 25–40%. It's the biggest free speed in cycling.

Use It Well
  • Close but safe: a steady gap to the wheel ahead — closer saves more, but stay in control.
  • Share the work: rotate turns at the front so no one gets cooked.
  • Huge on event day: finding a good group transforms a fondo or rally.
Signals & Calls

In a group you're the eyes for riders behind. Point out and call hazards they can't see.

Common Signals
  • Point down: potholes, debris, drain covers.
  • Call it: "car back," "car up," "stopping," "slowing."
  • Hand behind back / wave: move over for an obstacle on that side.
  • Pass it along: relay calls up and down the line.
Group Etiquette

Smooth, predictable riding keeps the whole bunch safe. Erratic riders make everyone nervous.

The Unwritten Rules
  • Hold your line: no sudden swerves, brakes, or surges.
  • No half-wheeling: match the pace beside you, don't creep ahead.
  • Brake smoothly: ease off rather than grab a fistful.
  • Pull off predictably: signal, then drift to the back smoothly.
Look Past the Wheel

Don't fixate on the wheel in front — look through the group and read what's coming.

Stay Aware
  • Soft focus: see the rider ahead and the road beyond them.
  • Anticipate: spot braking and turns early, react gently.
  • Cushion the gap: a touch of space gives you reaction time.
Taking a Turn

A good pull keeps the pace steady — your job at the front is consistency, not a surge.

At The Front
  • Hold the pace: don't accelerate when you hit the wind.
  • Short pulls are fine: a brief, smooth turn beats a long, fading one.
  • Pull off into the wind: so the line stays sheltered as you drift back.
Ride Within Your Group

Pick a group that matches your level. Hanging on by a thread is unsafe for you and everyone around you.

Be Honest
  • Right pace: a group you can ride and contribute to, not just survive.
  • New to it: start at the back, watch, and learn the group's habits.
  • Speak up: if the pace is unsafe or too hot, say so or drift off.

Road Safety

Be Visible

Assume drivers haven't seen you until proven otherwise. Make yourself impossible to miss.

Visibility Kit
  • Lights always: a daytime running light front and rear, even in sun.
  • Bright clothing: high-contrast colours, reflective detail at dusk.
  • Position to be seen: ride where drivers naturally look, not in the gutter.
Ride Predictably

Predictable riders are safe riders. Let drivers and other cyclists anticipate you.

Habits
  • Hold a straight line: don't weave between parked cars.
  • Signal clearly: point turns and lane changes well in advance.
  • Obey the road: stop signs and lights — you're traffic too.
  • Claim the lane when it's too narrow to share safely.
Scan & Anticipate

Defensive riding means seeing trouble early — before it becomes a decision you have to make fast.

What To Watch
  • Parked cars: watch for brake lights, exhaust, and opening doors.
  • Intersections: make eye contact; assume turning cars don't see you.
  • Driveways & junctions: cover the brakes through pinch points.
  • Mirror or shoulder check: know what's behind before you move.
Surfaces & Weather

The road itself is a hazard — wet, loose, or broken surfaces demand more space and lower speed.

Adapt
  • Wet roads: brake earlier and gentler; corners are slick, paint and metal worse.
  • Gravel & debris: stay loose, look ahead, avoid braking or steering hard on it.
  • Heat & cold: dress and fuel for conditions; both impair judgment.
Ride Prepared

A few precautions mean a mishap stays minor instead of becoming a real problem.

Before You Roll
  • Carry ID & phone: and an emergency contact set up on the lock screen.
  • Share your route: tell someone where and how long, or use live tracking.
  • Know basic repairs: a flat shouldn't strand you miles from home.
Stay Heads-Up

Your attention is safety gear. Distraction and tuned-out senses cause avoidable crashes.

Keep Aware
  • Hearing matters: skip headphones, or keep one ear open at most.
  • Eyes up: don't fixate on the computer; glance, don't stare.
  • Fatigue dulls reactions: a tired rider is a slower-reacting one.

Injury Prevention & Comfort

Knee Pain

The most common cycling overuse complaint — usually a fit or load problem, not a knee problem.

Address It
  • Check the fit: saddle height and cleat position are the usual culprits.
  • Spin, don't grind: low cadence in big gears overloads the knees.
  • Build gradually: sudden jumps in volume or hill work provoke it.
  • Persistent or sharp knee pain — get a professional bike fit and rest.
Saddle Sores & Comfort

Sit-bone and soft-tissue discomfort is a solvable equipment issue — don't just tough it out.

Prevent It
  • Right saddle: matched to your sit-bone width, not the softest one.
  • Quality shorts & chamois cream: reduce friction on long rides.
  • Clean & dry: change out of kit promptly after riding.
  • Move around: shift position and stand periodically on long rides.
Numb Hands & Feet

Tingling or numbness means too much pressure on a nerve — a fixable fit and habit issue.

Fixes
  • Hands: bar tape/gloves, lighter grip, and move hand positions often.
  • Weight off the bars: a fit that's too long or low overloads the hands.
  • Feet ("hot foot"): check shoe fit, cleat position, and not over-tight straps.
Neck & Back Aches

Upper-body aches usually trace to a position too aggressive for your flexibility — or a static one held too long.

Relieve It
  • Raise the bars: a more upright position is fine and often faster long-term.
  • Move on the bike: change hand positions, shrug, look around.
  • Off-bike work: core and mobility build the support to hold a position.
Overtraining

Too much load with too little recovery leads to stalled fitness and burnout — not gains.

Warning Signs
  • Watch for: elevated resting HR, poor sleep, low motivation, flat legs.
  • Performance drop: getting slower despite training harder.
  • The fix: rest is the only cure — take it early, before it's forced on you.
The Prevention Toolkit

Most cycling injuries are overuse, not accident — and overuse is preventable.

Stay Healthy
  • Fit first: a proper bike fit prevents the majority of overuse pain.
  • Progress gradually: respect the ~10% rule on volume.
  • Don't ride through sharp pain: dull fatigue is fine; sharp or worsening pain isn't.
  • Strength & mobility: a little off-bike work goes a long way — see below.

Strength & Recovery

Sleep Is King

No supplement or gadget rivals 7–9 hours of quality sleep for recovery and adaptation.

Protect It
  • Consistent schedule: regular bed and wake times stabilize recovery.
  • Extra after big days: hard training raises your sleep needs.
  • It's training: treat sleep as a session you don't skip.
Cyclists Need the Gym

Cycling builds a fit engine but a narrow body. Off-bike strength fills the gaps.

Why Bother
  • More durable: stronger riders hold form and resist injury late in rides.
  • Bone health: cycling is low-impact — lifting protects bone density.
  • Better power: a stronger base supports your top-end efforts.
Strength Essentials

You don't need to be a powerlifter — two short sessions a week covers the basics.

The Movements
  • Legs: squats, lunges, deadlifts, step-ups — the cycling prime movers.
  • Posterior chain: glutes and hamstrings power the pedal stroke.
  • Timing: heavier in the off-season, lighter and maintenance in-season.
Core & Mobility

A stable core lets you hold a good position for hours; mobility makes that position comfortable.

Focus Areas
  • Core: planks and anti-rotation work stop the upper body from collapsing.
  • Hips & hamstrings: cycling shortens them — stretch and mobilize regularly.
  • Thoracic spine & neck: mobility here eases the riding posture.
Active Recovery

Gentle movement on rest days aids recovery more than total stillness — but keep it genuinely easy.

Good Options
  • Easy spin or walk: light blood flow, zero training stress.
  • Mobility & stretching: a calm session to undo riding posture.
  • The trap: "recovery" rides that creep into real efforts — keep them tiny.
Periodize & Taper

Fitness comes from stress then rest, repeated — and from arriving at events fresh.

Structure The Season
  • Recovery weeks: every 3–4 weeks, ease off to absorb the training.
  • Taper: 1–2 weeks before an event, cut volume, keep light intensity.
  • Off-season: a real break each year refreshes body and motivation.

Mental Game

Break It Into Chunks

A century is daunting; "ride to the next feed zone" is not. Shrink the task to a size your mind accepts.

How To Do It
  • Segment the route: climb to climb, town to town, bottle to bottle.
  • Stay present: ride the kilometer you're in, not the 80 still to come.
  • Tick them off: each chunk done is real, visible progress.
Bad Patches Pass

On any long ride you'll have a low moment. It's normal, it's usually fixable, and it almost always passes.

Ride It Out
  • First, fuel: a rough patch is often just low blood sugar — eat and drink.
  • Ease off briefly: back the pace down for ten minutes rather than quitting.
  • Don't decide low: never make a "I'm done" call in the depths of a bad patch.
Manage Self-Talk

The voice in your head sets the ceiling of your effort. It can be coached.

Practical Cues
  • Reframe sensations: "this is hard" → "this is me working, and it's working."
  • Have a mantra: a short phrase to fall back on when it bites.
  • Process over outcome: focus on cadence, breathing, the next pedal stroke.
Calm the Pre-Event Nerves

Nerves are normal and even useful. Preparation channels them into focus instead of dread.

Settle In
  • Trust the training: the work is done; the event is just the payoff.
  • Routine over rumination: a familiar warm-up gives the mind a job.
  • Lean on the plan: your A/B/C goals make the start line less scary.
Use the Crowd & the Bunch

Events are shared experiences. Other riders and spectators are a resource, not just scenery.

Draw On It
  • Ride with others: a steady wheel lifts both pace and morale.
  • Soak up support: feed-zone volunteers and crowds genuinely help.
  • Give it back: a word of encouragement to a struggling rider lifts you too.
Remember Why You Ride

For the amateur, the point is the joy, health, and adventure — the result is a bonus, not the purpose.

Keep Perspective
  • Celebrate finishing: completing the distance is the real achievement.
  • Bad days happen: one rough event doesn't undo a season of riding.
  • Play the long game: a lifetime of riding beats one perfect season.
