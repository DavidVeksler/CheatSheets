The 15 Commitments of Conscious Leadership

A Cheatsheet for Leading "Above the Line" based on the work of Jim Dethmer, Diana Chapman, and Kaley Warner Klemp.

Leading from Above the Line

Conscious leadership involves recognizing whether you are operating "Above the Line" or "Below the Line".

  • Below the Line: Primary commitment is to being right; defensive; closed; reactive; ego-driven survival based on perceived threat (real or imagined).
  • Above the Line: Primary commitment is to learning; curious; open; leading from security and trust. Enables creativity, innovation, and collaboration.
  • The Shift: Actively moving from a below-the-line state (closed, defensive, right) to an above-the-line state (open, curious, learning). A master skill of conscious leaders.
  • Self-Awareness: The foundation is knowing your state and telling yourself the truth.
Four Contexts (Ways of Leading): (See Details)
  • To Me: Victim consciousness, blaming external factors.
  • By Me: Taking responsibility, focuses on learning from experiences.
  • Through Me: Aligned with a higher purpose or flow.
  • As Me: Embodies oneness and universality.
1: Taking Radical Responsibility

Commit to 100% responsibility for your life and well-being.

  • Shift from believing the world *should* be a certain way to accepting how it shows up (seeing reality).
  • Move from rigidity and self-righteousness to curiosity, learning, and wonder.
  • Self-blame is NOT responsibility; it's still toxic.
  • Support others in taking responsibility (primarily by embodying it yourself).
  • Recognize motivation levels and lead from levels 3-5.
  • Identify Victim-Villain-Hero drama and step out of it.
2: Learning Through Curiosity

Commit to growing in self-awareness and regarding every interaction as a learning opportunity. Prioritize learning over being right.

  • Curiosity is the path to rapid learning. End blame and criticism.
  • Key leadership predictors: Self-awareness, Learning Agility, Communication, Influence.
  • Pause, breathe consciously, and ask: "Where am I - above or below the line?" Accept the answer without judgment.
  • Wonder is a key shift move and state of consciousness.
  • Practice Shift Moves to interrupt reactivity.
3: Feeling All Feelings

Commit to feeling your feelings fully through to completion using somatic awareness, breath, movement, and vocalization.

  • Feelings aren't distractions; resisting or repressing them is. Access all centers: head (thinking), heart (feeling), gut (instinct/intuition).
  • Primary emotions: anger, fear, sadness, joy, sexual feelings. Label sensations accurately.
  • Feelings release naturally if not prevented (repression) or intensified by thoughts (recycling). Allow their full life cycle (often < 90 seconds).
  • Distinguish authentic joy (unconditional well-being) from circumstantial happiness (dependent on external events).
  • Release process: Locate, Breathe, Allow, Match.
4: Speaking Candidly

Commit to saying what is true for you (candor) and being someone others can express themselves to (conscious listening).

  • Reveal withheld thoughts, opinions, judgments, feelings, sensations (leads above the line). Withholding drains energy and creates disconnection.
  • Three circles of candor: Truth, Openness, Awareness.
  • Reveal what is unarguable to avoid drama and promote clarity.
  • "Eat" your projections: Ask, "How is this true about me?"
  • Listen for feelings (heart center) and underlying desires (gut/instinctive center), not just content (head center).
5: Eliminating Gossip

Commit to ending gossip by talking directly *to* people you have an issue with, using candor.

  • Encourage others to speak directly to the person involved.
  • Listening to gossip is participating in it. Set boundaries.
  • Distinguish clearly between facts and the stories you create about them. Drama comes from believing stories are facts.
  • Being "clear" means full expression, often using a structured process like the Clearing Model.
6: Practicing Integrity

Commit to masterful integrity.

  • Four pillars: Radical Responsibility, Speaking Candidly, Feeling Feelings, and Keeping Agreements.
  • Breaches block energy flow and cause disengagement. There's no "small" breach (Tom Peters).
  • Know when to lead unilaterally (assigning) vs. bilaterally (agreeing). Agreements require a whole body YES.
  • Impeccable people renegotiate agreements promptly.
  • Continuously clean up breaches using tools like the Integrity Inventory (unfelts, unsaids, unkepts, unowneds).
7: Generating Appreciation

Commit to living in appreciation, fully open to both receiving and giving it genuinely.

  • First step is sensitive awareness. What you place attention on grows.
  • Refusing appreciation (e.g., deflecting, downgrading) robs the giver and denies a truth about yourself.
  • Aim for a high ratio (~5:1) of appreciation to criticism.
  • Masterful appreciation includes: Sincerity, Unarguable truth, Specificity, Succinct language.
  • Appreciation (recognizing value) grows connection, contrasting with entitlement (expecting rewards without gratitude).
8: Excelling in Your Zone of Genius

Commit to expressing your full magnificence and supporting others to live in their zone of genius.

  • Identify activities aligned with innate talents and passions vs. zones of Incompetence, Competence, or Excellence.
  • If an activity is a "dead-end" (Incompetence/Competence): stop, delegate, or do it differently (find play/creativity).
  • Fear often guards the line between Excellence and Genius, related to the Upper Limits Problem (Gay Hendricks).
  • Use tools like the Genius Email Exercise or Best Stuff Exercise to identify your genius.
9: Living a Life of Play and Rest

Commit to creating play, improvisation, and laughter. Maximize energy by honoring rest, renewal, and rhythm.

  • See life unfold easefully and effortlessly, releasing struggle.
  • Co-create playfully with others (e.g., using Persona Play).
  • Laughter (genuine, lighthearted) indicates playfulness.
  • Rest (naps, breaks, sleep, Sabbath) boosts productivity and creativity (research cited).
  • Energy management requires honoring natural life rhythms (sleep/wake, seasons, sprint/recovery). Confront Workaholism.
10: Exploring the Opposite

Commit to seeing that the opposite of your story (story) is as true or truer than the original.

  • Recognize you interpret the world; life itself has no inherent labels until we assign them. Take responsibility for being the "labeler."
  • Question all labels and the need to be right about your story.
  • Use inquiry methods like "The Work" to become curious about all possibilities.
  • Remain unattached to outcomes or needing your story to be 'right'. Shift from certainty to curiosity.
11: Sourcing Approval, Control & Security

Commit to being the source of your own approval, control, and security.

  • Humans' core wants: approval, control, security. Seeking them externally ("out-there-ness", "if-only-ness") leads to suffering because it stems from a belief in lack.
  • The issue isn't the wants themselves, but the "wanting", which implies lack.
  • Use release techniques like The Sedona Method:
    1. Identify the wanting.
    2. Welcome the wanting (allow it).
    3. Identify if it stems from seeking approval/control/security.
    4. Welcome it again; consider letting it go (Could I? Would I? When?).
  • Rest as the awareness beyond wanting; experience inherent wholeness.
12: Having Enough of Everything

Commit to experiencing sufficiency – having enough.

  • Challenge toxic myths of scarcity:
    1. There is never enough.
    2. More is better.
    3. That's just the way it is (hopeless/helpless).
  • Sufficiency is not an amount; it's an experience, a context, a knowing. "There is enough and we are enough." Shift perspective (e.g., time perception, money value) and practice presence (in the 'now', there is always enough).
  • Collaboration grounded in sufficiency creates prosperity (Lynne Twist).
13: Experiencing the World as an Ally

Commit to seeing all people and circumstances as allies perfectly suited for your growth.

  • Shift out of comparison and competition; see everyone as equally valuable contributors to your learning.
  • Welcome pressure and challenge as catalysts for awakening, emergence, or growth (like muscles growing stronger after breakdown).
  • Ask: "If this person/circumstance were a perfect ally, what is here for me to learn?" "How is this 'for me'?"
  • Requires taking responsibility (C1) and curiosity (C2).
14: Creating Win-for-All Solutions

Commit to creating solutions that are a win for all involved.

  • Address issues considering the benefit for you, others, the organization, and the wider system/whole.
  • Requires shifting from scarcity (zero-sum game) to sufficiency (enough resources for all).
  • Relies on collaboration rather than competition (win/lose) or compromise (often lose/lose).
  • Integrates other commitments like Candor (C4), Curiosity (C2), Sufficiency (C12), and Allies (C13).
15: Being the Resolution

Commit to being the resolution or solution that is needed.

  • See what's perceived as missing (e.g., communication, beauty, efficiency) not as lack, but as an invitation from life/the universe.
  • Accept the invitation (if a whole body YES) to embody the required quality or contribution.
  • Ask: "What is the universe inviting me to be or become related to what I perceive as missing?" Focus on 'being' first, then 'doing'.
  • Moves beyond apathy, resentment, or blame about perceived problems.

Based on The 15 Commitments of Conscious Leadership by Jim Dethmer, Diana Chapman, & Kaley Warner Klemp.
