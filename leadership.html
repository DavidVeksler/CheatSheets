The 15 Commitments of Conscious Leadership

A Cheatsheet for Leading "Above the Line" based on the work of Jim Dethmer, Diana Chapman, and Kaley Warner Klemp.

Leading from Above the Line

Conscious leadership involves recognizing whether you are operating "Above the Line" or "Below the Line".

  • **Below the Line:** Primary commitment is to being right; defensive; closed; reactive; ego-driven survival.
  • **Above the Line:** Primary commitment is to learning; curious; open; leading from security and trust.
  • **The Shift:** Actively moving from below to above the line state.
  • **Self-Awareness:** The foundation is knowing your state and telling yourself the truth.
Four Contexts for Experiencing the World:
  • To Me: Victim consciousness, blaming external factors.
  • By Me: Taking responsibility, focuses on learning from experiences.
  • Through Me: Aligned with a higher purpose or flow.
  • As Me: Embodies oneness and universality.
1: Taking Radical Responsibility

Commit to 100% responsibility for your life and well-being.

  • Shift from believing the world *should* be a certain way to accepting how it shows up.
  • Move from rigidity and self-righteousness to curiosity, learning, and wonder.
  • Self-blame is NOT responsibility; it's still toxic.
  • Support others in taking responsibility.
  • Recognize motivation levels.
2: Learning Through Curiosity

Commit to growing in self-awareness and regarding every interaction as a learning opportunity. Prioritize learning over being right.

  • Curiosity is the path to rapid learning. End blame and criticism.
  • Key leadership predictors: Self-awareness, Learning Agility, Communication, Influence.
  • Pause, breathe consciously, and ask: "Where am I - above or below the line?"
  • Wonder is open-ended curiosity.
3: Feeling All Feelings

Commit to feeling your feelings fully through to completion using somatic awareness (locating in the body), breath, movement, and vocalization.

  • Feelings are not distractions; resisting them is. Access all centers: head, heart, gut.
  • Primary emotions: anger, fear, sadness, joy, sexual feelings. Label sensations.
  • Feelings release naturally if not prevented. Allow their full life cycle (often < 90 seconds).
  • Distinguish authentic joy (unconditional) from circumstantial happiness (conditional).
4: Speaking Candidly

Commit to saying what is true for you (candor) and being someone others can express themselves to.

  • Reveal thoughts, opinions, judgments, feelings (leading above the line).
  • Three circles of candor: Truth, Openness, Awareness.
  • Reveal what is unarguable: a thought, a feeling, a sensation.
  • "Eat" your projections: Ask, "How is this true about me?"
  • Listen for feelings and underlying desires (gut/instinctive center).
5: Eliminating Gossip

Commit to ending gossip by talking directly *to* people you have an issue with.

  • Encourage others to speak directly to the person involved.
  • Listening to gossip is participating in it.
  • Distinguish clearly between facts and the stories you create about them.
  • Being "clear" means full expression: saying and feeling everything related to the issue.
6: Practicing Integrity

Commit to masterful integrity.

  • Acknowledge feelings, express unarguable truth, keep agreements, take 100% responsibility.
  • Breaches cause disengagement. There's no "small" breach (Tom Peters).
  • Know when to lead unilaterally vs. bilaterally.
  • Impeccable people renegotiate agreements promptly.
  • Continuously clean up "unfelts, unsaids, unkepts, and unowneds."
7: Generating Appreciation

Commit to living in appreciation, fully open to both receiving and giving it genuinely.

  • First step is awareness: simply pay attention.
  • Refusing appreciation robs the giver.
  • Aim for a high ratio (~5:1) of appreciation to criticism.
  • Masterful appreciation includes: Sincerity, Unarguable truth, Specificity, Succinct language.
  • Appreciation (vs. entitlement) grows value and connection.
8: Excelling in Your Zone of Genius

Commit to expressing your full magnificence and supporting others to live in their zone of genius.

  • Identify activities aligned with your innate talents and passions.
  • If an activity is a "dead-end": stop, delegate, or do it differently.
  • Fear often guards the line between the zone of *excellence* (what you're good at but may not love) and the zone of *genius*.
9: Living a Life of Play and Rest

Commit to creating play, improvisation, and laughter. Maximize energy by honoring rest, renewal, and rhythm.

  • See life unfold easefully and effortlessly.
  • Co-create playfully with others. Avoid struggle and suffering.
  • Laughter indicates playfulness.
  • Rest boosts productivity.
  • Energy management is key to long-term effectiveness and avoids burnout.
10: Exploring the Opposite

Commit to seeing that the opposite of your story (story) is as true or truer than the original.

  • Recognize you interpret the world; life itself has no inherent labels.
  • Question all labels. Take responsibility for being the "labeler."
  • Use inquiry methods like "The Work" to become curious about all possibilities.
  • Remain unattached to outcomes or needing your story to be 'right'.
11: Sourcing Approval, Control & Security

Commit to being the source of your own approval, control, and security.

  • Humans' core wants: approval, control, security. Seeking them externally leads to suffering.
  • Use release techniques like The Sedona Method:
    1. Identify the wanting.
    2. Welcome the wanting.
    3. Identify if it stems from seeking approval/control/security.
    4. Welcome it again; consider letting it go.
  • Rest as the awareness beyond wanting.
12: Having Enough of Everything

Commit to experiencing sufficiency – having enough (time, money, love, energy, etc.).

  • Challenge toxic myths of scarcity:
    1. There is never enough.
    2. More is better.
    3. That's just the way it is (hopeless/helpless).
  • Sufficiency is not an amount; it's an experience, a context, a knowing. "There is enough and we are enough."
13: Experiencing the World as an Ally

Commit to seeing all people and circumstances as allies perfectly suited for your growth.

  • Shift out of comparison and competition; see everyone as equally valuable.
  • Welcome pressure as a catalyst for awakening or emergence.
  • Ask: "If this were a perfect ally, what is here for me to learn?"
14: Creating Win-for-All Solutions

Commit to creating solutions that are a win for all involved.

  • Address issues considering the benefit for you, others, the organization, and the whole system.
  • Collaboration opens possibilities unavailable through competition and compromise.
15: Being the Resolution

Commit to being the resolution or solution that is needed.

  • See what's missing in the world or a situation as an invitation to become or provide that missing piece.
  • Ask: "What is the universe inviting me to be or become related to what I perceive as missing?"

Based on The 15 Commitments of Conscious Leadership by Jim Dethmer, Diana Chapman, & Kaley Warner Klemp.
