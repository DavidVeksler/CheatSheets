Nuclear Preparedness: Fallout Is a Solvable Physics Problem

Outside the immediate blast and fire zone, survival is dominated by ordinary physics: time, distance, shielding, air, water, and information. Kearny's Oak Ridge work showed untrained families could build useful fallout protection with hand tools; modern FEMA and CDC guidance still starts with shelter first.

Threat Model, Honestly

Flash

Light and heat arrive immediately. If you see a flash, do not look at it; drop behind cover and protect skin.

Blast

The shock wave arrives after the flash. At damaging distances it can still be tens of seconds later; cover cuts glass and debris injuries.

Fallout

Radioactive particles from ground or near-ground bursts can arrive minutes to hours later. This is the hazard sheltering can beat.

EMP

Electronics and grid damage are possible. The survival response is still analog: shelter, battery radio, printed instructions.

Five deadly myths

  1. "If you see the flash, you are dead." Wrong. Seeing the flash can be the warning that lets you drop before blast; Hiroshima injury studies made flying glass one of Kearny's practical reasons for duck-and-cover.
  2. "Fallout poisons all air." Fallout is particulate. Keep dust out, but do not make an occupied shelter airtight.
  3. "Radiation lasts for centuries everywhere." Fallout dose rate decays fast at first; contamination sites are a different problem.
  4. "Nuclear winter makes shelter pointless." Climate debate does not change the first-hour and two-week survival problem.
  5. "Gear matters more than mass." A basement corner with heavy shielding can beat expensive equipment in a bad location.

The useful mental model

Dose = dose rate x time / protection factor. You can improve all three variables: wait while dose rate falls, reduce outside trips, and put heavy material between you and fallout.

The 7-10 Rule and Two-Week Clock

Rule

Every 7-fold increase in time after detonation cuts fallout dose rate by about 10-fold.

This is a planning rule, not a meter reading. Real fallout varies by particle mix, weathering, and local deposition.

Protection Factor Math

Formula

PF = outside dose / inside dose

Example: if outside is 31 R/hr and your shelter is 0.2 R/hr, then PF 155. Kearny uses this exact style of measurement example for finding shelter PF.

Goal: a practical basement corner should aim for PF 40+; expedient earth shelters can be much higher.

Worked example: basement PF-40 corner
  1. Pick geometry: choose the basement corner farthest from exterior walls, roof, and exposed windows. Corners under earth-grade walls usually win.
  2. Build overhead mass: lay a sturdy interior door or table over the sleeping area. Load it only if the supports can safely carry the weight.
  3. Add side mass: stack book boxes, filled water containers, tool chests, canned food, and earth-filled drawers around two exposed sides. Twelve inches of books/paper or water is roughly 1.5 halving-thicknesses; 24 inches is roughly 3.
  4. Block line of sight: gamma scatters, but direct sky/ground paths through windows and stairs are bad. Turn the entry path or stack mass near it while preserving airflow.
  5. Keep air: do not tape yourself into a sealed room. Use cloth or filters to reduce dust entry, not to suffocate.
  6. Estimate: ordinary basement PF 10-20 plus several halving-thicknesses of dense improvised mass can plausibly reach PF 40+ in the best corner. Verify with a meter if available.

Example final answer: outside 100 R/hr at H+7 and shelter PF 40 means inside dose rate about 2.5 R/hr. Six hours inside adds about 15 R, not 600 R.

Expedient Shelters: Kearny Designs

Frame honestly: Kearny shelters matter most with warning, rural land, tools, and enough people to dig. Urban readers usually get more survival value from improving the best building shelter immediately.

Kearny App. A.1: PF about 200-250 with about 2 ft of earth on the roof; trench about 3 ft wide, 4.5 ft deep. Tools/materials: shovel, pick, saw/knife, plastic rainproofing, one door per occupant plus earth cover. Trial note: a family of six completed a PF 200 version in 34 hr after receiving instructions.

Kearny's car-over-trench variant uses a car body as quick overhead cover above a narrow trench, then improves shielding with earth and distance. Spec plate: narrow body-length trench sized to the car; tools/materials: shovel or pick, car, boards/plastic if available, and a ventilation plan; PF/person-hours are not presented as a standalone Appendix A design, so treat it as an emergency variant of the trench family rather than a preferred planned shelter. Kearny uses it to illustrate why crowded shelters need pumped air in warm conditions.

Kearny App. A.4: PF about 200 with 30 in of earth; PF about 100 with the 20 in illustrated cover. Minimum illustrated length shelters four people with six doors. Tools/materials: doors, sheets or plastic for earth rolls/walls, shovel, pick, stakes/cord, rainproofing. Person-hours depend heavily on earth hauling because it is above ground; build it when digging a deep trench is blocked by high water table, rock, or frozen ground.

Kearny Air Pump

Kearny warns that crowded high-PF shelters can become dangerously hot and humid. His KAP instructions target about 40 cfm/person in hot humid weather; the 36 x 29 in model can pump about 1000 cfm with proper openings.

Do not: seal a shelter airtight. Fallout dust is a problem; suffocation, heat, carbon dioxide, and humidity can be faster problems.

Water beside shelter

Kearny's expedient shelter instructions call for storing 15 gal/person in or near the shelter. That roughly matches a two-week survival minimum, not comfort.

Gotcha: water is also shielding. Put containers where they add mass between occupants and fallout, but do not create a leak/flood hazard.

The Kearny Fallout Meter

What it is

The KFM is a homemade electroscope dose-rate meter built from common materials such as a can, foil leaves, thread, plastic, tape, and a drying agent. Kearny says average untrained families could build one in 3-4 hr and learn to use it in about 1.5 hr.

It measures gamma dose rate by timing the discharge of charged foil leaves. Kearny's instructions include tables for converting leaf movement into R/hr.

Kearny describes the KFM as accurate to about +/-25% when built, charged, and read correctly. Free plans are in Appendix C of the public-domain Nuclear War Survival Skills PDF linked in the sources.

Critical limit: digital printouts or photocopies of the templates may not be scale-accurate. If you plan to rely on one, build and test it before a crisis.

Modern alternatives

  • GM counter: useful for contamination and low-level checks; many cheap units saturate or under-read at high fallout dose rates.
  • Ion chamber / survey meter: better for high dose rates if calibrated and powered.
  • Chirper alarm: useful as a wake-up warning, not a replacement for dose planning.
  • No meter: use the 7-10 rule, official instructions, and conservative shelter timing.
Operational use: If the inside reading is 0.2 R/hr and outside is 31 R/hr, your PF is 155. Future inside readings can estimate outside rate by multiplying by 155, but contamination on the meter can cause false high readings.

Communications and Modern Updates

WEA/IPAWS

FEMA IPAWS feeds Wireless Emergency Alerts, EAS radio/TV, and NOAA Weather Radio. Keep phone alerts enabled, but assume networks may be degraded.

NOAA radio

NOAA Weather Radio All Hazards broadcasts official warnings and post-event information. Store batteries and know your local channel.

AM/FM

AM broadcast is robust and easy to receive. Battery radios with local station presets are worth more than internet-only plans.

Handheld radio

Use for listening and licensed amateur/GMRS operations. Program local repeaters, NOAA channels, 146.520 MHz, 446.000 MHz, and local public-safety receive channels where legal. Do not transmit illegally unless life safety clearly requires emergency use.

What changed since 1979: CONELRAD/civil-defense siren assumptions are obsolete; IPAWS, WEA, EAS, NOAA Weather Radio, phone alerts, and web tools now exist. What did not change: FEMA's current planning guidance still validates the shelter-first logic for the dangerous early fallout period.

NUKEMAP, used calmly

NUKEMAP is useful before a crisis to understand scale, wind sensitivity, and why basement shielding beats panic driving. Do not use a browser model during the first hour when your job is to get inside and improve PF.

Cell networks

Expect overload, power loss, and damaged towers near the event; text messages may work before voice, and partial service can return as backup power, mobile towers, and routing recover. Keep plans that do not require cellular data.

Kearny said
Read warnings, build or improve shelter before attack if warned, use fallout meters when possible, and stay sheltered until local dose rates justify outside work.
FEMA/CDC now say
Get inside, stay inside, stay tuned. Let emergency officials decide when evacuation beats sheltering, because early self-evacuation can cross the plume at the worst time.

Common Mistakes

Fleeing by car

Driving during fallout arrival trades a low-PF vehicle and traffic gridlock for the highest dose-rate period. Shelter first unless officials give a route and timing.

Sheltering upstairs

Comfort upstairs can mean roof and exterior-wall exposure. Move to basement or center core, then improve comfort.

Leaving on day 2

H+49 hours is much safer than H+1, but not automatically safe. Use meter readings or official instructions.

Taking KI like a cure

KI is thyroid-only and timing-specific. It does nothing for external fallout gamma dose.

Sealing airtight

Dust control is good. Carbon dioxide, heat, humidity, candle fumes, and sanitation gases can become immediate hazards.

One fragile water store

A single bathtub liner or jug stack can fail. Spread water across multiple containers and shielded locations.

Buying before reading

Two hours with Kearny, FEMA, CDC, and your basement layout beats another gadget you do not know how to use.

Ignoring sanitation

A bucket, bags, lid, absorbent, gloves, bleach, and privacy sheet prevent disease and panic in ways no meter can.
