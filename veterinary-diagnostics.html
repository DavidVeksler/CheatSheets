Veterinary Diagnostics Cheatsheet

An interactive guide to essential terminology, diagnostic tests, and principles in veterinary medicine for effective animal healthcare.

Intro to Terminology

Word Structure & Basics

Veterinary terms are often composed of prefixes, suffixes, and root words. Understanding these components is key to deciphering complex medical language.

Key Components:
  • Prefixes: Modify the meaning of the root word (e.g., ante- before, endo- within).
  • Suffixes: Indicate a procedure, condition, disease, or part of speech (e.g., -ectomy surgical removal, -logy study of).
  • Root Words: Often derived from Greek or Latin, provide the core meaning (e.g., hemo/hemato for blood, osteo for bone).

Common abbreviations (e.g., SID - once daily, q - every) are also vital for efficient communication.

Directional & Positional Terms

Precise terms describe locations on an animal's body. Examples include anterior/cranial, posterior/caudal, dorsal, and ventral.

Common Directional Terms:
  • Anterior (Cranial): Toward the head end of the body.
  • Posterior (Caudal): Toward the tail end of the body.
  • Dorsal: Toward or pertaining to the back or upper surface.
  • Ventral: Toward or pertaining to the belly or lower surface.
  • Medial: Toward the midline of the body.
  • Lateral: Away from the midline, toward the side.
  • Proximal: Nearer to the point of attachment or origin (limbs).
  • Distal: Farther from the point of attachment or origin (limbs).
  • Palmar: Pertaining to the caudal surface of the forelimb, distal to the carpus (wrist).
  • Plantar: Pertaining to the caudal surface of the hindlimb, distal to the tarsus (ankle).
Body Planes:
  • Sagittal Plane: Divides body into left and right portions. (Midsagittal or median plane is exactly down the midline).
  • Transverse Plane: Divides body into cranial and caudal portions.
  • Frontal (Dorsal) Plane: Divides body into dorsal and ventral portions.

Diagnostic Principles

Purpose & Importance

Diagnostic tests are essential for detecting disease, monitoring health, guiding treatment decisions, and establishing prognoses in animals. They provide objective data to support clinical findings.

Effective diagnostic testing allows veterinarians to:

  • Screen for subclinical diseases.
  • Confirm or rule out differential diagnoses.
  • Assess organ function and damage.
  • Monitor response to therapy.
  • Provide a prognosis for the patient.
Sample Collection & Handling

Accurate diagnostic results depend heavily on proper sample collection (e.g., blood, urine, tissue), correct anticoagulants/preservatives, and appropriate storage and transport.

Key Considerations:
  • Patient Preparation: Fasting status, stress levels.
  • Correct Site & Technique: Aseptic technique for sterile samples.
  • Appropriate Collection Tubes/Containers: E.g., EDTA for CBC, serum separator tubes for chemistry.
  • Proper Labeling: Patient ID, date, sample type.
  • Timely Processing & Transport: Temperature requirements, avoiding hemolysis or degradation.

Preanalytical errors (errors before the sample is analyzed) are a common source of inaccurate results.

Test Interpretation

Interpreting results involves comparing them to reference intervals and understanding concepts like sensitivity, specificity, and predictive values.

Key Concepts:
  • Reference Interval: Established from a healthy population; results outside this range may indicate abnormality but must be interpreted in context of the individual patient.
  • Sensitivity: True Positives / (True Positives + False Negatives). High sensitivity tests are good for ruling OUT disease (SNOUT).
  • Specificity: True Negatives / (True Negatives + False Positives). High specificity tests are good for ruling IN disease (SPIN).
  • Predictive Value Positive (PVP): Probability that a patient with a positive test result actually has the disease.
  • Predictive Value Negative (PVN): Probability that a patient with a negative test result actually does not have the disease.
  • Quality Control (QC): Procedures to ensure lab tests are accurate and reliable.

Laboratory Diagnostics

Hematology

Evaluation of blood cells: RBCs, WBCs, and platelets. Key test is the CBC.

Common Hematology Tests & Terms:
  • Complete Blood Count (CBC): Includes RBC count, WBC count (total and differential), platelet count, hemoglobin, hematocrit/PCV.
  • Anemia: Deficiency in RBCs or hemoglobin. Can be regenerative (bone marrow responding) or non-regenerative.
  • Leukocytosis: Increased WBCs (often indicates infection/inflammation).
  • Leukopenia: Decreased WBCs.
  • Thrombocytopenia: Decreased platelets.
  • Blood Smear Evaluation: Microscopic assessment of blood cell morphology.
  • Coagulation Profile: Tests like PT (Prothrombin Time) and aPTT (Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time) assess clotting ability.
Clinical Chemistry

Measures enzymes, metabolites, and electrolytes in serum/plasma to assess organ function (liver, kidney, pancreas) and metabolic status.

Key Chemistry Analytes:
  • Kidney: BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen), Creatinine, SDMA (Symmetric Dimethylarginine).
  • Liver: ALT, AST, ALP, GGT, Bilirubin, Bile Acids.
  • Pancreas: Amylase, Lipase, PLI (Pancreatic Lipase Immunoreactivity).
  • Proteins: Total Protein, Albumin, Globulin.
  • Electrolytes: Sodium (Na), Potassium (K), Chloride (Cl), Calcium (Ca), Phosphorus (P).
  • Glucose: Blood sugar.
  • Endocrine: T4 (thyroid), Cortisol (adrenal).
Urinalysis (UA)

Provides insights into kidney function, hydration status, and can detect urinary tract inflammation, infection, or other systemic diseases.

Components of a Urinalysis:
  • Gross Examination: Color, clarity, odor.
  • Specific Gravity (USG): Measures urine concentration, kidney's concentrating ability.
  • Chemical Analysis (Dipstick): pH, protein, glucose, ketones, bilirubin, blood (heme).
  • Microscopic Sediment Exam: Cells (RBCs, WBCs, epithelial), casts, crystals, microorganisms (bacteria, fungi).
  • Urine Culture & Sensitivity: If infection suspected.

Cystocentesis is the preferred collection method for culture.

Fecal Analysis

Primarily used to detect helminth ova and protozoal cysts/oocysts. Can also identify indicators of maldigestion or bleeding.

Common Fecal Tests:
  • Fecal Flotation: Concentrates parasite eggs using solutions of differing specific gravity (e.g., zinc sulfate, sugar solution).
  • Direct Smear: For motile protozoa (e.g., Giardia trophozoites).
  • Fecal Occult Blood Test: Detects hidden blood, indicating GI bleeding.
  • Fecal PCR Panels: Detect DNA of specific enteric pathogens (bacteria, viruses, parasites).
  • Fecal Cytology: To look for inflammatory cells or abnormal cells.
Microbiology

Involves culturing samples to identify pathogenic bacteria or fungi, and determining their antibiotic sensitivity.

Key Microbiology Procedures:
  • Culture & Sensitivity (C&S): For bacteria (aerobic, anaerobic) and fungi.
  • Stains: Gram stain (differentiates bacteria), Acid-fast stain (for Mycobacterium).
  • PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction): Highly sensitive detection of pathogen DNA/RNA.
  • Serology: Detects antibodies (host response) or antigens (pathogen parts) for infectious diseases (e.g., FeLV, FIV, Heartworm, Lyme).
Cytology

Rapid evaluation of cell types from FNAs, impression smears, or body fluids to identify inflammation, infection, or neoplasia.

Common Cytology Applications:
  • Fine Needle Aspirates (FNA): From skin masses, lymph nodes, internal organs (often ultrasound-guided).
  • Impression Smears: From ulcerated lesions or surgical biopsies.
  • Fluid Analysis: Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), synovial (joint) fluid, effusions (abdominal, thoracic). Differentiates transudates, modified transudates, and exudates.
  • Helps differentiate inflammation (e.g., neutrophilic, eosinophilic) from neoplasia (cancer).
Histopathology

Evaluation of biopsied tissue architecture and cellular detail by a pathologist to provide a definitive diagnosis, especially for cancer (grading/staging).

Key Aspects of Histopathology:
  • Sample Collection: Incisional biopsy, excisional biopsy, punch biopsy, endoscopic biopsy.
  • Fixation: Usually 10% neutral buffered formalin is crucial for preserving tissue structure. Ratio of tissue to formalin should be 1:10.
  • Processing: Embedding in paraffin, sectioning, staining (commonly Hematoxylin and Eosin - H&E).
  • Interpretation: Identifies cell types, tissue architecture, presence of inflammation, infection, necrosis, dysplasia, or neoplasia.
  • Cancer Diagnosis: Determines if a tumor is benign or malignant, its type (e.g., carcinoma, sarcoma), grade, and status of surgical margins.
Molecular Diagnostics

Utilizes techniques like PCR to detect genetic material (DNA/RNA) of pathogens, genetic mutations associated with disease, or cancer biomarkers.

Applications of Molecular Diagnostics:
  • Pathogen Detection: Highly sensitive and specific for viruses, bacteria (e.g., Mycoplasma, Bartonella), fungi, and parasites (e.g., Tick-borne disease panels).
  • Genetic Disease Testing: Identifies inherited conditions (e.g., PRA in eyes, HCM in hearts).
  • Oncology: Detection of cancer-specific mutations (e.g., BRAF mutation in TCC), clonality testing for lymphoma (PARR), liquid biopsies for cancer screening.
  • Pharmacogenomics: Predicting drug response/sensitivity based on genetic makeup (e.g. MDR1 gene mutation).
Immunology

Focuses on tests evaluating the immune system, including antibody/antigen tests for infectious diseases, allergy testing, and markers for autoimmune conditions.

Common Immunological Tests:
  • Serology (Antibody/Antigen Tests):
    • ELISA (Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay): Common for FeLV/FIV, Heartworm, Parvovirus, Lyme.
    • IFA (Immunofluorescent Antibody): For tick-borne diseases, FIP.
    • Titers: Measure antibody levels (e.g., post-vaccination or for certain infectious diseases).
  • Allergy Testing: Serum IgE testing or intradermal skin testing to identify environmental or food allergens.
  • Autoimmune Disease Markers: ANA (Antinuclear Antibody) for lupus-like diseases, Coombs' Test for immune-mediated hemolytic anemia (IMHA).
  • Measurement of Immunoglobulins: E.g., IgG, IgA, IgM levels, useful for diagnosing immunodeficiencies.

Diagnostic Imaging

Radiography (X-rays)

Uses X-rays to create 2D images of internal structures. Excellent for bones, chest (lungs, heart silhouette), and abdomen (organ size/shape, foreign bodies, gas patterns).

Key Aspects of Radiography:
  • Indications: Trauma (fractures), coughing/dyspnea, vomiting/diarrhea, suspected masses, lameness.
  • Views: Typically at least two orthogonal views (e.g., lateral and ventrodorsal/dorsoventral) are needed.
  • Interpretation: Based on radiographic densities (gas, fat, soft tissue/fluid, bone, metal). Assesses size, shape, position, opacity of structures.
  • Contrast Studies: Using contrast agents (barium for GI, iodinated contrast for urinary/vascular) to highlight specific organs or tracts.
Ultrasonography

Uses high-frequency sound waves to create real-time images of soft tissues and organs, assessing internal architecture. Excellent for abdomen, heart (echocardiography), and eyes.

Key Aspects of Ultrasonography:
  • Indications: Abdominal pain, organomegaly, fluid accumulation, suspected masses, cardiac murmurs, pregnancy.
  • Advantages: Non-invasive, no radiation, excellent soft tissue detail, allows for guided sampling (FNA, biopsy).
  • Echocardiography: Specialized ultrasound of the heart to assess chamber sizes, wall thickness, valve function, blood flow (Doppler).
  • Interpretation: Based on echogenicity (hyperechoic, hypoechoic, anechoic) and tissue patterns.
Advanced Imaging (CT/MRI)

CT and MRI provide detailed cross-sectional images. Endoscopy allows direct visualization of internal tracts.

Computed Tomography (CT):
  • Excellent for bone detail, lungs, nasal passages, complex fractures, surgical planning for masses, angiography. Uses X-rays.
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI):
  • Superior for soft tissue detail, especially brain, spinal cord, nerves, and joints (ligaments, tendons). Uses magnetic fields and radio waves.
Endoscopy:
  • Visual examination of internal organs using a flexible or rigid scope with a camera (e.g., gastroscopy, colonoscopy, bronchoscopy, rhinoscopy). Allows for biopsy and foreign body retrieval.

These modalities often require general anesthesia.

Other Specialized Tests

Cardiac & Systemic Monitoring

ECG/EKG assesses heart rhythm. Blood gas analysis measures oxygenation and acid-base balance. Tonometry checks eye pressure.

Key Specialized Tests:
  • Electrocardiography (ECG/EKG): Records electrical activity of the heart, used to diagnose arrhythmias and conduction abnormalities.
  • Blood Pressure Measurement: Crucial for diagnosing hypertension or hypotension, monitoring anesthesia and critical patients.
  • Blood Gas Analysis: Measures PaO2, PaCO2, pH, bicarbonate in arterial or venous blood to assess respiratory function and acid-base status.
  • Tonometry: Measures intraocular pressure (IOP), essential for diagnosing and monitoring glaucoma.
Specialized Function Tests

Dynamic tests assess organ responses, like the ACTH stimulation test for adrenal function or bile acid stimulation for liver function.

Examples of Function Tests:
  • ACTH Stimulation Test: Evaluates adrenal reserve; used to diagnose hypoadrenocorticism (Addison's) and hyperadrenocorticism (Cushing's).
  • Dexamethasone Suppression Test (LDDST/HDDST): Helps differentiate pituitary-dependent vs. adrenal-dependent Cushing's disease.
  • Bile Acid Stimulation Test: Assesses liver function, particularly portosystemic shunts, by measuring pre- and post-prandial bile acids.
  • Thyroid Stimulating Hormone (TSH) Response Test: Historically used for diagnosing hypothyroidism (less common now).

Specialized Panels & Technologies
