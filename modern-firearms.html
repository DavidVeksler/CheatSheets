Core Concepts & Terminology

Action Types

The mechanism that loads, fires, and ejects cartridges. It's the "engine" of the firearm.

Semi-Automatic
One trigger pull = one shot. Uses firing energy to automatically load the next round. The most common modern action.
Manual
User performs a physical action (pump, bolt, lever) for each shot. Slower but often very reliable.
Revolving
Cartridges are held in a rotating cylinder. Simple, reliable action specific to revolvers.
Select-Fire
Can switch between semi-auto and automatic/burst fire modes. Standard for most military rifles.
Ammunition Explained

A self-contained cartridge is the hallmark of modern firearms. It consists of four key components.

Caliber (Rifles/Handguns)
Bore diameter measured in inches (.308) or millimeters (9mm).
Gauge (Shotguns)
Bore diameter based on the number of lead spheres that equal one pound. Smaller gauge number = larger bore.
Cartridge Components
Case (holds components), Primer (ignites), Propellant (creates gas), Projectile (bullet/shot).

Lever-Action Rifles

Modern Lever-Action Rifles
Hunting Cowboy Action Home Defense

Classic American rifle action featuring a lever mechanism to cycle rounds. Fast follow-up shots, compact design, and iconic profile make them enduringly popular for hunting and sport.

Key Concepts
  • Action types: traditional tubular magazine vs. modern box magazine design affecting capacity and loading speed.
  • Caliber options: pistol calibers (.357 Mag, .44 Mag, .45 Colt) for short-range versatility; rifle calibers (.30-30, .45-70) for big game hunting.
  • Loading gates: traditional side-loading vs. modern tube-fed systems; some models feature both tubular and box magazine options.
  • Optics mounting: modern versions include Picatinny rails and drilled/tapped receivers for scopes; traditional models use tang or receiver sights.
  • Barrel lengths: carbine (16"-18") for maneuverability; full-length (20"-24") for increased velocity and accuracy.
Defining Attributes
Operating System
Manual lever action cycles a fresh cartridge with each lever stroke. Simple, reliable mechanism with fewer parts than semi-autos.
Magazine Types
Traditional tubular magazine under barrel (4-10 rounds); modern box magazines (5-6 rounds) allow pointed bullets and faster reloading.
Effective Range
Pistol calibers: 50-100 yards; .30-30: 150-200 yards; .45-70: 200-250 yards with modern loads.
Common Uses
Big game hunting, home defense (pistol calibers), cowboy action shooting, backcountry carry, traditional sport shooting.
Pros & Cons
  • Fast cycling: Lever action allows rapid follow-up shots without breaking sight picture.
  • Compact profile: Short overall length makes them excellent for dense woods and brush hunting.
  • Proven reliability: Simple mechanical design with over 150 years of field use.
  • Legal advantages: Often exempt from restrictive legislation in various jurisdictions.
  • Limited capacity: Typically 4-10 rounds vs. 20-30 in modern semi-autos.
  • Slower reloading: Tubular magazines require individual cartridge loading (except box-mag models).
  • Optics challenges: Traditional models lack ideal scope mounting; side ejection complicates mounting on some platforms.

Classic & Surplus Military Rifles

Classic Military Surplus Rifles
Collecting Sport Shooting Hunting

Historic military rifles from WWI through the Cold War, now available as collectibles and shooters. These rifles represent significant engineering milestones and remain popular for their reliability, accuracy, and historical significance.

Key Concepts
  • CMP Program: Civilian Marksmanship Program sells authentic M1 Garands, M1 Carbines to qualified U.S. citizens at reasonable prices.
  • Surplus ammunition: Many platforms use corrosive military surplus ammo (7.62x54R, 8mm Mauser); requires immediate cleaning after shooting.
  • Collector grades: Condition ranges from arsenal refinished to all-original matching numbers; significantly affects value.
  • Parts availability: Some platforms (Mosin, SKS) have abundant cheap parts; others (Enfield, G43) require expensive sourcing.
  • Historical value: Many rifles appreciated significantly; some serve dual purpose as investments and shooters.
Defining Attributes
Action Types
Bolt-action (Mauser 98, Lee-Enfield, Mosin-Nagant), semi-automatic (M1 Garand, SKS, M1 Carbine), and select-fire (deactivated or semi-only variants).
Ammunition Considerations
Most use full-power rifle cartridges (.30-06, 7.62x54R, 8mm Mauser, .303 British). Surplus ammo often corrosive. Modern commercial loads available but expensive.
Typical Features
Iron sights (often excellent), military-grade steel construction, hardwood stocks, simple robust design prioritizing reliability over refinement.
Common Uses
Collecting, historical reenactment, Civilian Marksmanship Program (CMP) matches, hunting (where legal), recreational shooting, investment.
Pros & Cons
  • Historical significance: Own and shoot pieces of military history; connection to major conflicts.
  • Robust construction: Built to military standards for extreme conditions and abuse.
  • Excellent iron sights: Many feature superb aperture or ladder sights designed for combat accuracy.
  • Affordable entry: Some models (Mosin-Nagant, SKS) remain inexpensive; CMP Garands are excellent value.
  • Heavy recoil: Full-power cartridges with heavy rifles and steel buttplates produce significant felt recoil.
  • Corrosive ammo: Surplus ammunition requires immediate, thorough cleaning to prevent bore damage.
  • Optics mounting: Requires gunsmithing or period-correct mounts; drilling/tapping affects collector value.
  • Parts/ammunition cost: Commercial hunting ammo for obscure calibers can be expensive.

Simple, elegant shotguns that "break open" at the breech for loading. Over/Under (O/U) barrels stacked vertically, Side-by-Side (SxS) barrels horizontal, or single-shot. Favored for clay sports, upland bird hunting, and traditional shooting.

Key Concepts
  • Action types: Over/Under (O/U) for consistent sight plane; Side-by-Side (SxS) for traditional aesthetics; Single-shot for simplicity and economy.
  • Choke systems: fixed chokes vs. interchangeable screw-in chokes determine shot pattern spread; typical combinations: IC/M, M/F, or adjustable.
  • Barrel selection: O/U typically use bottom barrel first; selectors allow choosing which barrel fires; important for choke management.
  • Triggers: single selective trigger (SST) fires both barrels sequentially; double triggers give instant barrel selection.
  • Fit and mount: break-actions emphasize proper gun fit; stock dimensions (LOP, drop, cast) critical for consistent mounting and accuracy.
Defining Attributes
Operating System
Manual break-action. Shotgun "breaks" open at the breech via a top lever, exposing chamber(s) for loading. After firing, manually eject and reload.
Capacity
Single-shot (1 round), double-barrel (2 rounds). Reloading requires breaking action open, removing spent shells, inserting fresh shells.
Reliability
Extremely reliable due to simple mechanical design. No gas system, no magazines, minimal parts. Virtually immune to cycling failures.
Common Uses
Sporting clays, trap, skeet, upland bird hunting (pheasant, quail, grouse), waterfowl hunting, traditional driven shoots, youth/entry shotguns (single-shot).
Pros & Cons
  • Simplicity & reliability: Minimal moving parts; no cycling failures or jams.
  • Two chokes available: O/U and SxS offer two different choke constrictions instantly available via barrel selection.
  • Superior balance: Well-designed break-actions balance naturally and mount smoothly for instinctive shooting.
  • Safety: Visibly open chamber when broken; easy to confirm unloaded status at a glance.
  • Limited capacity: 1-2 shots before manual reload. Not ideal for home defense or high-volume shooting.
  • Slow reloading: Breaking action, extracting shells, and reloading is slower than pump or semi-auto.
  • Cost: Quality O/U shotguns are expensive (premium models $2,000-$10,000+); budget options available but quality varies.
  • Weight: Many models are heavy (8-9 lbs) to reduce recoil for clay sports; can be tiring for all-day carry.

Practical Knowledge & Safety

The 4 Universal Rules of Firearm Safety
  1. Treat all guns as if they are always loaded.
  2. Never let the muzzle cover anything you are not willing to destroy.
  3. Keep your finger off the trigger until your sights are on the target and you have made the decision to shoot.
  4. Be sure of your target and what is beyond it.
