Core Concepts & Terminology

Action Types

The mechanism that loads, fires, and ejects cartridges. It's the "engine" of the firearm.

Semi-Automatic
One trigger pull = one shot. Uses firing energy to automatically load the next round. The most common modern action.
Manual
User performs a physical action (pump, bolt, lever) for each shot. Slower but often very reliable.
Revolving
Cartridges are held in a rotating cylinder. Simple, reliable action specific to revolvers.
Automatic
Fires continuously while the trigger is held. Heavily restricted and primarily for military/LE use.
Ammunition Explained

A self-contained cartridge is the hallmark of modern firearms. It consists of four key components.

Caliber (Rifles/Handguns)
Bore diameter measured in inches (.308) or millimeters (9mm).
Gauge (Shotguns)
Bore diameter based on the number of lead spheres that equal one pound. Smaller gauge number = larger bore.
Cartridge Components
Case (holds components), Primer (ignites), Propellant (creates gas), Projectile (bullet/shot).

Handguns: Sidearms

Semi-Automatic Pistols
Home Defense Concealed Carry Competition

The dominant modern handgun, fed by a detachable magazine. One trigger pull fires one round and automatically chambers the next.

Defining Attributes
Firing Mechanism
Striker-Fired or Hammer-Fired
Frame Size
Full-Size, Compact, Subcompact, Micro-Compact
Common Calibers
9mm, .45 ACP, .40 S&W, 5.7x28mm
Practical Considerations
  • High capacity (10-20+ rounds) & fast reloads
  • Lighter trigger pulls & large aftermarket support
  • More complex operation than revolvers
  • Can be susceptible to user-induced malfunctions (limp-wristing)
In Pop Culture

The Glock and Beretta 92 are staples in action films, while the 1911 is a classic icon seen in everything from noir films to modern video games.

Revolvers
Home Defense Concealed Carry Backcountry

Features a revolving cylinder with multiple chambers. Prized for its absolute simplicity and reliability.

Defining Attributes
Action Type
Double-Action or Single-Action
Firing Mechanism
Exposed or Shrouded/Internal Hammer
Common Calibers
.38 Special, .357 Magnum, .44 Magnum
Practical Considerations
  • Extremely simple and reliable operation
  • Not sensitive to ammunition power levels
  • Low capacity (typically 5-8 rounds)
  • Slow to reload compared to semi-automatics

Rifles: Shoulder-Fired

Modern Sporting Rifles (MSRs)
Home Defense Competition General Purpose

A category of modular, semi-automatic rifles, typified by the AR-15 platform. Highly adaptable for many uses.

Defining Attributes
Action
Semi-Automatic (Gas-Operated)
Caliber
Intermediate cartridges (e.g., 5.56x45mm, .300 Blackout)
Practical Considerations
  • Highly modular and customizable
  • Low recoil, high capacity, and accurate
  • Often subject to complex legal restrictions
  • Intermediate cartridge not suitable for large game hunting
In Pop Culture

The AR-15 platform is ubiquitous in modern military films and video games like *Call of Duty*, representing the standard for a modern rifle.

Precision / Sniper Rifles
Competition Long-Range

Rifles engineered for extreme accuracy at long distances, typically using a bolt-action for maximum consistency.

Defining Attributes
Action
Primarily Bolt-Action
Caliber
High-performance cartridges (6.5 Creedmoor, .300 Win Mag, .338 Lapua)
Practical Considerations
  • Supreme accuracy and long effective range
  • Purpose-built for hitting very distant targets
  • Very heavy, slow rate of fire, and expensive
  • Requires significant skill and practice to use effectively

Shotguns: Smoothbore

Pump-Action Shotguns
Home Defense Hunting Tactical

The user manually slides a forend ("pump") to cycle the action. Known for its rugged reliability and versatility.

Defining Attributes
Action
Manual Pump-Action
Gauge
12 Gauge (most common), 20 Gauge
Practical Considerations
  • Extremely reliable, durable, and affordable
  • Cycles a wide variety of shell types reliably
  • Slower rate of fire than a semi-auto
  • User can cause a jam by "short-stroking" under stress
Semi-Automatic Shotguns
Competition Hunting

Uses firing energy to automatically cycle the action. Offers faster follow-up shots and reduced felt recoil.

Defining Attributes
Action
Semi-Automatic (Gas- or Inertia-operated)
Gauge
12 Gauge, 20 Gauge
Practical Considerations
  • Fast rate of fire and reduced felt recoil
  • Excellent for shooting sports and waterfowl hunting
  • More expensive and complex than pump-actions
  • Can be sensitive to light ammunition loads

Practical Knowledge & Safety

The 4 Universal Rules of Firearm Safety
  1. Treat all guns as if they are always loaded.
  2. Never let the muzzle cover anything you are not willing to destroy.
  3. Keep your finger off the trigger until your sights are on the target and you have made the decision to shoot.
  4. Be sure of your target and what is beyond it.
Hollywood MythBusting
Myth: "Silencers" make guns silent.
Truth: Suppressors reduce the sound to hearing-safe levels (like a jackhammer), but they are far from silent.
Myth: Racking a shotgun is the ultimate intimidation tactic.
Truth: It also gives away your position and ejects a perfectly good, unfired shell from the weapon.
Myth: Pistols are often fired sideways ("gangster style").
Truth: This makes aiming nearly impossible and can cause the firearm to malfunction as the empty casing may fail to eject properly.
