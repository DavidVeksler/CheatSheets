Core Concepts & Terminology

Action Types

The mechanism that loads, fires, and ejects cartridges. It's the "engine" of the firearm.

Semi-Automatic
One trigger pull = one shot. Uses firing energy to automatically load the next round. The most common modern action.
Manual
User performs a physical action (pump, bolt, lever) for each shot. Slower but often very reliable.
Revolving
Cartridges are held in a rotating cylinder. Simple, reliable action specific to revolvers.
Select-Fire
Can switch between semi-auto and automatic/burst fire modes. Standard for most military rifles.
Ammunition Explained

A self-contained cartridge is the hallmark of modern firearms. It consists of four key components.

Caliber (Rifles/Handguns)
Bore diameter measured in inches (.308) or millimeters (9mm).
Gauge (Shotguns)
Bore diameter based on the number of lead spheres that equal one pound. Smaller gauge number = larger bore.
Cartridge Components
Case (holds components), Primer (ignites), Propellant (creates gas), Projectile (bullet/shot).

Practical Knowledge & Safety

The 4 Universal Rules of Firearm Safety
  1. Treat all guns as if they are always loaded.
  2. Never let the muzzle cover anything you are not willing to destroy.
  3. Keep your finger off the trigger until your sights are on the target and you have made the decision to shoot.
  4. Be sure of your target and what is beyond it.
Firearms Debunked: Separating Fact from Fiction

This section addresses common but significant misunderstandings about how firearms function and their capabilities, separating the physics of reality from the fiction of popular media.

Myth: Bullets Knock People Down

Cinema suggests projectiles throw people backwards. Physics says otherwise.

The Misconception

Movies and games often depict people being hurled through the air by gunfire, implying the bullet imparts enough force to physically knock targets down.

The Reality: Newton's Third Law
  • For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.
  • If a bullet could topple a 180 lb person by pure force, the equal recoil on an ~8 lb rifle and shooter would launch them backwards too — which does not happen.
  • Firearms incapacitate by rapid disruption of vital tissue and the nervous system, not by kinetic “push.”
The Takeaway

Firearms wound through tissue destruction, not kinetic push. The “knockdown” effect is cinematic, not physical reality.

Myth: AR-15s are Unusually High-Powered Rifles

Looks can mislead. The AR‑15’s 5.56mm is an intermediate cartridge, not a full‑power hunting round.

The Misconception

Because of its military styling and media attention, the AR‑15 is often assumed to be more powerful than common civilian hunting rifles.

The Reality: Intermediate vs. Full‑Power
  • Intermediate cartridges (5.56×45mm, 7.62×39mm) balance recoil, weight, and controllability for military use.
  • Full‑power cartridges (.30‑06, .308 Win, .270 Win) used in many hunting rifles are larger and carry substantially more energy.
  • Typical muzzle energy: ~1,800 J for 5.56mm vs. ~3,900 J for .30‑06 — more than double.
  • Many jurisdictions restrict 5.56mm for deer‑sized game due to limited terminal performance.
The Takeaway

The AR‑15 commonly fires an intermediate round; traditional hunting rifles often fire larger, more powerful ammunition.

Myth: Suppressors Create Movie‑Silent Gunshots

Suppressors tame gas blast, not the bullet’s sonic crack. “Silent” is fiction.

The Misconception

Films reduce suppressed gunfire to a faint “pew,” implying near‑silence.

The Reality: Managing Supersonic Gas
  • A gunshot’s sound has two parts: muzzle blast (propellant gases) and the bullet’s supersonic crack.
  • A suppressor works like a muffler, cooling and slowing gas with baffles to cut the blast.
  • It does not affect the bullet’s sonic crack; true quiet requires subsonic ammo.
  • Suppressed supersonic rifle shots are still ~130–140 dB — loud, though often hearing‑safe compared to >160 dB unsuppressed.
The Takeaway

Suppressors reduce “instantly deafening” to “hearing‑safer,” not silent.

Myth: Shotguns Have a Wide, Unavoidable Spread

Patterns grow with distance, but at room ranges they are still small and demand aiming.

The Misconception

Games often depict a huge cone of damage that hits anything in a room and fades out completely beyond short range.

The Reality: Patterning and Chokes
  • A shotgun launches a pattern of pellets that gradually spreads; at 7–10 yards, it’s often the size of a fist to a dinner plate.
  • Chokes at the muzzle control spread: tighter (Full) for longer range, open (Cylinder) for faster spread.
  • With appropriate choke and load, shotguns remain effective to 40–50 yards for wingshooting.
The Takeaway

Shotguns are precision tools at indoor distances; they still require careful aiming and pattern knowledge.

Myth: Bigger Caliber = Instant Stop

“Stopping power” is mostly placement and penetration, not caliber size.

The Misconception

Larger handgun calibers (.40/.45) are assumed to reliably “drop” attackers faster than 9mm.

The Reality: Penetration, Disruption, and Human Factors
  • Modern duty JHP loads in 9mm, .40, and .45 are engineered to meet similar FBI gel standards: 12–18 inches penetration with controlled expansion.
  • Physiological incapacitation results from disruption of CNS or rapid blood‑loss; both require adequate penetration and accurate hits under stress.
  • Recoil and capacity matter to outcomes: 9mm offers faster follow‑ups and more rounds on board, improving hit probability.
  • Temporary cavity from handguns is generally insufficient to cause remote injury; permanent cavity (the hole) is what matters most.
The Takeaway

Placement and penetration trump caliber size among common service cartridges; shoot the most controllable load that meets duty standards.

Myth: Hollow Points Always Stop Inside the Body

Expansion is conditional; barrier performance and velocity determine outcome.

The Misconception

Hollow‑point handgun rounds are believed to expand perfectly and always remain in the target, eliminating over‑penetration risk.

The Reality: Thresholds, Barriers, and Design
  • Expansion requires sufficient impact velocity; short barrels can drop velocity below expansion thresholds.
  • Common barriers (denim, auto glass, sheet metal) can clog cavities or deform bullets; FBI protocols test across these precisely because expansion is not guaranteed.
  • Even expanded JHPs can exit; the largest risk to bystanders is still missed shots, not through‑and‑through hits.
  • Bonded designs trade expansion diameter for barrier integrity; non‑bonded may expand more but fragment through glass.
The Takeaway

Quality JHPs reduce—not eliminate—over‑penetration; shot placement and backdrop awareness remain essential.

Myth: 5.56 “Tumbles in Air”

Stability in air; yaw and potential fragmentation occur in tissue.

The Misconception

5.56×45mm is said to “tumble wildly” in flight, explaining wounding behavior.

The Reality: Gyroscopic Stability vs. Tissue Yaw
  • In air, bullets are gyroscopically stabilized by spin; they fly point‑forward when properly matched to barrel twist.
  • Upon entering tissue, drag forces cause yaw; some 5.56 FMJ loads can fragment when velocity exceeds ~2,300–2,700 fps.
  • Short barrels reduce velocity and thus the fragmentation window, often leading to narrower wound tracks.
  • Bullet construction (OTM, bonded, monolithic) governs whether it yaws, fragments, or mushrooms in tissue.
The Takeaway

Tumbling is a tissue phenomenon, not an in‑air behavior; terminal effects depend on speed and construction.

Myth: Short AR Barrels Give Up Little Performance

Velocity loss strongly affects fragmentation and barrier performance in 5.56.

The Misconception

Compact AR pistols/SBRs are thought to match 16–20" rifles except for a little muzzle flash.

The Reality: Velocity, Gas System, and Bullet Design
  • 5.56 performance is velocity‑sensitive; chopping from 20" to 10.3" can cost 400–700 fps depending on load.
  • Below certain speeds, FMJ/OTM loads stop fragmenting, reducing wound severity and barrier disruption.
  • Short gas systems increase dwell pressure and blast; reliability requires tuned buffers, springs, and port sizes.
  • Loads optimized for short barrels (e.g., bonded soft points) can reclaim some terminal effect at lower velocities.
The Takeaway

Short barrels trade velocity and terminal effect for compactness; choose bullets designed for SBR speeds.

Myth: Handguns Easily Defeat Body Armor

NIJ soft armor stops common pistol rounds; rifle plates are needed for rifles.

The Misconception

Movies show pistols punching through vests; reality is standard soft armor neutralizes typical handgun threats.

The Reality: NIJ Levels and Threats
  • Level II/IIIA soft armor is rated for 9mm/.40/.45 and .357/.44 Magnum profiles—not for common rifle threats.
  • Rifle rounds (5.56, 7.62×39, .308) typically require Level III/IV plates rated for high‑energy penetrators.
  • Specialty pistol armor‑piercing or high‑velocity loads can challenge soft armor, but are atypical and often restricted.
  • Backface deformation can still injure even when bullets don’t penetrate; armor mitigates lethality but does not remove trauma.
The Takeaway

Handguns rarely penetrate quality soft armor; rifles are a different threat category requiring hard plates.

Myth: Water Instantly Stops Bullets

Water decelerates bullets fast, but ricochet and fragmentation are complex.

The Misconception

Bullets are believed to become harmless on water impact or stop within inches regardless of caliber.

The Reality: Hydrodynamic Drag and Angle
  • High‑velocity projectiles can fragment or yaw abruptly on entry due to extreme drag, dumping energy quickly.
  • At shallow angles, rounds often ricochet off the surface; at steeper angles, they can penetrate several feet depending on speed and construction.
  • Handgun rounds typically travel several feet in water; rifle rounds may fragment early but can remain dangerous near the surface.
  • Real‑world outcomes depend on angle, distance, bullet type, and whether the projectile destabilizes.
The Takeaway

Water is not a magic shield; it rapidly slows bullets but does not guarantee safety near the surface.

Myth: Manual Safeties Make Guns “Safe”

Safeties block certain mechanisms; they don’t replace safe handling.

The Misconception

Engaging a manual safety is thought to eliminate discharge risk entirely.

The Reality: Layers and Human Factors
  • Safeties address specific failure modes (e.g., trigger movement) but cannot prevent negligence like pressing the trigger or pointing at non‑targets.
  • Drop safeties and firing‑pin blocks mitigate mechanical accidents; they do not override the Four Rules.
  • Complex safeties can induce mode errors under stress if users rely on them instead of sound handling.
  • Training emphasizes holistic risk reduction: muzzle discipline, trigger discipline, and target awareness.
The Takeaway

Safeties are one layer; safe outcomes depend on behavior and fundamentals.

Myth: Warning Shots and “Leg Shots” Are Safer

Stray rounds and low‑probability hits create greater risk; physics and law align here.

The Misconception

Firing into the air or aiming for limbs reduces lethality and liability.

The Reality: Ballistics and Outcomes
  • Bullets fired upward can travel far; returning rounds still cause injury. Ground impacts ricochet unpredictably.
  • Limbs are small, fast‑moving targets with major vessels; misses endanger bystanders, and hits can still be fatal.
  • Effective defensive shooting prioritizes high‑probability stops (upper thoracic/CNS) when deadly force is justified.
  • Many jurisdictions treat warning shots as use of deadly force; consult local law and training best practices.
The Takeaway

Low‑probability shots increase risk; prioritize control, backdrop, and lawful intent.
