Comparative Religion Map

A structural guide to traditions, texts, practices, and ultimate concerns. It is a map for asking better questions—not a ranking, a substitute for adherents, or a claim that every tradition fits one template.

Start here: “religion” is an external category. A tradition may be simultaneously a people, a way of life, a ritual system, a philosophical inheritance, and a set of institutions. Read each column as a lens, not a box.

Shared roots

A historical connection can explain vocabulary or stories. It does not erase later disagreement. Compare both inheritance and the specific claim each community makes now.

Syncretism and contact

Trade, migration, empire, translation, intermarriage, and colonial rule shape lived religion. “Pure” boundaries are often a modern simplification.

Institution ≠ adherent

Official teaching, a clergy member's view, a household's practice, and a survey response can differ. State which one you mean.

Profiles: ask the same questions, then notice when they do not fit

Judaism

Orientation: covenantal life, Torah, peoplehood, and communal memory. Example: Shabbat is a recurring practice of rest and sanctification, not merely a “Jewish weekend.” Do not flatten: Rabbinic, Reform, Conservative, Orthodox, Reconstructionist, secular, and many other Jewish lives differ.

Christianity

Orientation: faith in God as disclosed in Jesus Christ, with worship and discipleship shaped by church traditions. Example: baptism may mark incorporation into Christ and the church, but its theology and timing vary. Do not flatten: Catholic, Orthodox, Protestant, Pentecostal, and independent traditions are not interchangeable.

Islam

Orientation: worship of God, Qur'anic guidance, and accountable ethical life. Example: salah structures daily prayer around set times; it is not equivalent to informal private reflection. Do not flatten: law schools, Sufi paths, Sunni and Shi'a traditions, cultures, and individual observance vary.

Hindu traditions

Orientation: a family of South Asian traditions with diverse philosophies, deities, scriptures, and practices. Example: bhakti may express devotion to a chosen deity through song, image, and pilgrimage. Do not flatten: “Hinduism” is not one centralized creed or church.

Buddhism

Orientation: diagnosing and ending dukkha through ethics, mental training, and wisdom. Example: meditation is one practice among generosity, precepts, chanting, ritual, and study. Do not flatten: “no God” does not describe every Buddhist cosmology or practice.

Sikhism and Jainism

Sikhism: devotion to one God expressed through remembrance and service; langar makes equality concrete at a shared meal. Jainism: radical ahimsa and disciplined non-possession; food and livelihood ethics show why “nonviolence” is an exacting practice, not a slogan.

Daoist, Confucian, and Shinto traditions

Orientation: often concerned with cultivation, ritual, relation, place, and harmony rather than a salvation story. Example: Confucian li frames ritual propriety as character and social formation. Do not flatten: Chinese and Japanese lived traditions can overlap.

Indigenous and nonreligious worldviews

Method: name a particular people or worldview, then learn from sources they authorize. Example: a place-based ceremony cannot be responsibly summarized as generic “nature worship.” Likewise: secular humanism is one family of ethical outlooks, not a synonym for all nonbelief.

A comparison method that travels well

  1. Ask an emic question: How does a participant name the practice and its purpose?
  2. Then an etic question: What historical, social, or comparative pattern is a scholar describing?
  3. Compare practice with doctrine: a catechism, sermon, ritual, household, and court ruling may tell different stories.
  4. Locate the level: historical claim, theological claim, demographic claim, and personal testimony need different evidence.
  5. Use symmetry: give each tradition enough space to sound intelligent to a well-informed adherent before you criticize or compare it.
Useful prompts for a first conversation
  • What does a good life look like in this tradition?
  • What practice would a newcomer actually see or do?
  • Who is authorized to interpret—and where is disagreement welcomed?
  • Which term is commonly mistranslated or misunderstood?
  • What would a respectful outsider stop assuming after learning more?

Turning traditions into belief checklistsIt hides ritual, law, art, institutions, and identity.Add a lived-practice example and an internal diversity note.

Using one sect as the wholeIt imports a local answer as universal.Name the school, region, period, or source.

Starting points and primary texts

For first-person sources, start with a tradition’s own texts and communities; then use critical scholarship to locate history and debate. Helpful gateways include the Jewish texts at Sefaria, the Catholic Bible, the Qur'an, SuttaCentral, Sri Guru Granth Sahib, and the Stanford Encyclopedia’s religion entry. Those sources do not eliminate disagreement; they make the disagreement inspectable.
