Quick Reference

The shortest useful BJJ operating system: improve position, keep structure, isolate a limb or neck, then finish only when control is stable.

Decision Rule

Escaping: defend the immediate submission, rebuild frames, make hip space, recover guard or come up. Attacking: immobilize hips and shoulders, isolate a lever or neck, remove slack, finish gradually.

1. Safety

Protect neck, elbows, knees, and wrists first. Tap before pain.

2. Structure

Base, posture, frames, head position, and inside space.

3. Position

Guard, pass, pin, mount, back, or escape route.

4. Isolation

Separate elbow, expose neck, trap shoulder, or split legs.

5. Finish

Apply controlled pressure or choking mechanics; release when partner taps.

Fundamentals

Concepts That Explain Most Rounds

These are not slogans. They are practical diagnostics for why a technique succeeds or fails against resistance.

Position Before Submission

Dominant position reduces the opponent's movement choices before you attack. Example: stabilize mount with knees tight and head low before hunting an arm triangle. Do not chase armbars from loose mount if the opponent can bridge freely.

Frames Create Space

A frame is bone structure placed between bodies to stop pressure without muscular pushing. Example: forearm across the hip in side control to make room for a knee. Do not frame across the face if it exposes your arm.

Base, Posture, Structure

Base resists being tipped, posture keeps the spine aligned, and structure uses limbs efficiently. Example: in closed guard, knees wide, spine tall, elbows in. Do not put both hands on the mat; that feeds triangles and armbars.

Inside Space

Inside space is the lane between torsos controlled by elbows, knees, head, and hooks. Example: pummel for an underhook in half guard before coming up. Do not let a passer staple both knees together.

Connection and Disconnection

Attach when controlling; disconnect when escaping. Example: top side control uses chest-to-chest pressure, while bottom side control turns to the side and inserts frames. Do not create space for an opponent you are trying to pin.

Chains Beat Single Moves

High-percentage attacks usually work as dilemmas. Example: collar drag to single leg, or kimura threat to back take. Do not expect one move to work after the opponent knows exactly what is coming.

Wedges and Levers

Wedges split space; levers move a larger structure through a smaller control point. Example: knee wedge inside half guard blocks chest pressure, while a kimura grip turns the shoulder line. Do not confuse flexible pushing with skeletal structure.

Rhythm, Feints, and Energy

Advanced timing comes from changing tempo and exploiting reactions. Example: threaten collar drag so the partner pulls back, then follow into ankle pick or guard entry. Gotcha: fake attacks without real danger teach partners to ignore them.

Strong Weapon, Weak Point

Sometimes you impose your best system; other times you attack the weakest part of their structure. Example: if a passer's head is low, front headlock beats forcing a favorite sweep. Do not tunnel on your A-game when their defense has already removed its entry.

Game Planning

Build a Simple Game

A good BJJ game is not a giant list of techniques. It is a small set of connected choices that return to familiar positions under pressure.

White Belt Priority

Survive, escape, recover guard, keep posture, and learn one takedown entry. Example: spend rounds starting from bottom side control and mount. Do not judge progress only by submissions.

Blue/Purple Priority

Connect guard, sweep, pass, and submission chains. Example: closed guard arm drag to back take, or knee slice to mount when they frame. Do not keep adding unrelated moves without a link.

Advanced Priority

Force dilemmas and deny opponent's best route early. Example: body-lock pass that alternates hip clamp, knee cut, and back step. Do not rely on surprise moves that fail after two rounds.

Round Review Loop

After each class, write one recurring problem, one attempted answer, and one experiment for next time. Example: "lost knee slice to underhook; next round crossface before sliding knee." Do not turn the journal into a giant move collection with no mat test.

Partner Variety

Different bodies expose different flaws. Example: technical rounds with smaller partners improve control; rounds with heavier partners test frames and escapes. Gotcha: using size or speed to "win" every mismatch blocks the lesson.

Defense-First Confidence

Reliable defense makes offense calmer. Example: start live rounds in bottom mount until protecting elbows, bridging, and elbow-knee recovery are automatic. Do not measure beginner progress by submission count.

Common mistakes

Anti-Patterns That Stall Progress

Most plateaus are not mysterious. They are repeated failures in posture, framing, connection, or training focus.

Collecting submissions before escapes.

If you cannot survive bad positions, your attacks disappear against pressure. Fix: start rounds from bottom mount or side control until escapes are automatic.

Using straight arms to push.

Extended arms become armbars, kimuras, and underhook gifts. Fix: frame with elbows connected and hands protecting inside space.

Training every round like a tournament final.

Constant maximal intensity hides technical errors and increases injury risk. Fix: mix technical rounds, positional rounds, and hard rounds intentionally.

Ignoring grip fighting.

Bad grips decide the exchange before the technique begins. Fix: break dangerous grips immediately and establish your preferred first grip.

Confusing movement with progress.

Scrambling without goals often gives up position. Fix: know whether you are recovering guard, coming up, passing, or isolating a limb.

Late tapping or refusing to reset.

Training partners are not opponents to defeat at any cost. Fix: tap early, ask what happened, reset, and repeat the learning cycle.
