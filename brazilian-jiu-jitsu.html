Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu Cheatsheet

Your comprehensive guide to the core concepts, positions, techniques, mindset, and principles of "The Gentle Art". Navigate the essentials of BJJ from white belt basics to advanced concepts.

Intro & Core Concepts

BJJ is a grappling art focused on ground control and submissions, famously using leverage and technique to overcome size and strength.

  • Origin: Evolved from Kodokan Judo in Brazil (Gracie family).
  • Goal: Achieve dominant position, control, and submit opponent (joint lock/choke).
  • Position > Submission

    • The fundamental hierarchy: Secure a dominant position (e.g., Mount, Back Control) *before* aggressively hunting for the submission. Control prevents escapes and creates openings.

  • Leverage > Strength

    • BJJ's cornerstone. Using angles, body mechanics, and timing to apply force efficiently, allowing smaller practitioners to manage larger opponents. Think physics, not just muscle.

  • Base, Posture, Structure

    • Base: Stable connection to the ground, hard to move. Posture: Strong spinal alignment (especially in guard) to resist control/attacks. Structure: Using limbs as effective frames (to create space) or levers (to apply force/control).

  • Frames & Grips: Using limbs to create space; controlling opponent's Gi/body.
  • Timing & Momentum: Exploiting opponent's movement and energy.
Fundamental Movements

The building blocks of all BJJ techniques. Master these for effective transitions, escapes, and attacks.

  • Shrimping (Hip Escape)

    • Moving hips away laterally to create space. Essential for escaping pins (Mount, Side Control) and guard retention.

  • Bridging (Upa)

    • Explosive hip lift to disrupt opponent's base, create space, or initiate reversals (especially from Mount).

  • Posting: Using limbs on mat/opponent for balance.
  • Sprawl: Defending takedowns by driving hips back/down.
  • Technical Stand-up: Safely rising from ground while protecting.
  • Rolling/Turning: Forward/backward shoulder rolls, Granby roll for defense/transitions.
  • Pummeling: Fighting for underhook control in clinch/guard.
Positional Hierarchy

Control leads to submissions. Understand the common positions from most to least dominant (generally for the top player).

  1. 1. Back Mount / Back Control: Highest control/attack potential. Opponent can't see.
  2. 2. Mount (Full Mount): Dominant chest-on-chest, controls hips.
  3. 3. Knee-on-Belly: High pressure, mobile, good for transitions.
  4. 4. Side Control: Stable control from side (variations: Kesa Gatame, North-South).
  5. 5. Guard (Top): Must pass opponent's legs.
  6. 6. Guard (Bottom): Using legs for control; aim to sweep/submit.
  7. 7. Turtle: Defensive shell, vulnerable back exposure.
  8. Note: Being on the bottom of Mount/Side/etc., means you are in a disadvantageous position needing escape.
The Guard (Bottom)

Your primary defensive and offensive hub when on your back. Use legs to control distance, prevent passes, sweep, or submit.

  • Closed Guard : Legs locked. Strong control.

    • Classic guard. Controls posture well, many high-percentage attacks (armbars, triangles, sweeps).

  • Open Guard : Legs unlocked. More dynamic.

    • A family of guards where legs aren't locked. Requires active feet/legs to manage distance and angles. Includes:

    • Spider: Feet on biceps/hips, sleeve grips (Gi).
    • Butterfly: Feet hooks inside thighs (seated/supine).
    • De La Riva / Reverse DLR: One leg hooks opponent's leg.
    • Lasso: Leg wraps around opponent's arm (Gi).
    • X-Guard / Single Leg X: Underneath opponent, controlling legs.
  • Half Guard : Controlling one leg.

    • Trapping one of the opponent's legs. Can be defensive (survival) or offensive (sweeps/back takes). Variations: Deep Half, Z-Guard, Lockdown.

  • Guard Retention: The skill of preventing the pass and re-establishing guard using frames, shrimping, inversions etc.
Guard Concepts
Guard Passing (Top)

Navigating past the opponent's legs (guard) to secure a dominant pin like Side Control or Mount.

  • Pressure Passing: Staying close, using weight and control grips.
    • Knee Slice / Cut Pass
    • Over-Under Pass
    • Double Under Pass
    • Stack Pass
  • Speed/Dynamic Passing: Using movement, redirection, and distance.
    • Toreando / Bullfighter Pass
    • Leg Drag Pass
    • X-Pass
  • Key Principles: Control grips (legs/hips/upper body), break posture, manage distance, clear leg lines, connect passes.
Passing Concepts
Sweeps (From Guard)

Reversing the position from bottom guard to achieve a top position. Off-balancing is key.

  • From Closed Guard:
    • Scissor Sweep
    • Hip Bump Sweep
    • Flower/Pendulum Sweep
  • From Open Guard:
    • Butterfly Sweep (Hook Sweep)
    • Tripod Sweep
    • Sickle Sweep
    • De La Riva Sweeps
    • Spider Guard Sweeps
    • X-Guard Sweeps
  • From Half Guard:
    • Old School / Plan B Sweep
    • John Wayne Sweep
    • Deep Half Sweeps
  • Core Idea: Kuzushi (Off-balancing), elevate hips, create angle, use leverage.

Submissions (Finishes)

Techniques forcing submission ("Tap") via chokes (blood/air restriction) or joint locks (hyperextension/rotation).

Chokes
  • RNC (Rear Naked Choke): From Back. High percentage.
  • Guillotine: Front Headlock. Standing or ground.
  • Triangle Choke: Legs around neck/arm. From Guard/Mount.
  • Arm Triangle / Head & Arm Choke: From Top (Side/Mount).
  • Cross Collar Chokes (Gi): From Mount/Guard. X-Choke, Sliding Collar.
  • Bow & Arrow Choke (Gi): From Back. Powerful.
  • Anaconda / D'Arce Chokes: Arm-in variations of front headlock.
Joint Locks
  • Armbar (Juji Gatame): Elbow hyperextension. Versatile.
  • Kimura: Figure-four shoulder lock (internal rotation).
  • Americana (Keylock): Figure-four shoulder lock (external rotation).
  • Omoplata: Shoulder lock using legs vs arm/shoulder.
  • Straight Ankle Lock: Basic leg lock (foot hyperextension).
  • Kneebar: Knee hyperextension.
  • Wrist Locks: Applying pressure to wrist joint.
  • Advanced: Heel Hooks, Toe Holds (often restricted by belt/ruleset).
Submission Ideas
Escapes & Defense

Surviving bad positions and submission threats. Defense is paramount for longevity and learning.

Positional Escapes
  • Mount Escape: Upa (Bridge & Roll), Elbow-Knee Escape (Shrimping).
  • Side Control Escape: Reguarding (Shrimp to Guard), Framing & Turning, Ghost Escape.
  • Back Control Escape: Hand/Grip Fighting, Turning In, Scooting Down.
  • Knee-on-Belly Escape: Framing knee, Shrimping away.
  • Turtle Defense/Escapes: Protecting neck/back, Granby roll, looking for reguard/standup.
Submission Defense
  • Armbar Defense: Stacking, Hitchhiker Escape, Grip Breaks, Turning in/out.
  • Triangle Defense: Posture Up, Angle Change, Pass Arm Across, Stack.
  • Choke Defense: Protect Neck ("Chin Tuck," Shoulder Shrug), Hand Fighting, Creating Space.
  • Kimura/Americana Defense: Straighten Arm, Grab Own Belt/Thigh, Turn Body into pressure.
  • Leg Lock Defense: "Booting" Foot, Turning Correct Direction, Clearing Knee Line, Hand Fighting grips.

Takedowns & Standing

Bringing the fight to the ground. BJJ utilizes techniques from Wrestling, Judo, and Sambo.

  • Wrestling Based:
    • Double Leg Takedown
    • Single Leg Takedown (various finishes)
    • High Crotch Takedown
    • Ankle Pick / Low Single
    • Snap Down
  • Judo Based:
    • Osoto Gari (Major Outer Reaping)
    • O Goshi (Major Hip Throw)
    • Seoi Nage (Shoulder Throw)
    • Uchi Mata (Inner Thigh Throw)
    • Foot Sweeps (De Ashi Barai, Kouchi Gari)
    • Sumi Gaeshi (Sacrifice Throw)
  • Guard Pulling: Intentionally going to a guard position from standing (strategy).
  • Clinch Work: Upper body control (Underhooks, Overhooks, Body Lock, Collar/Sleeve Grips).

Mindset & The Journey

BJJ is a marathon, not a sprint. Cultivating the right mindset is crucial for long-term progression and enjoyment.

  • Growth Mindset: Embrace challenges, view failures as learning opportunities. Understand skill is developed, not fixed.
  • Patience & Persistence: Progress isn't linear. Expect plateaus. Keep showing up.
  • Humility: Accept you don't know everything. Be willing to learn from everyone, regardless of belt. Tap often (see Safety).
  • Conceptual Understanding

    • Move beyond just collecting techniques. Strive to understand the *why* behind them – the underlying principles of leverage, weight distribution, frames, timing, and control.

  • Focus on Process, Not Just Outcome: Enjoy the learning, the problem-solving during rolls, not just the submission or "win".
  • Handling Adversity: Learn from bad rolls, injuries (smart recovery), and setbacks. Resilience is key.
Training & Progression

Learning structure, advancement, and gym culture.

Training Methods
  • Warm-ups: Movement drills (shrimping, bridging etc.).
  • Technique Drilling: Repetitive practice with partner. Focus on quality reps.
  • Positional Sparring: Rolling from specific spots with goals (Guard Pass, Mount Escape etc.).
  • Rolling (Sparring): Live grappling applying techniques against resistance. Vary intensity.
Belt System (Adult)
  • White
  • Blue
  • Purple
  • Brown
  • Black
  • Coral/Red

Stripes (0-4) mark progress within belts. Time varies greatly (avg. 8-15 yrs to Black). Focus on learning, not the belt.

Gym Etiquette
  • Hygiene FIRST: Clean Gi/No-Gi gear *every* time. Clean body, trim nails short.
  • Safety: Tap early/often/clearly. Be aware of space/partners. No shoes on mat. Protect partners.
  • Respect: Instructor, partners (esp. higher belts), bowing, listening attentively.
  • Punctuality & Focus: Be on time, minimize chatter during instruction/drilling.
  • Health: Don't train sick or with skin infections (staph, ringworm). Cover cuts.
  • Community: Be a good training partner - help others learn, offer constructive rolls.
Tips for Success & Faster Progress

Advice for navigating the BJJ journey effectively and accelerating your learning curve.

  • Consistency is King: Show up regularly. Mat time is the #1 factor.
  • Master the Fundamentals: Solid escapes, posture, base, and basic positions are crucial.
  • Prioritize Defense & Survival: Confidence comes from knowing you can stay safe.
  • Learn Concepts, Not Just Moves: Understand *why* techniques work (leverage, frames, etc.).
  • Ask Smart Questions: Seek clarification on details and concepts.
  • Set Realistic Goals: Short-term (e.g., hit a sweep in rolling) & long-term (e.g., belt goals).
  • Keep a Journal: Note techniques learned, rolling observations, areas to improve.
  • Manage Your Ego: Embrace being a beginner, learn from taps, focus on improvement.
  • Know Your Learning Style: Are you visual, kinesthetic, auditory? Adapt your learning.
  • Train with Variety: Roll with different partners (size, style, level) safely.
  • Study Off the Mat: Watch instructionals, match footage, analyze concepts.
  • Conditioning Helps: Support your BJJ with strength, cardio, and flexibility training. Prevents injury, improves performance.
  • Rest & Recover: Sleep, nutrition, and rest days are vital for progress and injury prevention.
  • Have Fun! Enjoy the journey, the community, and the constant puzzle-solving.
