Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu Cheatsheet

A Quick Guide to Key Concepts, Positions, Techniques, and Principles of "The Gentle Art".

What is BJJ & Core Concepts

BJJ is a grappling-based martial art focused on ground fighting and submissions, emphasizing leverage and technique over strength.

  • Goal: Control & Submit opponent via joint locks or chokes.
  • Position Before Submission

    • Establish a dominant position (like Mount or Back Control) before attempting to finish the fight. Control is paramount.

  • Leverage Over Strength

    • Using technique, body mechanics, and leverage allows smaller individuals to overcome larger, stronger opponents.

  • Base, Posture, Structure

    • Fundamental principles for balance (Base), spinal alignment for defense/strength (Posture), and using limbs effectively as frames/levers (Structure).

  • Frames & Levers: Using limbs to create space or apply force.
  • Timing & Momentum: Using opponent's energy against them.
Fundamental Movements

Essential body movements for escaping, creating space, defending, and attacking.

  • Shrimping (Hip Escape)

    • Moving hips away laterally to create space, crucial for escaping pins and retaining guard.

  • Bridging

    • Explosive hip lift to disrupt opponent's base, used to escape Mount, Side Control, etc.

  • Posting

    • Using hands, feet, or elbows on the mat or opponent to maintain balance and prevent being swept or controlled.

  • Sprawl

    • Defending takedowns (esp. leg attacks) by thrusting hips back and dropping weight onto the opponent.

  • Shoot

    • Lowering your level and penetrating forward quickly to initiate takedowns like the Single or Double Leg.

  • Technical Stand-up: Safely getting up from the ground while protecting yourself.
Positional Hierarchy (Top > Bottom)

Dominant positions allow control and attack opportunities. Being on top is generally advantageous.

  1. Back Mount / Back Control: Most dominant; opponent can't see attacks.
  2. Mount (Full Mount): Sitting on opponent's torso, controlling hips.
  3. Knee-on-Belly: High pressure, mobile, good transitions.
  4. Side Control: Controlling from the side (chest-to-chest, Kesa Gatame, North-South variations).
  5. Guard (Top): Standing/kneeling while opponent uses legs (needs passing).
  6. Guard (Bottom): Controlling opponent from your back using legs (aim to sweep/submit).
  7. Turtle: Defensive posture on hands/knees.
  8. (Bottom positions like Bottom Mount indicate disadvantage)
Guards (Bottom Position)

Using legs from your back to control the opponent, prevent passes, set up sweeps, or submit.

  • Closed Guard

    • Legs locked around opponent's torso. Good control, many attacks/sweeps.

  • Half Guard

    • Controlling one of the opponent's legs between yours. Can be defensive or offensive.

  • Butterfly Guard

    • Using feet ("hooks") on the inside of opponent's thighs, often seated. Good for sweeps.

  • Spider Guard (Low/High)

    • Using feet on opponent's biceps/shoulders/hips with sleeve grips (Gi). Controls posture and distance.

  • Open Guard: General term for guards where legs aren't locked (includes Spider, De La Riva, Lasso etc.).
Guard Passing (Top Position)

Techniques to get around the opponent's legs (guard) to achieve a more dominant position like Side Control or Mount.

  • Straight Pass / Knee Slice: Cutting one knee through the guard while controlling legs/hips.
  • Cross Pass / Cross Knee: Passing across the opponent's body, often blocking the far hip.
  • Stack Pass: Compressing/stacking the opponent's legs towards their head.
  • Bull Fighter / Toreando Pass: Controlling both legs/pants and steering them aside like a matador.
  • Arm & Leg Pass / Leg Drag: Controlling an arm while dragging one leg across the opponent's body.
  • Double Under Pass: Scooping both arms under the opponent's legs to lift and pass.
Sweeps (From Guard)

Techniques used from a bottom guard position to reverse the situation and get on top.

  • Scissor Sweep

    • From Closed Guard, using legs in a scissoring motion to off-balance and reverse.

  • Hip Bump Sweep

    • From Closed Guard, sitting up and bumping opponent's base with your hip while controlling an arm.

  • Flower / Pendulum Sweep: Using leg momentum like a pendulum to sweep from Closed Guard.
  • Butterfly Sweep: Lifting with butterfly hooks while controlling upper body.
  • Cowboy Sweep: Specific sweep involving particular grips/leg actions (name varies).
  • Wind Mill Sweep: Often from open guard, using circular leg motions.
  • Ankle Pick / Ankle Hip / Ankle Knee Sweeps: Initiated by controlling the ankle/leg.
Submissions (Finishes)

Techniques forcing an opponent to "tap out" (submit) via chokes or joint locks.

Chokes (Restricting Blood/Air):
  • Rear Naked Choke (RNC): From Back Control.
  • Guillotine Choke: Front headlock choke.
  • Triangle Choke: Using legs around neck and one arm.
  • Cross Collar Choke: Using Gi collars (from Mount/Guard).
  • Bow and Arrow Choke: From Back Control using collar and leg.
Joint Locks (Hyperextending/Twisting):
  • Arm Bar (Juji Gatame): Hyperextending the elbow.
  • Kimura: Figure-four grip shoulder lock.
  • Americana / Key Lock: Shoulder lock, often from Mount/Side Control.
  • Omoplata: Shoulder lock using legs from Guard.
  • Ankle Lock / Straight Foot Lock: Basic leg lock.
  • (Others: Heel Hook, Kneebar, Toe Hold - often advanced)
Escapes (Getting Out)

Techniques to get out of disadvantageous positions or submission attempts.

  • Mount Escapes: Using Bridge (upa) and Shrimp variations.
  • Side Control Escapes: Using Shrimping, framing, turning in/away.
  • Back Control Escapes: Hand fighting, turning into opponent.
  • Knee-on-Belly Escape: Shrimping, framing the knee away.
  • Head Lock Escapes: Critical for self-defense and basic BJJ.
  • Arm Bar Escape: Stacking, pulling elbow out, turning.
  • Triangle Escape: Posturing up, controlling arm/head.
  • Guillotine Escape: Posturing, creating space, hand fighting.
  • Ankle Lock Escape: "Booting" the foot, turning.
  • Working Position Escapes

    • General term likely referring to escaping the core dominant positions (Mount, Side, Back) by regaining guard or creating space to stand up.

  • Shoot (Escape Context)

    • Sometimes used explosively to create scrambles or transitions out of bad spots, potentially leading to a takedown or reversal.

Takedowns (Getting to the Ground)

Techniques to bring a standing opponent to the mat. BJJ incorporates Wrestling and Judo takedowns.

  • Single Leg Takedown: Attacking and controlling one leg.
  • Double Leg Takedown: Attacking and controlling both legs.
  • Osoto Gari

    • Major Outer Reaping; a common Judo throw involving reaping the opponent's leg from the outside.

  • Leg Pick / Ankle Pick: Picking the ankle to off-balance.
  • Hip Toss (e.g., O Goshi): Using hip leverage to throw.
  • Foot Sweeps (e.g., De Ashi Barai): Sweeping the opponent's foot as they step.
  • Body Lock Takedown: Clinching around the torso to take down.
Guard Breaks (Opening Closed Guard)

Techniques used by the top player to open the opponent's locked legs (Closed Guard).

  • Posture & Base: Maintaining strong posture is key.
  • Palm Chest / Elbow Knee: Using hand/elbow pressure inside opponent's thigh/knee.
  • Hands in Hip: Posturing up, placing hands on opponent's hips to create distance.
  • Stack Break: Driving weight forward to stack the opponent, forcing legs open.
  • Standing Break: Standing up inside the guard to use gravity and leverage.
  • Standing Knee Break: While standing, driving a knee into the opponent's tailbone or thigh to split legs.
Training & Progression

How learning and advancement typically happen in BJJ.

Training Methods:
  • Drilling: Repetitive practice of techniques.
  • Positional Sparring: Rolling from specific positions with set goals (e.g., Pass/Sweep, Escape/Submit). Includes "Penning" (holding position), "Break Breaking", "Pass & Sweep".
  • Rolling (Live Sparring)

    • Free-form grappling applying all learned techniques against a resisting opponent, aiming for submission.

Belt System (Adult):
  • White -> Blue -> Purple -> Brown -> Black -> Red/Black -> Red
  • Stripes (usually 0-4) mark progress within belts.
  • Promotion based on skill, knowledge, time, consistency (instructor discretion). Avg. 8-12+ years to Black Belt.
Etiquette & Safety

Key practices for a safe and respectful training environment.

  • Tap Early, Tap Often

    • Signal submission clearly (tap partner, mat, or say "Tap!") before injury occurs. Ego < Safety.

  • Hygiene: Keep yourself and gear (Gi/No-Gi) clean. Trim nails. Avoid training when sick or with infections.
  • Mat Safety: No shoes on mats. Wear footwear off mats. Be aware of surroundings during rolling (spatial awareness).
  • Respect: Instructors, training partners (esp. higher belts), the space. Bowing on/off mats is common.
  • Be a Good Partner: Control techniques, don't crank subs, be mindful of size differences.
  • Listen to Your Body: Rest, recover, don't train through major injuries. Communicate limitations.
  • Oss / Ossu: Common term of respect/affirmation.
