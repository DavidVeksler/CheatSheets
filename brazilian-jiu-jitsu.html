Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu Cheatsheet

Your comprehensive guide to the core concepts, positions, techniques, and principles of "The Gentle Art". Navigate the essentials of BJJ from white belt basics to advanced concepts.

Intro & Core Concepts

BJJ is a grappling art focused on ground control and submissions, famously using leverage and technique to overcome size and strength.

  • Origin: Evolved from Kodokan Judo in Brazil (Gracie family).
  • Goal: Achieve dominant position, control, and submit opponent (joint lock/choke).
  • Above the Line: Learning, Open, Curious (Effective BJJ).
  • Below the Line: Right, Defensive, Closed (Ineffective/Stagnant BJJ).
  • Position > Submission

    • The fundamental hierarchy: Secure a dominant position (e.g., Mount, Back Control) *before* aggressively hunting for the submission. Control prevents escapes and creates openings.

  • Leverage > Strength

    • BJJ's cornerstone. Using angles, body mechanics, and timing to apply force efficiently, allowing smaller practitioners to manage larger opponents. Think physics, not just muscle.

  • Base, Posture, Structure

    • Base: Stable connection to the ground, hard to move. Posture: Strong spinal alignment (especially in guard) to resist control/attacks. Structure: Using limbs as effective frames (to create space) or levers (to apply force/control).

Fundamental Movements

The building blocks of all BJJ techniques. Master these for effective transitions, escapes, and attacks.

  • Shrimping (Hip Escape)

    • Moving hips away laterally to create space. Essential for escaping pins (Mount, Side Control) and guard retention.

  • Bridging (Upa)

    • Explosive hip lift to disrupt opponent's base, create space, or initiate reversals (especially from Mount).

  • Posting: Using limbs on mat/opponent for balance.
  • Sprawl: Defending takedowns by driving hips back/down.
  • Technical Stand-up: Safely rising from ground while protecting.
  • Rolling/Turning: Forward/backward shoulder rolls, Granby roll for defense/transitions.
  • Pummeling: Fighting for underhook control in clinch/guard.
Positional Hierarchy

Control leads to submissions. Understand the common positions from most to least dominant (generally for the top player).

  1. 1. Back Mount / Back Control: Highest control/attack potential. Opponent can't see.
  2. 2. Mount (Full Mount): Dominant chest-on-chest, controls hips.
  3. 3. Knee-on-Belly: High pressure, mobile, good for transitions.
  4. 4. Side Control: Stable control from side (variations: Kesa Gatame, North-South).
  5. 5. Guard (Top): Must pass opponent's legs.
  6. 6. Guard (Bottom): Using legs for control; aim to sweep/submit.
  7. 7. Turtle: Defensive shell, vulnerable back exposure.
  8. Note: Being on the bottom of Mount/Side/etc., means you are in a disadvantageous position needing escape.
The Guard (Bottom)

Your primary defensive and offensive hub when on your back. Use legs to control distance, prevent passes, sweep, or submit.

  • Closed Guard : Legs locked. Strong control.

    • Classic guard. Controls posture well, many high-percentage attacks (armbars, triangles, sweeps).

  • Open Guard : Legs unlocked. More dynamic.

    • A family of guards where legs aren't locked. Requires active feet/legs to manage distance and angles. Includes:

    • Spider: Feet on biceps/hips, sleeve grips (Gi).
    • Butterfly: Feet hooks inside thighs (seated/supine).
    • De La Riva / Reverse DLR: One leg hooks opponent's leg.
    • Lasso: Leg wraps around opponent's arm (Gi).
  • Half Guard : Controlling one leg.

    • Trapping one of the opponent's legs. Can be defensive (survival) or offensive (sweeps/back takes). Variations: Deep Half, Z-Guard, Lockdown.

Guard Concepts
Guard Passing (Top)

Navigating past the opponent's legs (guard) to secure a dominant pin like Side Control or Mount.

  • Pressure Passing: Staying close, using weight and control grips.
    • Knee Slice / Cut Pass
    • Over-Under Pass
    • Double Under Pass
    • Stack Pass
  • Speed/Dynamic Passing: Using movement, redirection, and distance.
    • Toreando / Bullfighter Pass
    • Leg Drag Pass
    • X-Pass
  • Key Principles: Control grips (legs/hips/upper body), break posture, manage distance, clear leg lines.
Passing Concepts
Sweeps (From Guard)

Reversing the position from bottom guard to achieve a top position. Off-balancing is key.

  • From Closed Guard:
    • Scissor Sweep
    • Hip Bump Sweep
    • Flower/Pendulum Sweep
  • From Open Guard:
    • Butterfly Sweep (Hook Sweep)
    • Tripod Sweep
    • Sickle Sweep
    • De La Riva Sweeps
    • Spider Guard Sweeps
  • From Half Guard:
    • Old School / Plan B Sweep
    • John Wayne Sweep
    • Deep Half Sweeps
  • Core Idea: Disrupt base, elevate hips, create angle, use leverage.

Submissions (Finishes)

Techniques forcing submission ("Tap") via chokes (blood/air restriction) or joint locks (hyperextension/rotation).

Chokes
  • RNC (Rear Naked Choke): From Back. High percentage.
  • Guillotine: Front Headlock. Standing or ground.
  • Triangle Choke: Legs around neck/arm. From Guard/Mount.
  • Arm Triangle / Head & Arm Choke: From Top (Side/Mount).
  • Cross Collar Chokes (Gi): From Mount/Guard. X-Choke, Sliding Collar.
  • Bow & Arrow Choke (Gi): From Back. Powerful.
Joint Locks
  • Armbar (Juji Gatame): Elbow hyperextension. Versatile.
  • Kimura: Figure-four shoulder lock (internal rotation).
  • Americana (Keylock): Figure-four shoulder lock (external rotation).
  • Omoplata: Shoulder lock using legs vs arm/shoulder.
  • Straight Ankle Lock: Basic leg lock (foot hyperextension).
  • Kneebar: Knee hyperextension.
  • Advanced: Heel Hooks, Toe Holds, Wrist Locks.
Submission Ideas
Escapes & Defense

Surviving bad positions and submission threats. Defense is paramount for longevity and learning.

Positional Escapes
  • Mount Escape: Upa (Bridge & Roll), Elbow-Knee Escape (Shrimping).
  • Side Control Escape: Reguarding (Shrimp to Guard), Framing & Turning, Ghost Escape.
  • Back Control Escape: Hand/Grip Fighting, Turning In, Scooting Down.
  • Knee-on-Belly Escape: Framing knee, Shrimping away.
  • Turtle Defense: Protecting neck/back, looking for reguard/standup.
Submission Defense
  • Armbar Defense: Stacking, Hitchhiker Escape, Grip Breaks.
  • Triangle Defense: Posture Up, Angle Change, Pass Arm Across.
  • Choke Defense: Protect Neck ("Chin Tuck"), Hand Fighting, Creating Space.
  • Kimura/Americana Defense: Straighten Arm, Grab Own Belt/Thigh, Turn Body.
  • Leg Lock Defense: "Booting" Foot, Turning Correct Direction, Clearing Knee Line.

Takedowns & Standing

Bringing the fight to the ground. BJJ utilizes techniques from Wrestling, Judo, and Sambo.

  • Wrestling Based:
    • Double Leg Takedown
    • Single Leg Takedown (various finishes)
    • High Crotch Takedown
    • Ankle Pick
  • Judo Based:
    • Osoto Gari (Major Outer Reaping)
    • O Goshi (Major Hip Throw)
    • Seoi Nage (Shoulder Throw)
    • Uchi Mata (Inner Thigh Throw)
    • Foot Sweeps (De Ashi Barai, etc.)
  • Guard Pulling: Intentionally going to a guard position from standing.
  • Clinch Work: Upper body control (Underhooks, Overhooks, Body Lock).

Training & Progression

Learning structure, advancement, and gym culture.

Training Methods
  • Warm-ups: Movement drills (shrimping, bridging etc.).
  • Technique Drilling: Repetitive practice with partner.
  • Positional Sparring: Rolling from specific spots (Guard Pass, Mount Escape etc.).
  • Rolling (Sparring): Live grappling applying techniques.

Gym Etiquette
  • Hygiene FIRST: Clean Gi/No-Gi gear *every* time. Clean body, trim nails.
  • Safety: Tap early/often. Be aware of space/partners. No shoes on mat.
  • Respect: Instructor, partners (esp. higher belts), bowing, listening.
  • Punctuality & Focus: Be on time, minimize chatter during instruction.
  • Health: Don't train sick or with skin infections.
Gi vs. No-Gi

Two styles of BJJ training with different attire and tactical approaches.

Gi (Kimono)
  • Attire: Traditional uniform (Jacket, Pants, Belt).
  • Emphasis: Grips on the fabric are crucial for control, chokes, and setting up techniques.
  • Pros/Tactics

    • Slower pace generally, more friction. Emphasizes breaking grips, strong posture defense via collars/sleeves. Many collar chokes and Gi-specific guards (Lasso, Spider).

No-Gi
  • Attire: Rashguard, Spats/Shorts.
  • Emphasis: Grips on body parts (wrist, neck, ankle) and body locks (over/underhooks). Friction is lower.
  • Pros/Tactics

    • Faster pace generally, more scrambles. Emphasizes wrestling concepts, leg locks (more prevalent/varied often), guillotines, RNC, arm triangles. Focus on controlling limbs and body position directly.

Most schools recommend training both for well-rounded development.

Tips for Success

Advice for navigating the BJJ journey effectively and enjoyably.

  • Consistency is King: Show up regularly, even for short sessions. Mat time compounds.
  • Focus on Fundamentals: Master escapes, posture, basic positions before complex techniques.
  • Prioritize Defense: Learn to survive before you learn to submit. Good defense builds confidence.
  • Ask Questions: Don't be afraid to ask instructors or senior belts for clarification.
  • Manage Your Ego: Everyone taps. See rolling as learning, not winning/losing.
  • Train with Everyone: Experience different body types, styles, and skill levels (safely).
  • Study Off the Mat: Watch instructionals, competition footage, analyze your rolls.
  • Rest & Recover: Overtraining leads to injury and burnout. Listen to your body.
  • Have Fun! Enjoy the process, the community, and the challenge.
