Cooling decision thresholds:
air → containment → RDHx → liquid → immersion

There is no universal “air limit.” The practical threshold is where your rack airflow, temperature rise, fan power, redundancy, acoustics, and room distribution stop closing simultaneously. Use this ladder to screen an architecture—then validate it against the actual server thermal report and a failure-mode design.

Core rule: density is a trigger for investigation, not a product-selection algorithm. At the same 20 kW/rack, a clean contained hall may stay on air while a legacy raised-floor room with recirculation needs a rear-door exchanger.

02 / Fundamentals

Turn watts into flow before choosing hardware

Nearly every watt delivered to IT becomes heat in the data hall. Cooling capacity and transport are separate problems: a chiller may have spare tons while a rack starves for airflow.

Heat balance
Q̇ = PIT

For a steady rack boundary, 40 kW electrical draw means approximately 40 kW thermal rejection. Use metered input or the vendor thermal report.

Airflow
CFM ≈ 3,160 × kW ÷ ΔT°F

From Q = 1.08 × CFM × ΔT. At 20°F rise: ~158 CFM/kW. Altitude, humidity, leakage, and fan curves change the result.

Water flow
L/min ≈ 14.3 × kW ÷ ΔT°C

For water near room temperature. At a 10°C loop rise: ~1.43 L/min per kW before glycol/property and margin corrections.

Worked example — a sustained 40 kW rack at 20°F air rise
1 / Heat40 kW = 136,480 Btu/h
2 / Airflow136,480 ÷ (1.08 × 20)
3 / Result≈ 6,319 CFM
4 / Meaning~149 air changes/min through a 42U rack face*

*Illustrative velocity framing, not a fan selection. Using a nominal 24 × 79 in rack face (~13.2 ft²), 6,319 CFM implies ~479 ft/min face velocity. Real open area is smaller and pressure drop matters.

~3,000×
Volumetric heat-capacity advantage

LBNL training material states it takes roughly 2,000–3,000 times the volume of air to cool what water can; the point is transport work, not that every liquid system is automatically efficient.

18–27°C
Recommended inlet band

ASHRAE’s recommended air temperature envelope is 18–27°C (64.4–80.6°F). Measure server inlets—room thermostat temperature can hide recirculation.

RCI = 100%
No recommended-range excursions

DOE’s Rack Cooling Index framing: 100% means no intakes outside the recommended range; below 90% signals poor thermal conditions. Trend top/middle/bottom inlet probes.

120 kW
A real rack anchor, not a forecast

NVIDIA documents approximately 120 kW for an NVL72 rack. The OCP MGX spec also uses 120 kW rack power and an 85% liquid heat-capture requirement, leaving residual air cooling.

04 / Working knowledge

Seven regimes, seven different constraints

A regime earns its place when it changes the limiting mechanism or the operating model—not merely because a vendor renamed a coil.

CRAC / CRAH room air

Purpose: reject whole-room heat through DX refrigerant (CRAC) or chilled-water coil (CRAH).

Use: low/mixed density with measured inlet control. Gotcha: installed tons do not prove air reaches the rack; verify fan curve and return path at failure state.

Hot/cold aisle containment

Purpose: stop supply and exhaust mixing so each CFM performs useful transport.

Use: before buying exotic cooling. Gotcha: open U spaces, cable cutouts, fire-system interfaces, and doors left open defeat the pressure boundary.

In-row air

Purpose: shorten the source-to-coil path and add capacity beside a dense row.

Use: localized retrofit. Gotcha: unit failure can create a sharp local transient; distributed valves, drains, fans, and controls multiply maintenance points.

Rear-door heat exchanger

Purpose: transfer server exhaust to liquid without modifying server internals.

Use: 20–80 kW product range or staged liquid transition. Gotcha: passive doors add pressure drop to server fans; concentrated hot spots can underuse coil area.

Single-phase DTC

Purpose: circulate water/glycol or dielectric liquid through cold plates while it remains liquid.

Use: OEM-qualified CPUs/GPUs and warm-water loops. Gotcha: memory, drives, PSUs, NICs, and switches may remain air-cooled; capture ratio is never assumed to be 100%.

Two-phase DTC

Purpose: boil dielectric fluid at the cold plate and condense it in a closed loop.

Use: high heat flux or waterless white space. Current ZutaCore rack units span 20–120 kW (as of Jul 2026). Gotcha: fluid availability, recovery, seals, and supplier dependence.

Immersion

Purpose: submerge compatible IT in dielectric fluid, removing most server air paths.

Use: dense standardized fleets with redesigned service. Gotcha: warranty/material compatibility, lifting, optics/cabling, drip control, fluid aging, and tank-level fault domains.

Hybrid is the normal end state

Purpose: liquid-cool dominant silicon while air serves residual components and conventional racks.

Example: NVIDIA documents liquid-cooled CPU/GPU/NVSwitch devices and air-cooled remaining components. Gotcha: “liquid cooled” never licenses removal of the air budget.

Heat rejection is still required

Purpose: move heat from CDU/tank loop to dry cooler, cooling tower, chiller, district heat, or another sink.

Gotcha: closed-loop liquid can have zero direct water consumption and still reject heat through a water-using facility plant—or vice versa.

05 / Edge & advanced

Design the loop, failure state, and exit path

High-density cooling is an interface problem: IT, controls, facility water, heat rejection, electrical redundancy, and service procedures have to agree on the same boundary conditions.

ASHRAE W classes: the name is the upper facility-supply temperature

As of the ASHRAE framework checked July 2026, all classes share a 2°C lower bound. W17, W27, W32, W40, W45 name their upper facility-supply temperature; W+ means above 45°C. Select equipment certified for the entire chosen class—not merely one nominal setpoint.

Decision: W17/W27 commonly implies colder infrastructure; W32/W40/W45 expands economizer/dry-cooler hours and heat-reuse quality, but only qualified IT can accept it. Check approach temperature from facility loop → CDU → technology loop → cold plate.

Hybrid load split: calculate residual air instead of quoting “fully liquid”

Use Qair = Prack × (1 − liquid heat-capture ratio). For the OCP MGX anchor, 120 kW × (1 − 0.85) = 18 kW remains outside the liquid-captured load. Then add any rack elements outside the stated boundary and validate the OEM thermal report. NVIDIA’s current GB200 documentation explicitly describes hybrid trays: major processors and switches are liquid-cooled; remaining components are air-cooled.

N+1: redundancy applies to flow and heat rejection, not just nameplate tons

Test loss of one CDU pump, one CDU, one facility pump, one heat-rejection cell, and utility-water interruption. Confirm check-valve behavior, automatic isolation, ride-through time, thermal mass, controls fail-position, and which racks shed load. A 1 MW CDU does not provide N+1 to a 900 kW load if it is the only CDU.

Climate and water: Phoenix and Minneapolis do not get the same “free cooling”

ASHRAE notes many locations can economize for as much as 50% of annual hours within the 18–27°C recommended envelope, but actual hours depend on dry-bulb, wet-bulb, humidity, air quality, and selected water class. Price annual energy and water from an hourly weather file; never transplant a vendor PUE from a different climate.

Heat reuse: higher-grade heat helps, but a customer and timing still matter

Track Energy Reuse Effectiveness (ERE) when exported heat displaces useful energy; PUE alone does not credit reuse. Warm W40/W45/W+ return can reduce temperature lift, yet value requires a nearby coincident sink, heat exchanger, backup, metering boundary, and contract. “100% recoverable” is not “100% economically reused.”

Two-phase vs single-phase: density alone does not decide

Two-phase can remove high chip heat flux at low mass flow; ZutaCore states 0.3 L/min per 1,000 W for its system and current in-rack units from 20 to 120 kW. Single-phase offers a broader water-cooling ecosystem and simpler fluid accounting. Compare qualified chip list, approach temperature, pump/compressor energy, fluid regulation and replacement, service recovery, control response, and supplier exit plan.

06 / Common mistakes

Failure begins in the assumptions

A useful design review attacks the input boundary before debating brands.

Using room average

Failure: 600 kW across 100 racks looks like 6 kW/rack while two GPU racks each peak at 80 kW.

Fix: size distribution from per-rack and per-row peaks plus coincidence; size plant from diversified load with explicit margin.

Adding PSU nameplates

Failure: nameplate maximum is mistaken for simultaneous heat. DOE’s Air Management Tool explicitly says not to substitute nameplate for measured power.

Fix: use branch/rack meters or vendor thermal reports; preserve a documented growth case.

Confusing IT and facility losses

Failure: UPS/PDU losses are blindly added to rack heat even when the equipment is outside the white space.

Fix: draw boundaries. Rack cooling uses delivered IT heat; facility plant must reject electrical and mechanical losses where they physically occur.

Believing “up to”

Failure: an 80 kW door rating is used without its entering-water temperature, flow, air volume, pressure drop, fouling, or redundancy conditions.

Fix: require a certified duty point and fan/pump curves at normal and failure states.

Assuming 100% heat capture

Failure: liquid-cooled CPUs/GPUs lead to deletion of room airflow.

Fix: use OEM component mapping and heat-capture ratio; calculate residual kW and verify inlet temperatures.

Ignoring maintenance geometry

Failure: doors cannot swing, hoses kink, tanks block egress, CDUs steal rack U, or lifted wet servers have no drip/service zone.

Fix: model service clearances, floor loading, pipe strain, containment, rigging, drainage, and concurrent maintenance.

Comparing PUE claims

Failure: a subsystem projection, vendor lab number, and annual whole-facility PUE are put in one ranking.

Fix: normalize boundary, annual weather, load factor, redundancy mode, water, and what parasitic energy is included.

Skipping controls commissioning

Failure: pumps fight, valves hunt, condensation alarms are untested, or facility and IT loops use incompatible setpoints.

Fix: functional-test sensor failure, low flow, high dew point, leak, power loss, restart, and degraded heat rejection.

No exit strategy

Failure: proprietary fluids, cold plates, manifolds, or firmware lock a fleet to one supplier.

Fix: document compatible alternatives, drain/flush plan, spares horizon, data/API ownership, and decommissioning cost.
