I. What is Objectivism?

Objectivism is a comprehensive, integrated philosophical system created by Ayn Rand — a philosophy for living on Earth, based on objective reality and man's use of reason as his absolute. It covers the traditional branches of philosophy, and Rand held that philosophy is not an intellectual luxury but a fundamental necessity for human survival and flourishing.

  • Definition: A comprehensive, integrated philosophical system created by Ayn Rand.
  • Core idea: A philosophy for living on Earth, based on objective reality and reason as an absolute.
  • Scope: The traditional branches — Metaphysics (reality), Epistemology (knowledge), Ethics (a moral code), Politics (the proper social system), and Aesthetics (art).
  • How to use this cheatsheet: a quick reference and overview — not a substitute for reading Rand's primary works. Objectivism is a hierarchical system; the interconnections between its branches matter.

◆ Foundational Pillars

The self-evident truths and core concepts at the base of the system.

The Axioms
Self-evident, irreducible truths at the base of all knowledge.OPAR Ch. 1; IOE 55–62
  • Existence Exists — reality is absolute.
  • Consciousness is Conscious — it perceives existence.
  • A is A (Identity) — everything has a specific nature.
Law of Causality
Identity applied to action; entities act according to their nature.OPAR Ch. 1
Primacy of Existence
Reality is primary, consciousness secondary (it perceives reality). Rejects all forms of idealism/subjectivism.OPAR Ch. 1; PWNI
Volition
Man possesses free will — primarily the choice to focus his consciousness ("to think or not to think"). Axiomatic via introspection.OPAR Ch. 2
Reason
Man's faculty for knowing reality, operating via logic based on sensory evidence. His basic tool of knowledge and survival.OPAR Ch. 5; VOS
Objectivity
Adherence to reality via logic within the full context of one's knowledge. Applies to concepts, knowledge, and values. Neither intrinsic nor subjective.OPAR Ch. 4; IOE Ch. 5

II. Branches of Philosophy

A · Metaphysics

Objective Reality

Core principle: Existence exists — reality is absolute. Identity: A is A.

Key ideas

  • Reality exists objectively and absolutely, independent of anyone's consciousness.OPAR Ch. 1
  • Primacy of Existence: consciousness perceives reality, it does not create it.OPAR Ch. 1
  • Rejection of supernaturalism: only this universe exists.OPAR Ch. 1
  • Causality: entities act according to their nature.OPAR Ch. 1

Nature, to be commanded, must be obeyed. / You can't fake reality.

B · Epistemology

Reason

Core principle: Reason is man's only means of knowledge and his basic tool of survival.

Key ideas

  • Knowledge starts with valid sense perception.OPAR Ch. 2
  • Concepts are formed objectively via measurement omission (abstracting common characteristics while omitting specific measurements).OPAR Ch. 3; IOE
  • Logic is the noncontradictory method of identification.OPAR Ch. 4
  • Objectivity demands adherence to reality via logic, context, and hierarchy.OPAR Ch. 4
  • Knowledge is hierarchical; complex concepts depend on simpler, previously validated ones.IOE Ch. 6
  • Rejects all non-rational paths to knowledge (faith, emotion, revelation).OPAR Ch. 5
  • Certainty is contextual but achievable — distinct from the arbitrary, the possible, and the merely probable.OPAR Ch. 5; IOE

Check your premises. / Reason is man's only absolute. / A is A.

C · Ethics

Rational Self-Interest

Core principle: Each individual is an end in himself. His own life is his ultimate standard of value; his own happiness is his highest moral purpose.

Key ideas

  • Rational self-interest ("selfishness"): pursuing values for one's rational survival and flourishing.OPAR Ch. 7; VOS
  • Life as the standard of value: that which furthers "man's life qua man" is the good.OPAR Ch. 7
  • Flourishing (happiness): non-contradictory joy from attaining one's rational values.VOS
  • Cardinal values: Reason, Purpose, Self-Esteem.OPAR Ch. 7
  • Major virtues: Rationality (primary), Productiveness, Pride, Independence, Integrity, Honesty, Justice (incl. the Trader Principle).OPAR Ch. 7–8; VOS
  • The Trader Principle: relationships based on voluntary exchange to mutual benefit — sacrificing neither self to others nor others to self.VOS
  • Emotions are automatic consequences of one's premises and values, not tools of cognition.VOS
  • Rejection of altruism (self-sacrifice for others).OPAR Ch. 7
  • Rejection of hedonism (whim-worship).OPAR Ch. 9

The Virtue of Selfishness. / Live for your own sake.

D · Politics

Laissez-Faire Capitalism

Core principle: The recognition and protection of individual rights is the foundation of a moral social system.

Key ideas

  • Individual rights: Life, Liberty, Property, Pursuit of Happiness (derived from man's nature as a rational being).OPAR Ch. 10; VOS
  • No initiation of physical force — the core political principle. Force is permissible only in retaliation.OPAR Ch. 8, 10
  • Government's sole purpose: protect rights by banning initiated force (police, military, courts under objective law).OPAR Ch. 10; VOS
  • Objective law: clearly defined, knowable, consistently enforced, protecting rights. Includes robust contract law.VOS
  • Retaliatory force: the only legitimate use of force, in self-defense or justice.VOS
  • Separation of state and economics (laissez-faire capitalism).OPAR Ch. 11; CUI
  • Rejection of collectivism (socialism, fascism, communism, welfare statism).OPAR Ch. 10; VOS
  • Rejection of anarchism.VOS

The smallest minority on earth is the individual. / Hands off!

E · Aesthetics

Romantic Realism

Core principle: Art is a selective re-creation of reality according to an artist's metaphysical value-judgments.

Key ideas

  • Concretizes metaphysics: allows direct perceptual grasp of abstract worldview elements.OPAR Ch. 12; RM
  • Serves a fundamental human need: spiritual/emotional fuel by concretizing a "Sense of Life."OPAR Ch. 12; RM
  • Sense of Life: a pre-conceptual, emotional, subconsciously integrated appraisal of man and existence, which art objectifies.RM
  • Psycho-epistemology in art: embodying abstractions in perceivable concretes, aligning with man's need to grasp universals through particulars.RM
  • Favored style: Romantic Realism — life "as it could be and ought to be," focused on volition, values, and heroic potential.OPAR Ch. 12; RM
  • Emphasis on intelligibility, integration, plot (in literature), purpose, heroism.OPAR Ch. 12; RM
  • Objective esthetic judgment is possible, based on philosophical principles and technical skill.OPAR Ch. 12

Art is the technology of the soul.

III. Key Terms & Concepts

A is A / Identity
Reality is absolute; things are what they are, independent of consciousness.
Primacy of Existence
Reality exists first and sets the terms; consciousness perceives reality, it does not create it.
Reason
Man's faculty for knowing reality via logic based on sensory evidence; his only means to knowledge and basic tool of survival.
Objectivity
The volitional adherence to reality by means of logic, within the full context of one's knowledge.
Measurement Omission
In concept formation, omitting specific quantitative measurements to form an abstraction applicable to all concretes of a kind.
Hierarchy of Knowledge
The structure whereby complex concepts and principles logically depend on simpler ones derived from perceptual reality.
Rational Self-Interest
The ethical principle that one should act to achieve one's own well-being and happiness, guided by reason.
Trader Principle
Human relationships based on voluntary, uncoerced exchange of values (material or spiritual) to mutual benefit.
Individual Rights
Moral sanction to act free from physical compulsion, based on man's nature as a rational being requiring liberty to live and think.
Retaliatory Force
The use of physical force only in response to those who initiate it, for self-defense or justice.
Capitalism (Laissez-Faire)
The social system protecting individual rights, with complete separation of state and economics; all property privately owned.
Sense of Life
A pre-conceptual equivalent of metaphysics; an emotional, subconsciously integrated appraisal of man and existence.
Psycho-Epistemology
The study of man's cognitive processes, especially concept formation and the cognitive function of art.
Benevolent Universe Premise
The conviction that the universe is knowable and conducive to human achievement and happiness, given rational effort.
Altruism
The doctrine that man has no right to exist for his own sake, that service to others is the only justification of his existence, and self-sacrifice his highest duty. Rejected.
Collectivism
The political theory that subordinates the individual to the group or state, treating the group as primary. Rejected.
Mysticism / Faith
Acceptance of ideas without evidence or from purported non-rational sources (revelation, intuition, emotion). Rejected.
Intrinsicism
That values (or concepts) are inherent in external reality, independent of consciousness or context. Rejected (objectivity is the mean between intrinsicism and subjectivism).
Subjectivism
That values (or concepts) are created by an individual's consciousness or feelings, independent of reality. Rejected.
Malevolent Universe Premise
The conviction that the universe is hostile or indifferent to man, and that success and happiness are impossible or accidental. Rejected.

V. Common Misconceptions

  • "Objectivist 'selfishness' means doing whatever you feel like (hedonism)." Correction It means rational self-interest — pursuing long-term survival and flourishing via reason, which often requires overriding destructive feelings or short-term whims. Guided by objective principles, not subjective desires.
  • "Objectivism is anarchism because it distrusts government." Correction It advocates a limited government whose sole function is protecting individual rights by retaliating against those who initiate force; it explicitly rejects anarchism as impractical, self-contradictory, and destructive of rights.
  • "Objectivism is the same as Libertarianism." Correction While sharing the political goal of laissez-faire capitalism, Objectivism bases it on a comprehensive philosophical system (metaphysics, epistemology, ethics) that differs from the diverse and often contradictory — or absent — philosophical bases of Libertarianism.
  • "Rejecting altruism means Objectivists are unkind or against charity." Correction Objectivism rejects the moral duty to sacrifice for others. It does not oppose voluntary benevolence, generosity, or emergency aid given freely, provided it involves no self-sacrifice and aligns with one's rational hierarchy of values.

VI. Disclaimer

For a complete and accurate understanding, reading Ayn Rand's primary works and associated Objectivist literature (such as Peikoff's OPAR) is essential.
