History of Israel

An Interactive Timeline Extensively Linked to Primary Sources & Treaties

Ancient Period (c. 1200 BCE – 63 BCE)

c. 1200 - 1000 BCE Emergence of Israel in Canaan
  • The Exodus & Conquest: According to biblical tradition, Israelites escape Egypt, wander the desert, and conquer Canaan. Historically, this aligns with the Late Bronze Age collapse.
  • Period of the Judges: A decentralized tribal confederation lacking a central government. Leadership arises in times of crisis via figures like Deborah and Gideon.
  • The Merneptah Stele (c. 1208 BCE): An ancient Egyptian inscription by Pharaoh Merneptah. It contains the earliest known extra-biblical reference to a people called "Israel" in Canaan, stating: "Israel is laid waste, its seed is not." Britannica: Merneptah Stele
c. 1000 - 930 BCE The United Monarchy
  • King Saul & David: Saul becomes the first king to unify the tribes. King David subsequently establishes Jerusalem as the capital and secures borders against the Philistines. Tel Dan Stele (House of David Evidence)
  • King Solomon (c. 970–931 BCE): Constructs the First Temple (Solomon's Temple) on Mount Moriah in Jerusalem. An era characterized by international trade, wealth, and extensive fortification projects (e.g., at Hazor and Megiddo).
930 - 586 BCE Divided Kingdoms & First Exile
  • The Split (930 BCE): The kingdom fractures into the Northern Kingdom (Israel, capital Samaria) and Southern Kingdom (Judah, capital Jerusalem).
  • Assyrian Conquest (722 BCE): The Neo-Assyrian Empire under Shalmaneser V and Sargon II destroys the Northern Kingdom. The population is deported, leading to the legend of the "Ten Lost Tribes."
  • Babylonian Exile (586 BCE): King Nebuchadnezzar II of Babylon conquers Judah, destroys the First Temple, and exiles the Jewish elite to Babylon. British Museum: Babylonian Chronicles
538 - 63 BCE Return to Zion & Hasmonean Dynasty
  • Decree of Cyrus (538 BCE): Cyrus the Great of Persia conquers Babylon and allows Jews to return to Judea and rebuild the Temple (completed c. 516 BCE, initiating the Second Temple Period). The Cyrus Cylinder
  • Hellenistic Rule (332 BCE): Alexander the Great conquers the Levant. Judea falls under Ptolemaic, then Seleucid rule.
  • Maccabean Revolt (167-160 BCE): A Jewish rebellion led by Judah Maccabee against the Seleucid King Antiochus IV Epiphanes, who outlawed Jewish practices. The rebels reclaim the Temple (origin of Hanukkah). Establish the independent Hasmonean Kingdom which lasts until Roman intervention.

Roman & Middle Ages (63 BCE – 1517 CE)

63 BCE - 73 CE Roman Rule & The Great Revolt
  • Roman Conquest (63 BCE): General Pompey captures Jerusalem. Judea becomes a Roman client kingdom.
  • Herodian Dynasty (37 - 4 BCE): King Herod the Great significantly expands the Second Temple and builds fortresses like Masada.
  • First Jewish-Roman War (66 - 73 CE): The Great Revolt against Roman taxation and cultural suppression. Josephus: The Jewish War
  • Destruction of the Second Temple (70 CE): Roman legions commanded by Titus besiege Jerusalem and destroy the Temple on Tisha B'Av.
  • Fall of Masada (73 CE): The last Jewish holdouts at the desert fortress of Masada commit mass suicide rather than surrender to the Romans.
132 - 636 CE Bar Kokhba, Byzantine & Talmudic Eras
  • Bar Kokhba Revolt (132 - 136 CE): Simon bar Kokhba leads a massive rebellion against Emperor Hadrian. The Romans crush the revolt, resulting in hundreds of thousands of Jewish deaths. Hadrian renames the province Syria Palaestina to erase its Jewish connection, and bans Jews from Jerusalem.
  • Rabbinic Judaism (c. 200 - 500 CE): With the Temple gone, Judaism transitions to a text-and-synagogue focus. The Sanhedrin moves to the Galilee. Judah ha-Nasi compiles the Mishnah (c. 200 CE), and the Jerusalem Talmud is formalized (c. 400 CE).
  • Byzantine Period (324 - 638 CE): The Roman Empire Christianizes. Emperor Constantine builds the Church of the Holy Sepulchre. Jews face increasing legal restrictions.
636 - 1517 CE Islamic Conquests, Crusaders & Mamluks
  • Arab Conquest (636 CE): Rashidun Caliphate captures Jerusalem. Caliph Abd al-Malik commissions the Dome of the Rock (completed 691 CE) on the Temple Mount.
  • Crusader Period (1099 - 1291): Following the First Crusade, European Christians conquer Jerusalem, massacring the city's Muslim and Jewish populations. They establish the Kingdom of Jerusalem. Fordham Univ: Crusades Sourcebook
  • Mamluk Sultanate (1291 - 1517): Egyptian-based Mamluks drive out the Crusaders. Jerusalem becomes a provincial religious center but the region as a whole suffers economic and demographic stagnation.

Ottoman Rule (1517 – 1917)

1517 - 1800 Early Ottoman Era & The "Old Yishuv"
  • Ottoman Conquest (1517): Sultan Selim I conquers the Levant from the Mamluks. His successor, Suleiman the Magnificent, rebuilds the Old City walls of Jerusalem (1537-1541), which still stand today.
  • Rise of Safed: In the 16th century, the Galilee city of Safed becomes a global center of Jewish mysticism (Kabbalah), led by figures like Rabbi Isaac Luria (The Arizal) and Joseph Caro (author of the Shulchan Aruch).
  • The "Old Yishuv": The pre-Zionist Jewish population of Palestine. They lived primarily in the Four Holy Cities (Jerusalem, Hebron, Safed, Tiberias), were deeply religious, and heavily relied on the Halukka (charitable funds collected from diaspora communities).
1800 - 1880 19th Century Shifts & Demographics
  • Egyptian Rule (1832 - 1840): Ibrahim Pasha of Egypt briefly conquers the region, introducing modernizing reforms and centralized administration before the Ottomans (with British help) retake it.
  • Tanzimat Reforms (1839 - 1876): The Ottoman Empire enacts sweeping reforms granting more rights to non-Muslim minorities (the Millet system) and allowing foreigners to purchase land, setting the stage for later settlements.
  • Expansion of Jerusalem (1860): British Jewish philanthropist Sir Moses Montefiore funds the building of Mishkenot Sha'ananim, the first Jewish neighborhood constructed outside the cramped walls of the Old City.
1881 - 1914 The Aliyahs & Political Zionism
  • The First Aliyah (1881 - 1903): Fleeing brutal pogroms in the Russian Empire, early Jewish pioneers (the "New Yishuv") begin organized agricultural settlement, establishing towns like Rishon LeZion and Petah Tikva, heavily subsidized by Baron Edmond de Rothschild.
  • First Zionist Congress (1897): Organized by Austro-Hungarian journalist Theodor Herzl in Basel, Switzerland, defining Zionism's modern political goal: "To establish a home for the Jewish people in Palestine secured under public law." The Basel Program (1897)
  • The Second Aliyah (1904 - 1914): A wave of Russian Jewish immigrants bringing socialist ideals. They establish the first collective farm or Kibbutz (Degania, 1909) and found the city of Tel Aviv (1909). Eliezer Ben-Yehuda spearheads the revival of Hebrew as a spoken language.

British Mandate (1917 – 1948)

1915 - 1917 WWI Diplomacy & Contradictory Promises
  • McMahon-Hussein Correspondence (1915-1916): The British promise independence to the Arabs (via the Sharif of Mecca) in exchange for an Arab revolt against the Ottoman Empire.
  • Sykes-Picot Agreement (1916): A secret Anglo-French pact dividing the Middle East into spheres of influence, placing Palestine under international administration. Sykes-Picot Agreement Text
  • Balfour Declaration (Nov 2, 1917): UK Foreign Secretary Arthur Balfour issues a letter stating His Majesty's Government views with favor the establishment in Palestine of a "national home for the Jewish people." Text of the Balfour Declaration
  • Conquest of Jerusalem (Dec 1917): British General Edmund Allenby captures Jerusalem from the Ottomans, ending 400 years of Ottoman rule.
1920 - 1929 Early Mandate & Rising Tensions
  • Establishment of the Mandate (1920-1922): The League of Nations formally assigns Britain the Mandate over Palestine, embedding the Balfour Declaration into international law. The Mandate for Palestine (1922)
  • Institution Building: The Jewish community (Yishuv) establishes self-governing institutions, including the Jewish Agency, the Histadrut (labor union), and the Haganah (defense paramilitary).
  • Intercommunal Violence: Rising Jewish immigration triggers Arab anxieties over dispossession. Violence erupts during the Nebi Musa riots (1920) and Jaffa riots (1921).
  • 1929 Hebron Massacre: Tensions over access to the Western Wall escalate into nationwide riots. Over 130 Jews are killed, including 67 in Hebron, leading to the evacuation of the ancient Jewish community there. Over 110 Arabs are killed, mostly by British police quelling the riots.
1936 - 1939 The Arab Revolt & The White Paper
  • The Great Arab Revolt (1936 - 1939): Palestinian Arabs launch a massive nationalist uprising demanding an end to Jewish immigration and British rule. Britain suppresses the revolt brutally, decimating Arab leadership.
  • The Peel Commission (1937): A British inquiry concludes that the Mandate is unworkable due to irreconcilable national aspirations. It recommends partitioning the land into separate Jewish and Arab states—the first official partition proposal. Rejected by Arab leadership; heavily debated by Jewish leadership. Peel Commission Report
  • The White Paper of 1939: Yielding to Arab pressure on the eve of WWII, Britain drastically curtails Jewish immigration to 75,000 over five years and restricts land purchases, trapping millions of Jews in Europe as the Holocaust begins.
1939 - 1947 WWII, the Holocaust & The Jewish Insurgency
  • World War II & The Jewish Brigade: Over 30,000 Palestinian Jews volunteer for the British Army. David Ben-Gurion states: "We will fight the war as if there is no White Paper, and we will fight the White Paper as if there is no war."
  • Post-War Crisis & Holocaust Survivors: Following the Holocaust, hundreds of thousands of Jewish displaced persons (DPs) attempt to reach Palestine. The British maintain the blockade. High-profile incidents, like the British boarding of the refugee ship SS Exodus in 1947, turn global public opinion against Britain.
  • Jewish Insurgency (1944 - 1947): Jewish underground groups (the Irgun led by Menachem Begin, and the radical Lehi) launch a guerilla war against British infrastructure and personnel. The 1946 bombing of the King David Hotel (British HQ) by the Irgun kills 91 people.
  • UN Partition Plan (Nov 29, 1947): Exhausted and bankrupt, Britain hands the problem to the United Nations. UN Resolution 181 proposes dividing Palestine into independent Arab and Jewish states, with an international regime for Jerusalem. Accepted by the Jewish Agency, rejected by the Arab Higher Committee. UN Resolution 181

State of Israel (1948 – Present)

1948 - 1949 Independence & The First Arab-Israeli War
  • Declaration of Independence (May 14, 1948): David Ben-Gurion proclaims the State of Israel hours before the British Mandate expires. Declaration of Israel's Independence
  • 1948 Arab-Israeli War: Surrounding nations (Egypt, Syria, Jordan, Iraq, Lebanon) invade. Israel survives and expands its borders beyond the UN partition lines.
  • The Armistice (1949): Borders known as the "Green Line" are established. Jordan annexes the West Bank and East Jerusalem; Egypt occupies the Gaza Strip.
  • Demographic Shift: Approx. 700,000 Palestinian Arabs flee or are expelled (the Nakba). Concurrently, over the next decade, approx. 850,000 Jewish refugees are expelled or flee from Arab and Muslim countries, most resettling in Israel.
1956 - 1973 Wars of Survival & Expansion
  • Suez Crisis (1956): Following Egypt's nationalization of the Suez Canal and blockade of the Straits of Tiran, Israel invades the Sinai Peninsula in a secret pact with the UK and France. The US and USSR force a withdrawal.
  • Six-Day War (June 1967): Facing troop buildups by Egypt and Syria, Israel launches preemptive strikes. In 6 days, Israel captures the West Bank and East Jerusalem from Jordan, the Gaza Strip and Sinai Peninsula from Egypt, and the Golan Heights from Syria. UN Res 242 (Land for Peace framework)
  • Yom Kippur War (Oct 1973): Egypt and Syria launch a massive surprise attack on Judaism's holiest day. Israel suffers heavy initial casualties but ultimately repels the invasion. The political fallout leads to the resignation of Prime Minister Golda Meir.
1978 - 1999 Peace Treaties & The First Intifada
  • Camp David Accords (1978) & Egypt-Israel Peace (1979): Brokered by US Pres. Jimmy Carter. Menachem Begin and Anwar Sadat sign the first peace treaty between Israel and an Arab state. Israel returns the Sinai to Egypt. The Camp David Accords
  • First Lebanon War (1982): Israel invades Lebanon to destroy PLO infrastructure. Israel maintains a security zone in southern Lebanon until 2000.
  • First Intifada (1987 - 1993): Spontaneous Palestinian uprising (protests, stone-throwing) in the West Bank and Gaza. Leads to the creation of the militant group Hamas.
  • Oslo Accords (1993, 1995): Yitzhak Rabin and Yasser Arafat sign agreements recognizing each other and establishing the Palestinian Authority (PA) with limited self-governance in parts of the West Bank and Gaza. Rabin is assassinated in 1995 by an Israeli extremist opposed to the peace process. Oslo I Declaration of Principles
  • Israel-Jordan Peace Treaty (1994): Signed by Yitzhak Rabin and King Hussein, resolving border disputes and normalizing relations.
2000 - Present Modern Conflict & Realignment
  • Second Intifada (2000 - 2005): A highly violent Palestinian uprising marked by suicide bombings targeting Israeli civilians, following the collapse of the Camp David Summit. Israel responds with military incursions and constructs the West Bank security barrier.
  • Gaza Disengagement (2005): Under Ariel Sharon, Israel unilaterally dismantles all settlements and withdraws its military from the Gaza Strip.
  • Hamas Takeover (2007): Following elections, Hamas violently ejects the PA/Fatah from Gaza. Israel and Egypt impose a strict blockade on the territory. Series of brief wars occur (2008, 2012, 2014, 2021).
  • The Abraham Accords (2020): US-brokered normalization agreements between Israel and the UAE, followed by Bahrain, Morocco, and Sudan. Shifts regional paradigms by separating Arab state normalization from the Palestinian issue. US State Dept: Abraham Accords
  • October 7 Attacks & War (2023 - Present): On Oct 7, 2023, Hamas launches a massive surprise terrorist assault on southern Israel, killing approximately 1,200 people and taking over 250 hostages. This triggers the most devastating war in Gaza to date and sparks widespread regional conflict involving Hezbollah (Lebanon), the Houthis (Yemen), and direct confrontations with Iran.
