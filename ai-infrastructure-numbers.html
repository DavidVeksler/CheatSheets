Power: chip → utility meter

Use boundary math, not GPU multiplication

Start with measured or engineered whole-server power. Add network and storage to reach IT load, then multiply by PUE for facility energy. For a quick upper envelope, four 10.2 kW DGX H100 systems make a 40.8 kW server rack; at PUE 1.17, that rack’s allocated facility burden is 47.7 kW before shared-load nuances.

facility power = (servers + network + storage) × PUE

Nameplate, demand, and energy answer different questions

Nameplate sizes safe circuits. Coincident peak demand sizes the service and capacity contract. Annual energy determines much of the bill and footprint. LBNL’s 2024 model used 60–80% of rated power for operating AI servers and 80% / 40% operational time for training / inference.

For 10.2 kW nameplate, 70% operating power × 80% operational time gives a 5.71 kW annual-average model—not permission to provision only 5.71 kW.

100 MW IT at PUE 1.56 = 156 MW facility. The 56 MW difference is cooling, power conversion, lighting, and other facility overhead. At PUE 1.17, the same IT load is 117 MW: PUE changes the overhead, never the 100 MW of compute.

Cooling: density changes the architecture

Air: optimize before replacing

Contain hot and cold aisles, seal bypass paths, manage blanking panels, and hold server inlets in the vendor/ASHRAE envelope. NVIDIA’s four-DGX rack is 40.8 kW and explicitly caps the reference pattern at four systems for thermodynamic reasons.

Use when: the equipment and rack density remain inside a validated airflow design. Do not use: a fleet-average kW/rack to excuse a hotter local rack.

Direct-to-chip: heat leaves in liquid

Cold plates take high-flux heat from CPUs/GPUs to a coolant distribution unit; residual DIMM, PSU, and network heat may still need air. AMD specifies 2.1 L/min per MI355X OAM and coolant inlet up to 43°C for its eight-GPU platform.

Use when: rack power and chip heat flux outrun economical air delivery. Do not assume: “liquid cooled” means zero facility water consumption.

Immersion / rear-door: different tradeoffs

Immersion places electronics in dielectric fluid; rear-door heat exchangers intercept exhaust at the rack. Both change service procedures, materials compatibility, leakage risk, floor load, and the facility loop.

Decision: compare total solution density—including CDUs and service clearance—not the bare tank or rack number.

Water: state the numerator and boundary

Site WUE is simple; interpretation is not

Microsoft defines WUE as annual liters used for cooling and humidification divided by annual IT kWh. Its FY25 owned-fleet values range from 0.03 L/kWh in EMEA to 0.34 in the Americas, with 0.27 global. Climate, cooling design, and load make a single global comparison hazardous.

100 MW IT × 24 h × 0.27 L/kWh = 648,000 L/day = 171,202 US gal/day

Direct and indirect water are different

Site WUE excludes water consumed by the electricity supply. LBNL models both because thermoelectric generation and hydropower can add a location-dependent indirect footprint. “Zero water for cooling” can be accurate at the facility and incomplete for the workload.

Decision: report site withdrawal, site consumption, and source-energy water separately; do not add unlike boundaries.

Dry is not automatically efficient. Microsoft’s zero-water evaporation design avoids more than 125 million liters per facility per year against its stated FY24 baseline, but the company also says energy use rises nominally. Optimize power and water together, in the actual climate.

Cost and time: capacity is a supply chain

Facility capex is not total cluster cost

Turner & Townsend’s 2025 traditional build benchmarks span $9.5/W in Charlotte to $13.3/W in Silicon Valley. A 100 MW facility therefore implies about $950M–$1.33B in comparable construction scope; high-density liquid cooling adds a reported 7–10% U.S. premium.

Excluded: land, utility off-site work, finance, owner costs outside methodology, and the accelerators/servers themselves. A vendor quote that combines these categories cannot be compared directly.

“Power-on” has multiple clocks

IEA says a data center can be operational in two to three years, while the broader energy system needs longer. ERCOT’s 2026 process studies ≥75 MW large loads in batches and was tracking 438 GW of requests; that queue is requests, not forecast realized demand.

PJM says its reformed generation queue targets one-to-two-year agreements, but large-load service also depends on utility studies, substations, transmission, transformers, permits, and construction. Do not substitute a generation-queue statistic for a load energization promise.

Edge and advanced checks

Behind-the-meter generation: MW is not enough

A 100 MW continuous campus consumes 2.4 GWh/day. A 100 MW generator nameplate cannot cover that load if it is intermittent, derated by temperature/altitude, down for maintenance, or constrained by fuel. Specify dependable capacity, ramp, startup, minimum load, storage duration, black-start behavior, emissions permits, and N+1 contingencies.

required generator nameplate ≈ continuous load ÷ capacity factor — before reserves

Grid queues: separate load, generation, and transmission

They are related but different processes. ERCOT’s Batch Zero is for large users ≥75 MW; PJM’s quoted one-to-two-year agreement target is for generation interconnection. Ask the serving utility/ISO for the actual study path, milestones, upgrade scope, and energization conditions for the site.

Training and inference are not interchangeable

Training tends toward long, synchronized runs with high accelerator occupancy and network intensity. Inference can be latency-sensitive, bursty, geographically distributed, and provisioned for peak demand. LBNL’s model uses 80% operational time for training and 40% for inference, but a real fleet must be measured at server, rack, and service boundaries.

Flexible load has operational value—and constraints

Checkpointing, workload migration, power capping, and staged commissioning can lower coincident demand or accelerate partial energization. Quantify how many MW can move, for how long, with what notice, and at what recovery cost. “Interruptible” without a tested workload contract is not firm grid flexibility.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  1. GPU TDP = facility demand.
    It omits CPUs, memory, storage, network, power conversion, and cooling.
  2. Nameplate = annual draw.
    Nameplate is for safe provisioning; meter data and operating profiles determine energy.
  3. PUE = compute efficiency.
    PUE can improve while useful work per kWh gets worse.
  4. Cross-climate PUE league tables.
    Weather, redundancy, load, boundary, and water tradeoffs differ.
  5. Average rack density sizes cooling.
    The hottest rack and row drive local delivery and return capacity.
  1. Training = inference.
    Their utilization, latency, redundancy, network, and geographic profiles differ.
  2. Peak vendor spec = delivered performance.
    TDP and peak FLOPS do not reveal sustained workload throughput.
  3. Closed-loop liquid = zero water.
    The facility heat-rejection system and power source determine consumption.
  4. Queue request = committed load.
    Requests can be speculative, duplicated, phased, or unable to secure upgrades.
  5. Cost per MW = all-in cluster cost.
    Confirm whether land, utility work, IT, finance, and owner costs are inside the boundary.
