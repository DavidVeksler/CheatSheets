02 // BUS

Bus, structure, and the launcher constraint

FLAT

Stackable bus

A thin spacecraft uses fairing volume efficiently: Falcon 9 can carry a dense, dispenser-free stack. The stack is clamped during ascent, then released so each vehicle separates before deploying its array.

−22%

Optimized mass

SpaceX lists V2 Mini Optimized at about 575 kg, nearly 22% lighter than the original V2 Mini. The gain buys up to 29 satellites per Falcon 9 instead of 23—six more capacity units per mission.

20×

Why V3 waits for Starship

SpaceX projects up to 60 V3 satellites and roughly 61 Tbps downlink capacity per Starship flight, more than 20× the per-launch capacity of a current Falcon 9 batch. This is a launch-system dependency, not merely a bigger panel.

4 RW

Attitude control

V2 uses four reaction wheels in a hot-spare architecture. They point the RF beams, align solar arrays, manage low-drag flight attitudes, and preserve control deep into targeted reentry.

STAR

Navigation cameras

“Star Trekker” cameras recognize star fields to estimate attitude. Combined with onboard position and velocity, that state estimate supports beam placement, formation spacing, and autonomous maneuver planning.

N/D

What remains private

SpaceX does not publish a V3 mechanical drawing, exact envelope, dry/wet mass split, processor count, thermal design, battery chemistry, or radiation-hardness figures. Treat detailed internet cutaways as conjecture.

03 // RF PAYLOAD

Phased arrays, gateways, and frequency roles

User links and backhaul are different paths. Electronically steered Ku beams serve many moving footprint cells; larger-capacity Ka/E links connect satellites to gateway sites. Optical ISLs move traffic between satellites but do not talk to the customer dish.

Digital beamforming: what actually changes?
A phased array changes the phase and amplitude applied to many antenna elements so the combined wavefront points electronically, without rotating a dish. SpaceX says V3 moves to next-generation computers, modems, beamforming, and switching; it has not published element count, converter resolution, beam count, or whether every stage is fully digital.
Direct to Cell is a sibling system, not a universal V2 feature
SpaceX’s 2026 prospectus explicitly distinguishes broadband and mobile satellite constellations even though both use “V” names. V1 Mobile provides light data, text, and over-the-top voice; V2 Mobile is a future Starship-deployed platform expected in 2027. Do not read “V2” and assume one shared spacecraft spec.

24,000+

Mesh scale

SpaceX reported more than 24,000 lasers in the constellation in its 2025 report. That is a fleet count, not 24,000 simultaneous end-to-end routes.

25+ Gbps

Mini Laser demo

Two third-party-bus Mini Laser demonstration units transferred more than 25 Gbps to the existing mesh in summer 2025, proving a smaller hosted-terminal path.

Δ route

Why the mesh matters

A satellite over an ocean can relay across moving neighbors until one sees a gateway. The scheduler must continuously reacquire links and route around weather, outages, geometry, and congestion.

05 // PROPULSION

Hall thrusters: krypton to argon

A Hall thruster ionizes stored gas and accelerates the ions electrostatically. It produces tiny force for long periods: excellent for orbit raising, spacing, conjunction avoidance, stationkeeping, and disposal—useless for launch from Earth.

170 mN

V2 Mini thrust

About the weight-force of a 17 g object on Earth. SpaceX reports 2.4× first-generation thrust and 1.5× its specific impulse; its 2023 release listed 2,500 s Isp.

DAILY

Stationkeeping

Starlink’s 2026 space-safety documentation says satellites use daily stationkeeping burns to constrain radial offset and along-track spacing. Avoidance burns are additional, conjunction-driven events.

AUTO

Collision avoidance

Onboard state and SpaceX’s screening system support autonomous planning. “Autonomous” does not mean uncoordinated: operators exchange ephemerides and SpaceX still applies maneuver policies and health gates.

06 // POWER

Solar arrays, batteries, eclipse

2 WINGS

Aero-neutral V2 layout

SpaceX says V2 uses dual solar arrays arranged to improve on-orbit maneuvering and control. In very low orbit, even a deployed solar wing sees enough residual atmosphere for drag attitude to matter.

BATTERY

Eclipse operation

High-capacity batteries run the bus and payload when Earth blocks the Sun. Charge state, thermal limits, and payload demand must be scheduled across repeated roughly 90-minute LEO orbits; exact capacity is private.

N/D kW

No trustworthy V3 power figure

SpaceX has not published a V3 solar-array area, nameplate output, or battery capacity. The often-repeated 10–12 kW range is an enthusiast estimate and is intentionally omitted.

07 // DEPLOY + LIFE

From launch stack to targeted reentry

Release

Falcon 9 releases a tensioned flat stack; the satellites drift apart. Starship tests have demonstrated a side-mounted “Pez” slot with simulator satellites, but operational V3 deployment has not occurred.

Checkout + raise

Vehicles inserted low must pass health checks and begin orbit raising within days. Multiple temporary waypoints keep the transfer flow separated from crewed stations and settled shells.

Serve + maneuver

At the assigned shell, daily burns maintain spacing while RF beams and optical crosslinks continuously hand off. SpaceX’s current broadband shell plan spans 450–490 km; V3 is planned lower.

Deorbit + demise

Health metrics trigger retirement. A controllable satellite lowers itself and targets open ocean; attitude control continues to about 125 km. Demisable parts are the last risk-control layer.

5+ yr

Designed service life

Starlink’s current space-safety documentation says satellites are designed to serve for more than five years. Individual retirement depends on health, capacity economics, shell changes, and conjunction risk.

<3 J

Component impact target

The 2025 progress report says Starlink targets less than 3 joules at component level, versus the U.S. debris-mitigation reference of 15 J. This is a design-analysis target, not a guarantee every atom vanishes.

330–370

V3 altitude band

SpaceX’s July 2026 public space-safety plan places V3 broadband between 330 and 370 km by end-2026, with detailed shell geometry still subject to change.

08 // ANTI-PATTERNS

Common misconceptions

MYTH // “V3 is just a bigger V2 Mini.”

CORRECTION // SpaceX identifies next-generation computers, modems, beamforming, and switching, plus an order-of-magnitude capacity jump and lower target orbit. The mechanical details remain private.

MYTH // “1 Tbps means my dish gets 1 Tbps.”

CORRECTION // Satellite capacity is shared across beams, gateways, users, routing overhead, spectrum, and time. Terminal capability, service plan, congestion, visibility, and weather constrain an individual session.

MYTH // “Laser links connect customers directly.”

CORRECTION // The user terminal uses RF. Lasers connect spacecraft to spacecraft, relaying aggregated traffic until it can reach a satellite with a usable ground path.

MYTH // “Dish Gen 3 equals satellite V3.”

CORRECTION // Customer-terminal generations, broadband spacecraft generations, and mobile/DTC spacecraft families are separate product lines with overlapping “V” or “Gen” labels.

MYTH // “The FCC filing is deployed hardware.”

CORRECTION // An application describes a requested operating envelope. It can be deferred, modified, partially granted, or never built. SpaceX design targets are not measured orbital performance.

MYTH // “All V2 Minis carry Direct to Cell.”

CORRECTION // DTC payloads are mission-specific mobile satellites. SpaceX’s own prospectus distinguishes V1/V2 Mobile from V2 Mini and V3 broadband spacecraft.
