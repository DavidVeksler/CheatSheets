The Power Chain: Grid to GPU

A watt does not jump from a power plant into an accelerator. It crosses planning studies, protection zones, voltage transformations, energy storage, conductors, and power electronics—each with a different job and failure mode.

01 / Fundamentals

A customer the grid can feel

A hyperscale campus is not merely a large building. Its size, power-electronic controls, rapid ramps, and ride-through behavior can affect transmission planning and bulk-system operations.

Flat energy, sharp edges

Many data centers run near-continuously, but AI training can switch between compute and checkpoint phases quickly.

Observed case: NERC shows a North American facility falling from about 450 MW to 40 MW in 36 seconds, then returning later over minutes (July 2025 report).

Power-electronic load

UPS rectifiers, server power supplies, variable-speed cooling drives, and GPU regulators do not behave like passive resistance.

Why it matters: models need harmonics, controls, protection settings, reactive demand, and voltage ride-through—not only peak MW.

Power quality sensitivity

A brief voltage sag may leave utility breakers closed while customer protection transfers or sheds compute.

2024 incident: a 230 kV fault sequence coincided with roughly 1,500 MW of customer-side load reduction in the Eastern Interconnection.

Capacity is layered

Utility service MW, total facility demand, critical IT MW, installed nameplate, and usable redundant capacity are different numbers.

Example: 120 MW at the meter with PUE 1.20 corresponds to 100 MW of IT—not a 120 MW GPU hall.

Reliability is end-to-end

A duplicate UPS does not create a duplicate path if both feeds share one transformer, switchboard, fuel pump, or rack transfer device.

Test: trace each A/B path on the one-line and remove one component at a time; the weakest shared point sets the outcome.

Every lost watt becomes heat

Transformer, UPS, conductor, and power-supply losses add electrical demand and cooling demand.

Example: a 95%-efficient UPS delivering 100 MW dissipates about 5.26 MW before the downstream cooling penalty.
“Interconnection queue” needs a qualifier. A data center submits a load service/interconnection request through its utility, transmission owner, and sometimes an ISO/RTO process. Berkeley Lab’s widely quoted generator queue statistics concern power plants and storage seeking to inject energy, not data centers seeking to consume it. The two can constrain the same project, but they are not one queue.

03 / Working knowledge

Size from the load outward

Start with the compute profile, decide what must ride through, add distribution losses and cooling, then test each normal and contingency state. Starting with campus nameplate reverses the logic.

Worked example

100 MW of IT at PUE 1.20

This is a transparent arithmetic model, not a “typical campus.” PUE is an energy ratio; using it with constant power assumes the same load for the full interval.

Facility power = IT power × PUE
100 MW × 1.20 = 120 MW Annual energy = 120 MW × 8,760 h
= 1,051,200 MWh/year

UPS segment sensitivity

If that entire 100 MW IT segment is behind a 95%-efficient UPS, the UPS needs 105.26 MW input and rejects 5.26 MW as loss. At 97%, input falls to 103.09 MW; the 2.17 MW difference equals about 19,010 MWh/year at continuous full output. Do not add this again if the measured PUE already includes it.

Double-conversion UPS

Use when continuous conditioning and a defined AC–DC–AC ride-through path justify conversion loss.

  • DOE: 95%+ efficiency was available in 2023 for large systems.
  • Eco modes can approach 99% but expose a different disturbance/transfer path.
  • Gotcha: 2N modules lightly loaded may operate below their best efficiency point.

Battery vs. flywheel

Both bridge an interruption; duration, cycling, footprint, maintenance, and fault behavior drive the choice.

  • Batteries provide electrochemical stored energy and can support longer bridges.
  • Flywheels provide short electromechanical ride-through with high cycle capability.
  • Gotcha: neither replaces a tested generator-start and transfer sequence.

208 V vs. 415/240 or 480 V

Keep voltage higher longer when equipment supports it; the same power at higher voltage requires less current.

  • Legacy 208/120 V designs often add a floor PDU transformer.
  • 415/240 V can feed 240 V server PSUs phase-to-neutral without that 208/120 V step.
  • 480 V distribution is common upstream but must match listed downstream equipment.

Generator ≠ UPS

Engine generators sustain the site through a long utility outage; the UPS bridges the start/transfer gap and conditions sensitive load.

  • Generator controls must accept the facility’s block loading and harmonics.
  • Fuel storage and delivery paths belong in the redundancy analysis.
  • Gotcha: emergency-engine permits are not blanket permission for routine generation.

Load interconnection

The serving utility/transmission owner studies whether the new demand violates thermal, voltage, stability, short-circuit, or reliability limits.

  • ERCOT’s process applies at 75 MW or more at one site (as of June 2026).
  • ERCOT reported tracking 438,000+ MW of large-load requests, 89% attributed to data centers; requests are not built load.
  • Energization may be staged below ultimate campus nameplate.

Generator interconnection

Onsite supply that operates in parallel—or a remote plant built for the campus—may need a separate generation study and agreement.

  • Berkeley Lab’s 2026 queue update: median request-to-IA exceeded 3 years; request-to-operation exceeded 5 years for reporting regions.
  • Those figures are for generators, not load requests.
  • Withdrawal rates make raw queue MW a poor build forecast.

AI transient behavior

Training transitions can move load faster than conventional scheduling intervals, so the grid needs ramp rate, protection, and ride-through models.

  • NERC documented a 50 MW block ramping at 1.9 per unit/second for about 250 ms.
  • The July 10, 2024 Eastern event shed about 1,500 MW customer-side and raised frequency to 60.053 Hz before recovery.
  • Coordinate restoration; a simultaneous restart is another step change.

Large-load tariff

A tariff converts engineering risk into contract terms: minimum billing demand, ramp/curtailment provisions, collateral, dedicated-facility cost, and service duration.

  • Ask who pays if forecast load arrives late or never.
  • Separate energy ($/MWh), demand ($/kW-month), and one-time upgrade obligations.
  • Do not infer firm service from an interconnection study or a public project announcement.
06 / Anti-patterns

Six category errors

Most bad explanations mix a time scale, a boundary, or a reliability term. Correct the category and the architecture becomes easier to reason about.

“Generators are the UPS.”A UPS bridges and conditions on millisecond-to-minute scales; generators sustain the load after starting and transferring. Test the handoff as one system.
“The queue is five years.”Which queue? Berkeley Lab’s timeline is for generator projects. Load studies vary by utility/ISO, voltage, size, upgrades, readiness, and jurisdiction.
“A 1 GW campus consumes 1 GW.”Nameplate or ultimate master-plan capacity is not interval demand. Ask for energized capacity, meter peak, IT critical load, PUE boundary, and schedule.
“2N means Tier IV.”2N is a capacity/path shorthand. Uptime Tier IV is a tested fault-tolerance outcome across every relevant subsystem; shared downstream points can defeat it.
“Behind the meter means off grid.”Parallel generation still needs protection and operating agreements; islanding needs controls, black start, stable fuel, and a complete facility balance.
“Interconnection approval delivers power.”An agreement can precede substation, transformer, line, generation, permit, easement, and construction completion. Track physical milestones separately.
