# Spec: Prepper Gear vs. Reality — Audit Your Preps by Probability

**Target file:** `prepper-gear-audit.html`

**Why this topic:** The preparedness industry is fear-funded (Pattern B): it sells $700 chest rigs and $300 freeze-dried buckets for cinematic scenarios while the actual emergency distribution — multi-day power outages, winter storms, wildfire/hurricane evacuations, job loss — is won by boring items and free skills. The community's own folklore admits this ("you bug out to a Hampton Inn, not the woods") but no gear-adjacent site can publish it. The site already owns the serious end of this cluster (nuclear, radio, celestial); this page is the front door that calibrates spending before the reader ever needs those.

## Content approach

Organizing device: **the probability ladder.** Every prep is judged against the ranked list of what actually happens to households, and the page repeatedly prices gear in "per realistic-emergency-averted" terms. Second device: **skills-per-dollar** — free/cheap competence repeatedly embarrasses purchases. Tone: not anti-prepping — pro-prepping, anti-cosplay; the FEMA baseline is the floor the page builds from, and the existing nuclear page is respectfully framed as the tail-risk annex.

Sections:

1. **Quick reference: the probability ladder.** The table that reframes everything — emergency scenarios ranked by realistic household frequency, each with "what it actually requires": multi-day power outage (the modal event; annual odds meaningful everywhere) / winter storm or heat wave / evacuation with 2–48 hrs notice (wildfire, hurricane, flood, chemical) / water advisory / **job loss or medical income shock (the emergency people prep least for and face most)** / regional disaster with 1–2 week service loss (earthquake, major hurricane) / grid-down-months fantasy scenarios (the catalog's favorite; the honest note on why prepping for #1–6 covers your plausible exposure to #7 anyway). Columns: scenario, realistic odds tier, duration, what wins it, what the catalog sells for it. Density: 8–10 rows.
2. **The audit table — catalog vs. reality.** The page's spine; density: 20–25 gear rows, columns: item, catalog price, verdict (BUY / BUY CHEAP / SKILLS FIRST / COSPLAY), what the emergency actually calls for, real-alternative cost:
   - **Water (the #1 real prep):** stored water (1 gal/person/day × 2 weeks; the $0 versions: cleaned 2-liter bottles, bathtub liner at warning), a gravity/squeeze filter (Sawyer-class, ~$25–40 — the highest-value object in the genre), purification tablets, and the COSPLAY row: $400 "tactical" filtration systems doing what the $30 one does.
   - **Food:** pantry-deepening beats buckets — the cost/calorie table: rice, beans, oats, canned goods, peanut butter, oil at ~$0.50–1.50 per 1,000 kcal vs. freeze-dried buckets at $8–15 per 1,000 kcal (5–10× markup for shelf life you don't need at the 2-week horizon); rotation ("store what you eat"); the honest freeze-dried niche (evacuation weight, true long-horizon); don't forget the manual can opener (the classic).
   - **Power:** the watt-budget worksheet (fridge ~600–800 W surge, phone charging trivial, CPAP the stealth priority, what you're NOT running) → generator sizing honestly (a 2–4 kW inverter unit covers the realistic list; the whole-house 12 kW is a comfort purchase); **the CO section in dead earnest — portable generators kill ~70–85 Americans/yr via CO, more than many disasters they're bought for: outside only, 20+ ft from openings, CO alarm mandatory**; transfer switch vs. suicide-cord warning; power stations (LiFePO4) vs. generators decision table (apartment/noise/indoor-safe vs. $/Wh and refuel-ability); the $20 answers: power banks, car-inverter, headlamps-not-flashlights.
   - **Comms:** the radio-cluster router — NOAA weather radio (BUY, $30), Baofeng + the license point (SKILLS FIRST: the radio is $25, the *license and practice* make it real — link ham-radio-technician), the folklore that texts beat calls on congested networks, offline maps downloaded today, the family out-of-state check-in contact (free, rehearsed).
   - **Medical:** boring kit beats tactical kit — the actual list (meds! a 2-week prescription buffer is the highest-value medical prep and costs negotiation not money; OTC basics, wound care) vs. the IFAK fetish (tourniquets save lives WITH training — Stop the Bleed is free and 2 hours; a CAT on an untrained belt is COSPLAY) — cross-link er-triage for the when-to-go layer.
   - **The cosplay shelf, named kindly:** plate carriers/chest rigs (you are evacuating, not clearing rooms), $300 "tactical" flashlights, gas masks (wrong threat, wrong fit, wrong filters), 60-lb "battle belts," katana-adjacent purchases. One honest line each on the rare legitimate contexts, then the verdict.
   - **The unsexy BUYs that outrank all of it:** CO + smoke alarms with fresh batteries, fire extinguisher (ABC, mounted, and the 10-second training video), water-heater seismic strapping in quake zones, a real first-aid class, cash in small bills ($200–500; card networks fail locally more often than society does), the document go-folder (copies of IDs/insurance/deeds + the estate-documents cross-link), and **insurance adequacy as the tail-risk prep nobody counts** (cross-link insurance-worth-it: correct homeowners/flood coverage transfers the six-figure tail).
3. **The 72-hour evacuation bag — done honestly.** The realistic scenario framing (hotel/relative/shelter, car-based, 2–48 hr notice), then the checklist with **real weights and real costs** per item, target: under ~20 lbs/adult — documents copies, meds, chargers + bank, cash, 3 days clothes, toiletries, kids/pets lines, the comfort item folklore (morale is a supply). The COSPLAY contrast: the 60-lb woods-survival ruck audited in one paragraph (weight listed, scenario named, verdict). Kids note: a small pack each, age-appropriate. Car kit as the bag's little sibling (blanket, water, scraper, jumper cables/jump box, the winter add-ons).
4. **The two-week home kit.** The shelter-in-place checklist organized by system (water / food / power+light / heat+cool / sanitation / meds / comms / cash+documents), quantities per person, ~costed at both "mostly free" (containers you own, pantry-deepening) and "buy it all" (~$300–600/household anchor) tiers. The sanitation block folklore (the bucket protocol gets one line + the nuclear-page link where it's covered in full). Storage reality for apartments (under-bed water, the closet shelf audit).
5. **Skills-per-dollar — the table that inverts the catalog.** The page's signature inversion; 10–12 rows: Stop the Bleed (free–$20, 2 hrs) / CPR-AED (~$40–80) / ham Technician (~$15 exam + free study — link) / CERT membership (free, and it comes with gear) / basic car maintenance (link auto page when it ships) / home fire drill + extinguisher practice (free) / swimming competence / gardening-preserving (honest: hobby-tier for emergencies, real for resilience culture) / physical fitness (the prep that improves every scenario including the 100%-probability ones — link strength-training/running pages) — columns: cost, hours, scenarios it wins, the gear it replaces. The line that frames it: *in offender interviews the burglar fears the dog; in disaster after-action reports the survivor asset is the neighbor* — neighborhood ties as the final "skill" row (free, highest returns, uncatalogable).
6. **Common mistakes:** buying for the fantasy tail while the CO alarm has no battery, food you've never cooked and won't rotate, the generator purchased after the storm starts running in the garage, all-your-preps-in-one-place (basement flood takes the flood kit), gear never unboxed until the event (the shakedown-weekend rule: run the house on the kit for 48 hours once), prescription buffer ignored because gear is easier to buy than doctors are to call, prepping alone while never meeting the neighbors, and letting the hobby's aesthetics (matching pouches) substitute for the boring audit this page is.

Research: ready.gov/FEMA and Red Cross baselines (primary), CPSC generator CO death data (primary, dated), power-outage frequency data (DOE/EIA reliability reports), USDA/grocery staples pricing for the calorie table (dated), Stop the Bleed/CERT/ARRL program facts. Per Rule 1 all figures are anchors — the calorie-cost and CO tables especially must be rebuilt from current sources.

## Visual design

**Packing-grid aesthetic** — the flat-lay loadout photo culture of the prepper world, redrawn as an honest audit: every item a labeled tile with weight and cost tags, arranged on a canvas grid.

- Palette: field tones — olive/coyote tan canvas background texture (subtle CSS, no images) with near-black ink — but the verdict accent is **blaze/safety orange reserved exclusively for the boring-real items** (the CO alarm, the Sawyer filter, the meds), inverting the tacticool color code: on this page, high-visibility = high-value, and the cosplay items sit in desaturated gray-green. That inversion IS the design thesis.
- **Shareable artifact & og:image: the two-loadouts graphic** — side-by-side flat-lay grids drawn as simple SVG line-art tiles: "THE CATALOG LOADOUT" (weight and price totals in red, scenario tag: "unclear") vs. "THE KIT THAT WORKS" (totals in orange/black, scenario tags: "outage / evacuation / storm"). Built to crop for 1200×630.
- **Item tiles as the repeating unit:** every gear entry rendered as a gear-tile card — item silhouette line-drawing or plain label, oz/lb tag top-right, $ tag bottom-right, verdict chip (BUY / BUY CHEAP / SKILLS FIRST / COSPLAY) as a stenciled tag. Stencil-style condensed caps for headers (military-crate lettering, used cleanly rather than as pastiche).
- The probability ladder as a horizontal frequency bar chart — scenario bars scaled by odds tier, the catalog's favorite scenario visibly the shortest bar.
- The watt-budget worksheet as a fill-in panel (appliance rows → running/surge watts → total → the generator/power-station size line) echoing the site's worksheet device family.
- **Checklists as gear manifests:** bordered manifest cards with real checkboxes (localStorage), monospace quantities column, print stylesheet producing a clean one-page manifest each (72-hr bag, two-week kit).
- The CO warning as the page's one hard-stop element: black-on-orange hazard band, no decoration competing with it.
- Skills table styled as a course-catalog card rack — deliberately un-tactical (plain paper cards) amid the canvas, reinforcing the inversion.
