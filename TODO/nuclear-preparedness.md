## Content approach

Editorial spine: **fallout is a solvable physics problem, and fatalism is the deadliest myth.** Outside the immediate blast radius — which for most people in most scenarios is where they are — survival is overwhelmingly determined by what you do in the first hour and where you spend the next two weeks. Kearny proved ordinary families can build adequate protection in 48 hours with hand tools.

Sections:

1. **Threat model, honestly.** What a detonation actually does by radius (flash/burns, blast, thermal, EMP footnote) for a representative 100 kt–1 MT range — with the key point that blast zones are small and fallout zones are large but survivable. Kill the myths explicitly, each in one line with the correction (the three below are the floor; 5–8 myths): "nuclear winter means survival is pointless" (contested magnitude, irrelevant to your first two weeks), "if you see the flash you're dead" (opposite — duck-and-cover works; cite Hiroshima survivor data on flying glass), "radiation lingers for centuries everywhere" (that's contamination sites, not fallout decay). Scenario table: single terrorist device / limited exchange / large exchange — what changes in your response (almost nothing in the first 48 h).
2. **Quick reference: the first hour.** Decision flowchart in text form: saw a flash → drop, cover, count to 60 (blast wave travel time), then you have **~15–60+ minutes before fallout arrives** (downwind distance table, 6–8 rows) → get inside the best building available, go to center/basement, seal, tune radio. The CDC triplet — *Get inside. Stay inside. Stay tuned.* — as the memorizable core.
3. **The 7-10 rule and the two-week clock.** Fallout decay: every 7× increase in time = 1/10th the dose rate. Table: H+1 hr = 1,000 R/hr reference → H+7 hrs = 100 → H+49 hrs = 10 → H+2 weeks ≈ 1. The operational meaning: the first 3 days are everything, two weeks of sheltering covers the vast majority of the hazard. Dose-effect table (6–8 dose bands: roentgen/gray → symptoms → prognosis) so readers understand *why* the math matters, including the crucial fact that dose is cumulative and rate-dependent.
4. **Protection factor math.** PF = outside dose ÷ inside dose. Halving thickness table for gamma (6–8 materials): ~1″ steel / 2.4″ concrete / 3.6″ packed earth / 7″ books or water. Building PF table (6–8 building types): frame house PF 2–3, basement PF 10–20, basement corner + improvised mass PF 40+, subway/deep shelter PF 100+. Worked example: turning a basement corner into PF 40 with tables, doors, and 12″ of books/earth-filled drawers — with the actual Kearny trial timings.
5. **Expedient shelters (the Kearny designs).** The car-over-trench, door-covered trench, and above-ground earth-rolled shelters — each with dimensions, tools, person-hours from the ORNL trials, and PF achieved. Ventilation: why sealed holes suffocate before radiation matters, and the **Kearny Air Pump** (KAP) concept in brief. Frame honestly: these matter if you're rural/warned; urban readers should focus on section 4.
6. **The Kearny Fallout Meter.** A measurable-accuracy dosimeter built from a can, foil, and thread — the emblematic artifact of the book. Cover: what it measures, claimed accuracy (±25%), and where the free plans live (public-domain PDF). Modern alternatives: cheap GM counters (GQ GMC-series class), NukAlert-type chirpers, and their real limitations at high dose rates (saturation).
7. **Life support for 14 days.** Water math (1 gal/person/day minimum; water heater ~40 gal, toilet tank, waterbed caveats; purification vs. fallout — dissolved vs. particulate, why filtering + settling works), food (fallout doesn't penetrate cans; peeling/washing rules), sanitation (the bucket protocol — Kearny's trials found this the #1 morale failure), KI dosing table (5 age bands per FDA; adult 130 mg, child doses; **thyroid-only, timing-critical, not a radiation cure** — say this loudly), meds, light (candle oxygen math).
8. **Communications & modern updates.** Ties to the site's radio pages: NOAA weather radio, AM broadcast as the survivor (EMP notes kept brief and non-hysterical), Baofeng monitoring frequencies, why cell networks fail-then-recover. What's changed since 1979: no more CONELRAD/civil defense network (FEMA IPAWS/WEA instead), NUKEMAP for preparedness visualization, current shelter-in-place doctrine (FEMA "Planning Guidance for Response to a Nuclear Detonation," 3rd ed.) — which *validates* Kearny's shelter-first logic against the evacuate-instinct.
9. **Common mistakes:** fleeing by car during fallout arrival (highest-dose choice available), sheltering upstairs for comfort, coming out on day 2 because the radio went quiet, taking KI as a general radioprotectant, sealing a shelter airtight, storing all water in one breakable container, buying gear instead of reading for the same two hours.

Research: NWSS 1987 edition (public domain, ORNL — cite chapters), FEMA nuclear detonation response guidance (current ed.), CDC radiation emergency pages, REMM dose tables. Kearny's numbers are experimentally grounded — keep his PF/timing figures and label the vintage; use FEMA/REMM for anything medical.

## Visual design

**Modernized civil-defense aesthetic** — the CD visual language (the yellow-black trefoil era) reinterpreted cleanly rather than as pastiche.

- Palette: warning-yellow, near-black, off-white, one alert red — used sparingly on a light background so the page reads as calm competence, not doom porn. Angular section headers with a thin yellow-black hazard rule.
- **Hero: the 7-10 rule as a large log-log decay curve** (inline SVG) with the four milestone callouts (1 h / 7 h / 2 d / 2 wk) — the single most shareable graphic on the page. **og:image / shareable artifact:** this curve, rendered as the 1200×630 social card, is also the "screenshot this" block; the printable shelter card is the *print* artifact, not the share card.
- **First-hour flowchart** as a bold vertical decision strip — big numbered steps, monospace timing annotations ("T+0:60 blast wave", "T+15:00 earliest fallout").
- **Halving-thickness table as a materials bar chart:** horizontal bars whose lengths are literal thicknesses, drawn to scale against a ruler axis — steel short, books long; instantly intuitive.
- Shelter designs as labeled cutaway line diagrams (SVG, blueprint-style single color) with a spec plate per design: PF / person-hours / tools.
- Dose and PF numbers in monospace with unit badges. A "Kearny said / FEMA now says" two-column callout style for the 1979-vs-today updates.
- Print stylesheet mandatory — like the celestial page, this is a genuinely-print-me artifact; consider a condensed printable "shelter card" section at the end echoing `emergency-radio-card.html`.
