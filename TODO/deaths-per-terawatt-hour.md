# Spec: Deaths per Terawatt-Hour — Every Energy Source, Honestly Ranked

**Target file:** `deaths-per-terawatt-hour.html`

**Why this topic:** The single most inverted risk perception in public life: nuclear power ranks at or near the *bottom* of deaths per unit of energy — alongside wind and solar, orders of magnitude below coal — yet dread risk puts it at the top of public fear. The correcting data (Markandya & Wilkinson's Lancet work, Our World in Data's synthesis, UNSCEAR/WHO accident assessments) is scattered across epidemiology papers no lay reader opens, and most writers touching it have an energy-tribe flag to fly. A no-affiliation page that shows the table, explains the mechanism (air pollution kills continuously and invisibly; accidents kill rarely and cinematically), and handles Chernobyl/Fukushima honestly is pure base-rate-correction leverage — and it completes the site's engineering cluster (data-center-myths is the same genre: numbers vs. vibes).

## Content approach

The page is one argument executed with discipline: **show the table, explain the inversion, stress-test it against its hardest objections.** Credibility strategy stated to the builder: this page must be scrupulously non-tribal — it is not "pro-nuclear content"; it is mortality accounting. That means the error bars are content, the worst-case-for-the-thesis numbers get shown (use the high-end Chernobyl estimates in the sensitivity check, not just the low ones), and hydro's Banqiao skeleton gets equal spotlight with nuclear's accidents. A reader from any energy tribe should leave unable to accuse the page of cooking a single number.

Sections:

1. **Quick reference: the ranking table + chart.** The headline log-scale figure and its table: coal (~25 deaths/TWh global, with the regional range), oil (~18), biomass (~4–5), gas (~3), hydro (~1.3 including Banqiao, ~0.02–0.04 without — both shown, the two-row honesty), wind (~0.04), nuclear (~0.03–0.07 depending on Chernobyl accounting — the range shown), solar (~0.02). Columns: deaths/TWh, dominant death mechanism (air pollution / accidents / falls), the source per row, and the "years of one large city's electricity per statistical death" translation column (the intuition-builder: one death per N city-years). All figures Rule-1 anchors — rebuild from OWID/primary sources at build time, show ranges where sources range.
2. **Why intuition inverts — the mechanism section.** Three short blocks: (a) **air pollution is the invisible bulk** — PM2.5 mortality attribution explained in plain terms (how epidemiology counts deaths nobody's death certificate names; the GBD method in one honest paragraph including its uncertainty), the WHO-scale global numbers, and the arithmetic that a single large coal plant's routine operation exceeds most famous nuclear accidents' tolls; (b) **dread risk** — the psychology (Slovic's dread/unknown axes; invisible-contamination fears; single-event vividness vs. distributed mortality) — cross-link actual-risk-dashboard's salience section; (c) **the denominator blindness** — per-TWh vs. per-incident thinking, with the aviation-vs-driving analogy the site's readers already know.
3. **The accidents, honestly — one block each.** The section that earns the page its trust:
   - **Chernobyl:** the confirmed acute toll (28 ARS deaths + 2 blast; ~15 fatal thyroid cancers among the documented), the UNSCEAR position, the modeled long-term ranges (WHO ~4,000-class projections vs. higher LNT-derived estimates — explain *why* estimates diverge: linear-no-threshold extrapolation over huge populations) — and the sensitivity line: even at the high-end modeled numbers, the per-TWh figure moves but the *ranking* doesn't (show that math explicitly; it's the page's strongest move).
   - **Fukushima:** the two-part answer — radiation deaths (0–1 attributed, the 2018 lung-cancer compensation case noted honestly) vs. **evacuation-related deaths (~2,000+ among elderly evacuees: stress, interrupted care)** — and the uncomfortable lesson that the *response* killed more than the release, which is a risk-communication finding, not a nuclear talking point; link nuclear-preparedness's evacuate-vs-shelter logic.
   - **Banqiao (1975):** the hydro skeleton — dam failure killing tens of thousands (the range and why records are murky), why hydro's per-TWh figure is a tale of two rows, and the general lesson: tail risk concentrates in *storage* (dams, tailings) more than generation.
   - **The fossil ledger:** no single disaster to name because the deaths are ambient — plus the acute ones people forget (coal mining deaths annually, gas explosions, oil-train/refinery events) with the per-year scale that dwarfs the famous accidents quietly.
   - **Wind/solar's small print:** installation falls and rooftop work dominate — construction-safety deaths, honestly counted, which is why solar isn't zero.
4. **The objections section — steel-manned.** Each objection stated at full strength, then answered with numbers: "the nuclear numbers exclude waste" (long-term waste mortality accounting — what the projections actually show; the honest "stored waste has killed approximately no one to date" + the deep-time uncertainty stated plainly); "LNT means all these numbers are guesses" (both directions — LNT uncertainty inflates *and* deflates depending on the source; show the ranking's robustness across assumptions); "per-TWh ignores catastrophe potential" (the tail-risk objection — engage seriously: worst-case envelopes, why modern designs change the envelope, and the honest note that the table measures the record, not the tail); "air-pollution attribution is statistical fiction" (the epidemiology's actual confidence levels; what would have to be wrong for coal to rank well — the counterfactual test); "the real comparison is system-level" (capacity factors, backup — one honest paragraph and a deliberate stop: Rule 4, this page is mortality, not grid design; link the gap in one line).
5. **Context blocks (small, disciplined):** deaths averted by nuclear historically (the Kharecha–Hansen estimate, attributed as an estimate); the "banana-equivalent" trap avoided — the page does NOT do cute dose comparisons (a stated choice: dose folklore is where these pages lose rigor; the nuclear-preparedness page owns dose tables); the trend line (all sources getting safer per TWh over decades — the good-news base rate).
6. **Common mistakes:** citing the table without the ranges (hands the debunker a free win), comparing a modern reactor to Chernobyl's design as if equivalent (one line on RBMK vs. the fleet, no more), using this page as an energy-policy argument-ender (mortality is one column of a policy decision — cost, speed, land use are others; the page says so and stops), quoting Fukushima's radiation toll without the evacuation toll (both errors of both tribes), forgetting hydro's two-row honesty in either direction, and treating air-pollution deaths as "not real deaths" because no certificate names them (the attribution method, restated once).

Research: Our World in Data's safest-sources page and its underlying sources (Markandya & Wilkinson, Lancet 2007; Sovacool et al.) — primary spine; UNSCEAR reports (Chernobyl, Fukushima); WHO assessments; GBD air-pollution attribution; Banqiao historical accounts (ranges with attribution); Kharecha & Hansen 2013. Every row's figure cited inline with year; ranges shown as ranges. Rule 1: the anchors above are from memory — the build reconstructs the table from current OWID/primary figures and keeps the error bars.

## Visual design

**Grid-schematic aesthetic** — the visual language of electrical engineering: one-line diagrams, substation symbology, and a dark control-room canvas where the data glows. Distinct from the site's other dark pages (micromort's dashboard, flight's departure board) by its schematic line-work identity.

- Palette: deep slate/near-black canvas with **electric amber** as the primary data color and a cool cyan for annotations/citations; fine schematic line-work (thin, precise, right-angled connector lines with node dots) as the decorative system. Condensed engineering sans for headers, tabular-nums throughout.
- **Signature element & og:image: the log-scale horizontal bar chart** — each energy source as a glowing amber bar on the log axis (0.01 → 100 deaths/TWh), each bar terminating in a schematic source-symbol node (cooling tower, turbine, panel, dam — simple single-line glyphs), error bars rendered as brighter whisker segments (the ranges ARE content — make them visible, not decorative). Hydro drawn as its two-row pair (with/without Banqiao) bracketed together. Values in real text at each bar end. The og:image is this chart with the title strap; it must survive a 1200×630 crop at legible size — design it at that constraint first.
- **The ranking table** beneath the chart with per-row citation chips (author-year in cyan monospace — citations as visible UI, the page's "with receipts" promise made literal).
- **Accident blocks as incident-report panels:** bordered schematic frames with a header plate (name, year, energy source glyph), the two-part tolls as separate labeled readouts (Fukushima: RADIATION 0–1 / EVACUATION ~2,000+ — the split rendered as two distinct meters, the page's most important visual honesty).
- The objections section as **test-point callouts**: each objection in a cyan "probe" tag connected by schematic line to its answer panel — the steel-man structure made visual.
- The mechanism section's air-pollution block gets the one soft visual: a wide, faint amber haze-gradient band scaled against the thin accident tick-marks — continuous vs. episodic mortality in one glance.
- Light-mode fallback (prefers-color-scheme) with the amber-on-slate inverted to slate-on-paper — the print stylesheet uses the light variant so the chart prints as a clean handout.
- 375-px: the chart rotates to vertical bars? No — horizontal bars stack naturally; labels move above bars, log axis preserved, values never truncate.
