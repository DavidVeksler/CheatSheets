# Spec: How to Survive a Hospital Stay

**Target file:** `hospital-stay-survival.html`

**Why this topic:** Medical error is among the leading causes of death in the US (estimates 100k–250k/yr), and the defenses are pure nurse folklore — transmitted between nurses, never compiled for patients because no clinician will publicly write "here's how to protect yourself from us." Every family faces this unprepared. The companion piece to er-triage.html: that page gets you to the hospital; this one gets you out intact.

(194 chars)
- **Reader outcome:** The reader can work a hospital shift as the patient's advocate — ask the four questions at every medication, re-verify the med list at every transition, and escalate rung by rung up to a family-activated rapid response team when the patient is deteriorating and nobody is reacting.

## Content approach

Voice: the experienced charge nurse who pulls your family aside and tells you how it really works. Practical, not anti-medicine — the framing is that hospitals are high-reliability systems staffed by good people, and an informed patient/advocate is a safety layer the system itself wants.

Sections:

1. **Quick reference: the advocate's one-page protocol.** The distilled card: never leave the patient alone if avoidable (especially nights/weekends), keep the running notebook (every med, every provider, every plan change), ask the four questions at every medication ("what is it, what's it for, what dose, who ordered it"), know your nurse's name each shift, be present at morning rounds, get the discharge plan in writing. Expect 8–12 protocol lines — enough to be complete, few enough to fit a wallet card. This block is the screenshot artifact (see Visual design).
2. **Who actually runs the floor.** The org chart nobody explains (8–10 roles, each with what they decide and when to talk to them): attending vs. hospitalist vs. resident/intern (and what July means in teaching hospitals), the charge nurse (the person who actually solves problems), your RN vs. techs/CNAs, case manager/discharge planner (start talking to them on day 1, not discharge day), the specialist-consult dance. Key folklore: the nurse sees you 20× more than the doctor; make the nurse your ally — and how (be specific, be brief, say thank you, don't cry wolf on the call button).
3. **The danger windows.** Where errors cluster, with the defense for each (4 windows × 2–3 concrete defenses):
   - **Shift change / handoffs** (7 AM/7 PM typically): don't schedule requests then; re-verify pending items after handoff.
   - **Nights and weekends:** thinner staffing, slower escalation — why elective procedures scheduled Friday afternoon are folklore-famous.
   - **Transitions:** ER→floor, floor→ICU, unit transfers — where med lists silently mutate. The medication reconciliation ritual: keep your own list, compare on every transition.
   - **Discharge:** the most error-dense hour of the stay.
4. **Medication safety — the big one.** ~1 error per patient per day is the classic estimate. The defenses (8–10 distinct entries): the four questions, insisting on barcode scanning before administration (it exists to be used), knowing your allergy band is accurate, the home-meds trap (bring the actual bottles or a photo list; never take your own meds unannounced), high-alert drugs to double-check (insulin, anticoagulants, opioids), sound-alike names (hydroxyzine/hydralazine as the example).
5. **Escalation: how to pull the andon cord.** The graduated ladder with scripts: ask your nurse → ask for the charge nurse (magic phrase: "I'd like the charge nurse to take a look") → **rapid response team — in most US hospitals the family can activate it directly** (this is the single least-known fact on the page; Condition H/Josie King story in one line) → patient advocate/ombudsman office → "I want a second opinion" (you can request one; how consults work). When to use each rung without burning goodwill.
6. **Preventing the classic complications.** The nurse-folklore checklist of what actually goes wrong (6–8 complications, each with its specific defense): falls (highest-volume incident — non-slip socks, call before getting up, night micro-urination trips), central line/catheter infections ("does this catheter still need to be in?" asked daily is evidence-based), pressure injuries (reposition q2h), DVT (compression devices actually worn, early walking), delirium in elders (glasses + hearing aids + daylight + familiar objects — cheap and dramatic), aspiration (HOB elevation).
7. **The paper trail and the money.** Your rights (6–8 entries): full chart access (21st Century Cures Act — OpenNotes is law; read your own notes in the portal daily), itemized bill on request, how observation-vs-inpatient status silently changes Medicare coverage (ask "am I admitted or under observation?" — the answer changes everything for SNF coverage), never pay the first bill without an itemized review, financial assistance/charity care exists at every nonprofit hospital by law.
8. **Discharge done right.** Teach-back method (repeat the plan in your own words), the med-list diff (home list vs. discharge list, line by line), red-flag symptoms in writing ("call if X"), follow-up appointments booked before you leave, the 72-hour pharmacy check, and when to refuse discharge (you can — the appeal process for Medicare patients, one paragraph).
9. **Common mistakes** (6–8 entries): being polite to the point of silence, assuming someone is coordinating (the attending sees the chart 10 min/day), letting the patient self-report history while impaired, sending the whole family home at night, treating the call button as an imposition, signing discharge papers unread.

Research: AHRQ patient safety materials, Josie King Foundation / Condition H programs, IHI, CMS observation-status rules, OpenNotes. The error statistics are contested — present the range honestly with sources, not the scariest number.

## Visual design

**Patient-chart aesthetic** — the page styled as the medical chart the family keeps, flipping the hospital's own visual language to the patient's side.

- Structure: sections as **chart tabs** (colored index tabs down the right edge, sticky-positioned as nav) — Orders / Meds / Escalation / Discharge — mimicking a paper chart binder.
- Palette: chart-manila and white, ink-navy text, with the hospital-wristband cyan as the accent; section headers styled as chart-divider labels (embossed-tape look).
- **The advocate protocol (section 1) as a laminated pocket card** design: bordered, two columns, drop-shadow suggesting a physical card — with a print button that outputs just that card at wallet/4×6 size.
- **Share card & screenshot artifact:** the pocket card is both — render it on the chart-manila background as the 1200×630 og:image, and keep the on-page version under one phone screen tall so screenshots survive cropping. (The escalation ladder is the interactive centerpiece, but the card is what families forward to each other.)
- **The escalation ladder as a literal vertical ladder graphic:** five rungs, each with its script in a speech-bubble monospace block; the rapid-response rung visually emphasized (this is the page's headline fact).
- Danger windows section on a **24-hour clock strip** — a horizontal day-band with shift-change and night hours shaded, annotations pinned to times.
- Checkbox lists throughout are real checkboxes (localStorage persistence) — families will use this live across a multi-day stay.
- Restrained, calm, high-legibility (older readers under stress): 18px+ base, strong contrast, no dark theme needed.
