# Spec: How Burglars Actually Break In (and What Actually Stops Them)

**Target file:** `how-burglars-break-in.html`

**Why this topic:** The "locks are theater" thesis, packaged as the question people actually search. The evidence base — convicted-burglar interview studies (UNC Charlotte/Blevins, Wright & Decker's *Burglars on the Job*), DOJ/BJS victimization data, insurance claims data — exists but has never been compiled into one consumer page; meanwhile the search results are alarm-company content marketing whose conclusion is always "buy monitoring." A no-affiliate page that says "the $15 strike plate beats the $300 smart lock" is the honest version that can't exist commercially.

## Content approach

Organizing principle: **think like the adversary, then spend in adversary order.** Every recommendation is ranked by what offenders say changed their behavior, not by what products cost. Recurring stat-tagging: each claim carries its source class inline — [offender interviews], [BJS], [claims data].

Sections:

1. **Quick reference: the burglar's decision math.** The mental model in one block: burglars optimize for (a) unoccupied, (b) unseen, (c) fast — the average break-in takes ~60 seconds to enter and 8–10 minutes inside. Everything that works attacks one of those three. Then the top-10 upgrade list ranked by deterrence-per-dollar, with prices: 3" strike-plate screws (~$5), door reinforcement kit (~$60–120), window pins (~$10), light timers/smart bulbs (~$30), visible dog presence, keeping the garage door down, trimming sightline shrubs ($0), long-throw deadbolt properly installed, glass-break film on sidelights, gun/valuables not in the master bedroom ($0 — the first room searched).
2. **How they actually get in — the entry-point table.** The data section (6–8 rows, entry point × share × typical method): front door ~34%, first-floor windows ~23%, back door ~22%, garage ~9%, unlocked entries ~25–30% of all burglaries (no force at all), second floor ~2%. Method reality: **kick-ins and pry bars, not lock picking** — the doorframe fails at the strike plate long before the lock cylinder matters; bumping/picking is a rounding error in offender data. The implication stated bluntly: upgrading the *lock* on a door with a ½"-screwed softwood strike plate is theater; the frame is the product.
3. **When and who.** Corrects the movie model: most residential burglaries happen **on weekdays between 10 AM and 3 PM** (occupants at work), not at night; summer peak; most burglars live nearby and case casually (knock-and-check pretexts — the "wrong address" and flyer/solicitor tells); repeat and near-repeat victimization is real (a burgled home and its neighbors face elevated risk for ~6 weeks — the one time an alarm sign upgrade is urgent). Social media/vacation signaling and the mail/package pile.
4. **What deters — straight from offender interviews.** The ranked list from the UNC Charlotte study and successors (expect 12–15 ranked items from the study battery), with the surprises flagged: dogs (any size — noise is the issue) rank at/near the top; visible cameras deter *selection* of the target but mostly displace to a neighbor; alarm signs/decals have real effect but decay (burglars know the fake-sign game); occupancy cues (car in drive, lights, TV sound) rank above hardware; deadbolts matter as *delay*, not prevention. And what ranked near zero: "beware of dog" signs without a dog, fake cameras (spotted), streetlight proximity.
5. **The hardware section — spending in the right order.** (25–35 distinct verdict-carrying entries across the five subsections.)
   - **Doors:** the strike-plate fix (long screws into the stud — the page's hero upgrade), reinforcement plates/door armor, solid-core vs. hollow, the sidelite/glazing-next-to-thumbturn problem, sliding doors (lift-and-derail attack; the dowel + anti-lift screws), garage: the emergency-release zip-tie/shield fix and never leaving the opener in a driveway car.
   - **Locks, honestly:** ANSI Grade 1/2/3 explained in two lines; pin-in-knob locks are nothing; when a better cylinder *does* matter (rentals with unknown key copies → rekey day one).
   - **Smart locks:** the honest verdict — mostly *convenience* products; attack surface trade (PIN codes end key-under-mat behavior = net win for most; battery/connectivity failure modes; the credential-sharing win for cleaners/contractors — cross-link hiring-a-contractor.html).
   - **Windows:** locks vs. pins vs. security film (what film actually does: converts smash-and-in to loud, slow work), and why bars are a fire-egress decision, not just a security one.
   - **Cameras & alarms:** what cameras are for (evidence quality settings, mounting height folklore — too high = hat-brim footage), local vs. cloud storage, and the alarm-reality paragraph: 90%+ false-alarm rates, verified-response policies in many cities mean police treat unverified alarms as low priority — the siren's value is the burglar's 8-minute clock, not the cavalry. Monitoring subscription verdict: honest BUY IF (insurance discount math included).
6. **Inside the house: the 8-minute search.** Where they go, in order: master bedroom (jewelry, cash, guns), office, living room electronics, medicine cabinet. Defenses: the decoy strategy (a $30 "sacrifice" box in the obvious spot), real storage in kid rooms/kitchen/garage (statistically last-searched), safes bolted down or they're carrying cases (cross-link `safes-what-they-stop.html`, spec in this batch), firearms: a burgled unsecured gun is the worst outcome on the page — locked, bolted, or not owned (ties to site's firearms cluster), photo inventory + serials for insurance/recovery.
7. **Renter's addendum.** What you can do without drilling the frame (5–7 entries): portable door braces/jammers, adhesive window alarms ($8, surprisingly rated by offenders as "noise = leave"), rekeying rights by state (many states require it between tenants — check), renter's insurance actually covering theft (the documentation habit).
8. **Common mistakes:** hiding a key (they check all five spots), upgrading the lock on a weak frame, announcing vacations online + a dark silent house, leaving ladders/tools out (you supply the entry kit), "safe" master-bedroom storage, alarm sign but windows open, buying cameras before fixing the door, assuming daytime = safe to leave things unlocked.

Research: Blevins/Kuhns UNC Charlotte offender survey (the deterrence rankings), Wright & Decker, BJS victimization tables for entry-point/timing stats, FBI UCR trends (burglary has fallen ~50% since 2010 — include for calibration; this page is anti-paranoia too), insurance claims data for loss patterns. Percentages vary by study — give ranges with sources, not false precision.

## Visual design

**"Casing the house" aesthetic** — the page adopts the burglar's-eye view: an annotated target-assessment look, dusk-toned and diagrammatic.

- Palette: deep dusk blue-gray base with amber "window light" accents and one alert red; feels like 6 PM reconnaissance, distinct from the site's true-dark dashboards.
- **Hero: an isometric cutaway house (inline SVG)** with every entry point tagged by its percentage — front door 34%, windows 23%, etc. — sized/heat-colored by frequency. Clickable tags scroll to the relevant hardware section. This diagram is the page's shareable artifact: it is the designated **og:image** (rendered as the 1200×630 social card) and the "screenshot this" block, so build it to stand alone.
- **Deterrence rankings as a "threat assessment" bar list:** offender-ranked items with dossier-style monospace annotations ("DOG — ANY SIZE: subject reports avoidance, 60%+").
- The strike-plate hero fix gets a **before/after exploded diagram**: ½" screws into jamb vs. 3" screws into stud, drawn blueprint-line style within a highlight panel.
- Timing data (section 3) as a **week × hour heat grid** — the 10 AM–3 PM weekday hot zone instantly visible.
- Upgrade list rendered as **price-tagged cards** sorted by deterrence-per-dollar, tag styling like hardware-store shelf labels.
- Myth corrections ("burglars pick locks," "night is the danger") as struck-through captions under a small "MOVIE VERSION / REALITY" paired motif used consistently.
