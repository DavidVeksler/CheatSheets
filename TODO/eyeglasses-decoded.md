# Spec: Eyeglasses — Why They Cost $400 and How to Pay $40

**Target file:** `eyeglasses-decoded.html`

**Why this topic:** One vertically-integrated company (EssilorLuxottica) owns the lens brands, the frame brands, the retail chains, AND a major vision-insurance plan — a fact most buyers don't know and no optical retailer will explain. The 10x price spread between retail ($300–600) and online ($20–80) is mostly channel, not product. Every "where to buy glasses" article online is affiliate-driven; the honest supply-chain literacy page can't exist commercially. Note for the builder: the ownership-map angle is the hook — build it entirely from public record (merger filings, brand lists); no personal or insider anecdotes on the page.

## Content approach

Three-act structure: **the market** (why the price is what it is), **your numbers** (Rx, PD, materials — the literacy core), **the playbook** (what to buy where). The tone rule: not anti-optometrist — the exam is real medicine (and the page says plainly that regular eye exams catch glaucoma, diabetes, hypertension); the skepticism is aimed at the retail markup chain after the exam room.

Sections:

1. **Quick reference: the playbook card.** The distilled strategy in one block: get the full Rx handed to you (your legal right, no purchase required), get/measure your PD, first-ever prescription or complex needs → buy in person; established single-vision Rx → online at 10–20% of retail; progressives → the hybrid strategy (primary pair fitted in person, backups/sunglasses online); always order a backup pair (the $25 insurance policy nobody mentions); replace scratched lenses, not frames you love (online lens-replacement services exist).
2. **The monopoly map — why glasses cost $400.** The page's hero: EssilorLuxottica's vertical integration drawn as one diagram — lens brands (Essilor, Varilux, Crizal, Transitions), frame brands (Ray-Ban, Oakley, Persol, Vogue, plus licensed manufacture for ~20 fashion houses — Chanel, Prada, Burberry...), retail (LensCrafters, Pearle Vision, Sunglass Hut, Target Optical), online (Glasses.com, FramesDirect), and **EyeMed vision insurance** — the same company on all four sides of the transaction. The economics stated plainly: frames cost single-digit dollars to produce; the markup is 10–20×; "designer" frames are the same factories with licensed logos. Density: the map covers 25+ named brands/chains grouped by layer, each verified against public record. One paragraph of history (the 2018 Essilor-Luxottica merger, ~70–80% estimates of the branded-eyewear market — use verified market-share figures with sources, or ranges with attribution).
3. **Your prescription is yours — the rights section.** The FTC Eyeglass Rule: the prescriber MUST give you the complete Rx after the exam, free, without you asking, and cannot require a purchase or charge for it; the Contact Lens Rule equivalent (and why contact Rx ≠ glasses Rx). What a complete Rx includes and the PD omission trick — PD is often left off because it's the retention moat. **How to measure PD at home:** the ruler-and-mirror method, the credit-card calibration photo method, app options; single vs. dual PD, distance vs. near PD (subtract ~3 mm), typical adult range (54–74 mm) so readers sanity-check. Expiration rules (1–2 yrs by state — one "verify your state" line).
4. **Reading the Rx — the decoder.** Every field with a worked example: OD/OS (right/left — the Latin), SPH (the ± diopter meaning, what -4.00 vs +2.00 sees like), CYL & AXIS (astigmatism as a pair — one without the other is a transcription error), ADD (the progressive/reading addition), prism/base (the rare field that means "buy in person"). A "transcribe it right" warning list: the minus-vs-plus cylinder convention difference between optometrists and online forms, common data-entry errors that ruin online orders.
5. **Lens materials and coatings — the verdict table.** Materials: CR-39 (cheap, heavy at high Rx, poor impact rating), polycarbonate (impact king — kids/sports mandatory), Trivex (poly's optics upgrade), high-index 1.61/1.67/1.74 — the decision rule by prescription strength (|SPH| < 2: don't pay for high index; 2–4: 1.61 maybe; 4–6: 1.67; 6+: 1.74 earns it) with edge-thickness the visible reason. Coatings, each with a verdict: anti-reflective (WORTH IT, the one upgrade that matters — but it's $30 online vs. $120 retail), scratch coating (usually bundled), UV (free in poly/high-index inherently — being charged for it is a tell), photochromic (lifestyle call; the car-windshield limitation stated), polarized for sunwear (WORTH IT for driving/water), **blue-light filtering (THEATER — the evidence review: no demonstrated eye-damage protection, sleep effects better handled by screen settings; cross-link theater-audit)**. Density: 6 materials + 7 coatings, every row with verdict + price-anchor online vs. retail.
6. **Buying online — the honest risk gradient.** Where online is a solved problem: single-vision distance/reading, established Rx, normal PD, frames sized from a pair that already fits (teach the temple-arm numbers: 52□18-140 decoded). Where it gets mixed: progressives (corridor placement and fitting-height measurement matter; success rates improve with conservative corridor choices; the hybrid strategy restated), strong prescriptions (high index + online = the best arbitrage BUT centration errors hurt more), rimless/drill-mount. Where the optician earns it: first progressive, prism, kids, post-surgery, frame adjustments (and the folklore: most shops adjust any frames free — walk in politely). Returns/remake policies as the real risk mitigator. Named examples per spec: Zenni, EyeBuyDirect, Warby Parker (home try-on model) — as *category examples with typical price bands*, no affiliate links, per Rule 4.
7. **Vision insurance — the math nobody shows.** The loading analysis: typical plan ~$150–200/yr premium buys an exam copay + ~$130–150 frame allowance + coating discounts at in-network (often EssilorLuxottica-owned) retailers — run the worked example: insured path at LensCrafters vs. cash exam ($75–125 at independent/Costco) + online glasses; the insurance usually loses for glasses-only users, breaks even for contacts+glasses families. The conflict-of-interest paragraph: EyeMed is EssilorLuxottica; VSP owns frame brands and labs too — "insurance" as a channel-steering device. When it IS worth it: employer-subsidized premiums, contacts users, multi-pair families (state the honest break-evens). Costco/Sam's optical as the middle path (independent-ish pricing, real opticians).
8. **Common mistakes:** leaving the exam without the Rx (going back is awkward; it's free at hand-off), buying the exam-day upsell under dilation (you literally can't evaluate lenses with dilated pupils — schedule the decision), paying high-index prices at |SPH| 1.5, blue-light surcharges, letting the shop keep PD hostage (ask directly; many will provide it — and you can measure it anyway), ordering progressives online as your first-ever pair, throwing away a great frame instead of re-lensing it, buying "designer" assuming different manufacture, skipping the exam entirely for years because retail burned you (decouple: cheap glasses, regular exams).

Research: FTC Eyeglass Rule and Contact Lens Rule texts (primary), EssilorLuxottica annual report/brand portfolio (primary for the map), AAO/AOA on blue-light evidence and exam intervals, verified market-share figures from reputable business press with attribution. All prices as ranges with "as of" dates.
