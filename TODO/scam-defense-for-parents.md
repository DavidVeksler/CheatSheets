## Content approach

Three-part architecture mirroring the three moments a reader arrives: **Before** (prevention setup — the family protocol), **During** (recognize the script in progress), **After** (damage control, without shame). Tone rule stated to the implementing model: zero condescension toward victims — these scripts work on surgeons and professors; the page repeatedly normalizes ("victims are targeted, not stupid") because shame is the #1 reason losses go unreported and repeat.

Sections:

1. **Quick reference: the three rules + the code word.** The distilled protocol: (1) **No legitimate entity is EVER paid in gift cards, wire transfers, crypto ATMs, or cash handed to a courier** — the payment method IS the tell, full stop; (2) **urgency + secrecy = scam** — anyone who says "don't tell your family" is the enemy; (3) **hang up, look up, call back** — end every inbound contact and re-initiate via a number you find yourself. Then the **family code word**: a shared secret phrase any family member must produce when calling in distress — defeats voice cloning entirely, costs nothing, takes one dinner conversation to set up. This block is the printable fridge card.
2. **The scam catalog.** One entry per scam, identical anatomy: *The script (as the victim experiences it, in dialogue) / The tells / The counter-move / Real scale (FTC/IC3 numbers, dated).* Density target: 12–14 entries — the twelve below are the floor, every entry carrying all four anatomy fields; a script without its counter-move doesn't ship. Ordered by dollar damage:
   - **Grandparent/family-emergency scam** (now with AI voice cloning — why the code word exists; the "bail money + lawyer + don't tell mom" structure).
   - **Romance scams** (the long con: months of grooming, never meets, always a reason; military/oil-rig/doctor-abroad personas; the money asks escalate; hardest to intervene — the dedicated family-conversation guidance: confrontation backfires, curiosity works, reverse-image-search the photos together).
   - **Pig butchering / fake investment platforms** (romance or friendship → crypto "investing" → fake dashboard showing gains → the withdrawal-fee trap; now the largest per-victim losses; the tell: any investing "opportunity" arriving via an unsolicited relationship).
   - **Tech-support popup** (frozen screen + phone number + remote access + "refund overpayment" bank-access con; the counter: the popup cannot hurt you — force-close the browser; nobody legitimate ever calls about your computer).
   - **Government imposters** (SSA "your number is suspended," IRS arrest threats, Medicare card scams — the flat facts: these agencies initiate by mail, never threaten arrest by phone, never take gift cards).
   - **Bank imposters / "fraud department" calls** (the cruelest: they call *warning you about fraud*, then have you "move money to a safe account" — banks never ask you to move money; spoofed caller ID explained: caller ID is trivially fakeable, treat it as decoration).
   - **Sweepstakes/lottery** ("you've won — pay the taxes first"), **charity scams** (disaster-chasing), **contractor/driveway knocks** (cross-link hiring-a-contractor.html), **funeral/obituary vultures** (cross-link death-logistics.html), **check overpayment** ("deposit this and return the difference" — the deposited-check clawback mechanic explained, because it's genuinely unintuitive), **toll/package smishing** (small dollars, credential harvesting).
3. **Why parents? The targeting mechanics.** Brief, destigmatizing: lead lists sold and resold ("sucker lists" — one victimization predicts re-targeting; the "recovery scam" that calls victims offering to recover their losses, the industry's most cynical product), cognitive changes affect scam-resistance before anything else is noticeable (cite the research gently), social isolation as the #1 risk factor (the romance-scam correlation) — which reframes defense: **regular family contact is a security control.**
4. **The hardening checklist (do these WITH your parent, one afternoon).** Density target: 10–14 checkable items, ordered by protection-per-minute. Practical setup list: bank/brokerage **trusted-contact person** designation (the quiet superpower — the institution can call you on suspicion) + account alerts >$X; carrier spam filtering + NOMOROBO-class apps; the phone answers-unknown-numbers-goes-to-voicemail default; credit freezes at all three bureaus (free, permanent, reversible — walk-through pattern); remove them from data-broker lists (cross-link privacy-data-broker-opt-out.html); a "before I send money to anyone new, I tell one person" pact framed as mutual (you commit too — it's a family norm, not surveillance); for higher need: view-only account access, bill-pay consolidation, and the honest escalation ladder toward POA (cross-link estate-documents.html) with the dignity-preserving framing for each step.
5. **"I think it's happening right now" — intervention.** For the reader mid-crisis: don't ridicule, don't ultimatum (scammers pre-script "your family will say I'm a scam — they don't want you to be happy"; confrontation confirms the script). What works: side-by-side verification ("let's call the bank together on the number on your card"), involving the bank's fraud team proactively (tellers can refuse/delay suspicious withdrawals; many states have report-and-hold laws), APS as a resource not a threat, and for romance scams: the slow-play (reverse image search together, request a live video call, let the evidence land without "I told you so").
6. **After the loss — the no-shame damage-control checklist.** The crisis-anchor section (#after). Density target: 10–12 time-tagged steps. Act in hours, not days — call the bank/card issuer fraud line first (wire recall windows are short but real; card chargebacks likelier), gift cards: call the issuer with card numbers/receipts (partial recovery happens more than folklore says), crypto: report fast to IC3 (tracing occasionally works; recovery firms promising guarantees are the recovery scam), report to FTC (reportfraud.ftc.gov) + IC3 + local police report (needed for insurance/taxes), place fraud alerts + freeze credit, change email password first (it's the master key), document everything with timestamps, and **expect the re-target** (victim lists — the next "investigator" who calls IS the same crew). Close with the mental-health paragraph: real losses include the shame spiral; AARP fraud victim support groups exist; report-rate stats to normalize.
7. **Common mistakes:** treating caller ID as identification, testing the parent with gotchas instead of building the protocol together, taking over all finances at the first incident (drives secrecy — graduated response instead), assuming "my parent is too smart" (professionals lose the most, dollar-wise), posting the scam publicly with the parent identifiable (guarantees they never tell you again), paying "recovery" services, and waiting to set up the code word until after the first cloned-voice call.

Research: FTC Consumer Sentinel + FBI IC3 annual reports for per-scam dollar figures (dated — update yearly; this is a freshness-cycle page), AARP Fraud Watch, state report-and-hold law status, trusted-contact (FINRA 4512) mechanics. All scam scripts written as realistic composites, not copied transcripts.

## Visual design

**Phone-screen aesthetic** — scams arrive through the phone, so the page renders them the way they actually look: as calls and texts on a phone. Warm, large-type, senior-shareable.

- Base: warm cream/ivory with high-contrast near-black text, **base font 19–20px** (the parent is a secondary reader; several blocks will be shown across a kitchen table), generous line height; one calm blue for structure, alert coral-red reserved exclusively for scam-side content.
- **Scam scripts rendered as phone UI mockups (pure CSS):** incoming-call screens ("Caller ID: 'SSA OFFICE' — remember: decoration, not identification") and SMS bubble threads for smishing — scammer bubbles in gray-red tint, the correct response rendered as the reply ("[Hang up. Look up. Call back.]"). Instantly scannable and screenshot-shareable; each mockup doubles as the social image for that scam's long-tail query.
- **The three rules + code word as the fridge card:** bordered, huge type, iconized (🎁🚫 payment-method rule), with a dedicated print stylesheet at half-letter size — the page's physical artifact, mirroring the hospital page's pocket card but visually distinct (rounded, warm, magnet-corner illustration).
- **Tells highlighted inside the scripts** with marker-style underline (not red boxes — the design teaches pattern-spotting inline, like an annotated transcript).
- The Before/During/After architecture as three large tab-like section markers with a phone-status-bar motif (●●○ progress dots).
- Checklists (hardening, after-the-loss) as real checkboxes with localStorage; the after-the-loss list numbered with time tags ("HOUR 1", "DAY 1", "WEEK 1") in badge styling.
- No dark patterns of its own: no fear-toned imagery, no stock-photo hooded hackers — the design brief is "kitchen table, not crime thriller."
- **og:image / shareable artifact:** the 1200×630 social card is the fridge card — the three rules + code word block in its huge-type bordered styling, which is also the page's physical print artifact, so the share card, the signature element, and the printout are one design. The per-scam phone-UI mockups remain the *secondary* shareables for each scam's long-tail query (as noted above), but the fridge card is the page's face.
