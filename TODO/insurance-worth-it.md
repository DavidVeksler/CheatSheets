# Spec: Insurance — What's Actuarially Worth Buying

**Target file:** `insurance-worth-it.html`

**Why this topic:** Every insurance guide online is written by an agent, a lead-gen site, or an insurer — the honest version cannot exist commercially because commissions poison the advice. The underlying decision framework (insure only large, unaffordable losses; self-insure the rest; expected value is always negative, that's fine) is stable, simple, and almost never stated plainly. Perfect no-affiliate LLM territory, and it pairs with the site's index-investing page.

## Content approach

Anchor everything to one principle, stated in the first paragraph: **insurance is a wealth transfer that's worth its negative expected value only when the loss would be financially catastrophic to you.** Loading (premium ÷ expected payout) is the price of that transfer. Everything else is applying this.

Sections:

1. **The framework.** Expected value, loading factor (typical: term life ~1.1–1.3, auto ~1.3–1.5, extended warranties 2–5+), risk of ruin, and the deductible principle: raise deductibles to the max you can absorb — you're buying less of a bad deal. Self-insurance = emergency fund. Include the "insure the tail, not the noise" one-liner.
2. **Quick reference: the verdict table.** Every common insurance product, columns: product, verdict (BUY / BUY IF / SKIP / TRAP), why in one line, typical loading, notes. Density target: 22–28 rows — the ~20 products below are the floor; fill breadth with close variants (travel medical, jewelry/valuables riders, identity-theft insurance, gap insurance, wedding insurance), each earning a real verdict. Rows:
   - **BUY:** term life (if anyone depends on your income — and only term), auto liability (high limits, they're cheap), homeowners/renters, umbrella ($1M+ costs ~$200–400/yr — most underrated product in the market), long-term disability (own-occupation; more likely than death during working years), health (US: at minimum catastrophic exposure capped).
   - **BUY IF:** long-term care (the hard one — honest treatment of the broken market, hybrid policies, self-insure-if-net-worth-above threshold), flood/earthquake by geography (NFIP gaps), professional liability.
   - **SKIP:** extended warranties/AppleCare (self-insure via the premiums you don't pay), phone insurance, rental car CDW (check your card + auto policy first — with the real gotchas: exotics, Ireland/Italy/Israel exclusions), flight insurance, pet insurance for most (worst-case vet bill ~$5–10k ≈ self-insurable; honest counter-case included), credit life, mortgage life.
   - **TRAP:** whole/universal life as investment (decompose it: term + opaque ~2–4% investment with heavy fees and surrender charges; the "buy term and invest the difference" arithmetic with one worked 30-yr example), cancer/dread-disease policies, kids' life insurance, timeshare-adjacent products.
3. **The agent's playbook.** Why whole life is pushed (first-year commissions often 50–100% of annual premium vs. term's much smaller cut), "permanent protection" framing, "forced savings" framing, the college-funding pitch, captive vs. independent agents vs. fee-only advisors — who's paid by whom.
4. **Coverage sizing quick math.** Term life: 10–12× income or DIME method, laddering; disability: 60% gross benchmark, taxability by who pays premium; umbrella: cover net worth + future earnings exposure; liability minimums vs. sane limits (state minimums are dangerously low — one example). Density target: four sizing methods, each ending in a worked number for a concrete household, not a formula alone.
5. **Common mistakes:** insuring boxes not tails (warranties yes, disability no — the exact inversion of correct), low deductibles + comprehensive on a beater, naming your estate as beneficiary, letting term lapse before dependents are independent, double-coverage ignorance (credit card benefits, employer policies), cancelling whole life without checking surrender timing/1035 exchange.

Research: NAIC loss-ratio data for loading figures (dated), Insurance Information Institute, standard personal-finance actuarial sources. The verdicts are framework-derived, not vendor-derived — keep it that way.

## Visual design

**Verdict-forward, ledger aesthetic** — the page should look like an audit, not a brochure.

- Design language: financial-statement styling — light cream/white background, near-black ink, tabular-nums serif or slab numerals, hairline table rules. Think Berkshire annual letter, not fintech gradient.
- **Verdict badges** as the signature element: bold stamped-look pills (BUY = green ink, BUY IF = amber, SKIP = gray strikethrough-adjacent, TRAP = red stamp, slightly rotated like a rubber stamp). Instantly scannable down the table.
- **Loading factor shown as a "cents-per-dollar" bar** per row: a $1.00 bar with the expected-payout portion filled — makes "you get back 40¢" visceral for warranties vs. 85¢ for term life.
- The whole-life decomposition (section 2 TRAP) as a **side-by-side 30-year ledger table** — two columns of compounding numbers ending in the punchline gap.
- One interactive element max: a deductible-savings mini-calculator (premium delta vs. deductible delta → break-even years). Plain JS.
- **og:image / shareable artifact:** the 1200×630 social card is a crop of the verdict table with the stamps doing the talking — a few rows showing a green BUY, a gray SKIP, and the rotated red TRAP is what makes the thumbnail stop a scroll. The "screenshot this" artifact is the full verdict table (same subject, deliberately aligned); the whole-life 30-year ledger is the secondary shareable for the term-vs-whole argument specifically.
