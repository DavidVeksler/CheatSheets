## Content approach

The mental model, stated in paragraph one and enforced everywhere: **a safe does not stop anything — it buys minutes, and the rating tells you how many, against which tools.** Every product on the market is honestly described by (burglary minutes, fire minutes, anchored or not). The page's job is teaching readers to read those three numbers through the marketing.

Sections:

1. **Quick reference: the ratings decoder table.** The spine of the page. Rows: no rating ("security container"/lockbox), CA DOJ gun safe standard (a floor, not a rating), **RSC Level I** (UL 1037: 5 min, hand tools — this is nearly every consumer and gun "safe" sold), RSC II (10 min, bigger tool list), **TL-15 / TL-30** (15/30 min against the *door* with power tools, by a professional — commercial jeweler grade), TL-30x6 (all six sides), TRTL (torch), plus **UL 72 fire classes** as a separate column group (Class 350 = paper, Class 125 = digital media, ½–4 hr). Columns: what it resists, for how long, typical weight, typical price new/used, who actually needs it — expect 10–12 rating rows, every cell populated (no "varies"). The punchline row annotation: the gap between RSC ($800–2,500 marketed as "gun safe") and used TL-15 (often similar money) is the page's best-kept folklore.
2. **The threat model — who is attacking your safe?** Three attacker profiles, because the right safe differs:
   - **The 8-minute burglar** (cross-link how-burglars-break-in.html): unplanned, hand tools, wants to *leave with* the safe — which is why **bolting down beats every lock upgrade**; an unanchored safe under ~750 lbs is a carrying case with a handle. The anchoring section: concrete anchors, wood-floor lag + steel plate patterns, why the bedroom closet install usually isn't anchored and what to do in a rental.
   - **The targeted/informed attacker** (knows you have coins/crypto/guns; brings time and tools): this is where RSC honestly fails and TL ratings begin; also the coercion paragraph — no safe defends against a wrench held on you; duress codes, decoys, and not advertising holdings (forum opsec, delivery discretion — the crane truck in the driveway tells the street).
   - **Fire** (the most likely event for most households): fire ≠ burglary, ratings are separate, and most burglary-focused steel is a bad oven for paper and worse for media.
3. **The bypass folklore — why cheap safes are theater.** The LockPickingLawyer-era catalog, 6–8 entries, each in two lines (what the attack is, which class it works on): rare-earth magnet on solenoid locks, bouncing/spiking, paperclip resets, factory bypass/override keys (sold online by safe model), prying stacked sheet-metal doors with a screwdriver, the reset-button-through-the-fascia trick on hotel-style units. Verdict framing: these attacks define the floor of the market — anything vulnerable to them is a *child-and-honest-guest deterrent*, which is a legitimate job, just not the job on the box.
4. **Gun safes — the marketing decoded.** The section for the site's firearms audience:
   - Steel gauge truth (12–14 ga bodies on most "gun safes" = angle-grinder and even axe territory; 10 ga and below is where bodies get honest; gauge numbers go DOWN as steel gets thicker — stated because marketing exploits the confusion).
   - Bolt-count theater (20 chrome bolts behind a thin door), paint-on "fire rating" with no UL/ETL verification (the manufacturer-tested 1200°F claims vs. UL 72), door seals that matter (intumescent).
   - **Quick-access pistol safes are a different product for a different job**: child access prevention + speed, not burglary resistance — buy them for that job (and check the solenoid-magnet vulnerability list before buying); safe storage laws by state exist (one-line note, dated).
   - The honest recommendation ladder: lock the guns (any rated container) → anchor it → if the collection's value is real, used TL-15 or a proper vault-door closet conversion.
5. **Documents, media, and seed phrases — the fire side.** What goes in a fire safe vs. what shouldn't be home at all: paper (Class 350, 1 hr covers most house fires — cite typical residential burn temps/duration), digital media needs Class 125 (or accept cloud as the real backup — the honest note that 3-2-1 backup beats any box for data), **crypto seed phrases**: fire safes are for paper, but stamped-metal seed plates survive what paper can't; the safe protects the *metal* from discovery, not fire (cross-link bitcoin-self-custody-guide.html). **Safe deposit boxes, honestly:** cheap, off-site, strong against burglary/fire — but not insured by the bank, access dies with banking hours, and the executor-access problem at death is real (cross-link estate-documents.html and death-logistics.html: what should NOT be in the box — the original will, POA, burial instructions).
6. **Placement, decoys, and the storage matrix.** Where safes go (not the master closet if avoidable — first place searched, cross-link burglars page; garage-floor anchoring is strong but casing-visible; basement corners; the weight-vs-floor-joist note), the $30 decoy safe strategy (give the 8-minute burglar a win that costs you nothing), diversion storage's honest limits (the burglar has read the same fake-book listicles). Then the **what-goes-where matrix**: cash / jewelry / guns / passports+documents / media / seed plates × home RSC / home TL / fire safe / bank box / not-at-home — with a verdict per cell (6 × 5 = 30 populated verdict cells, no blanks).
7. **Buying guide — without links.** Weight as the honest proxy (RSC ~300–800 lbs, TL-15 1,500+ lbs), **the used commercial market** (jewelers and pawn shops upgrade/close; TL-15s at RSC prices; what to check used: door fit, relocker status, get the combo changed by a safe tech), dial vs. electronic locks (dial: slower, decades-reliable; electronic: fast, keypad wear shows your code, battery/failure modes; the EMP fear → cross-link theater-audit verdict; key-override = added bypass surface), delivery and install reality (safe movers exist; 800 lbs does not go down basement stairs on a hand truck), insurance riders (some policies require rated containers for jewelry/firearm schedules — check wording; photo inventory habit).
8. **Common mistakes:** not bolting it down (the #1, worth repeating), buying burglary steel for a fire problem or vice versa, believing box-store "fireproof" (nothing is fire*proof*, only fire-*rated*), storing the original will in the bank box, gun safe in the garage facing the street window, keeping the factory code/override key in the junk drawer, letting the safe's delivery announce itself to the neighborhood, $2,000 safe + $0 of anchoring + spare house key under the mat.

Research: UL 1037/687/72 rating definitions (primary source the claims), CA DOJ firearm safety device standards, residential fire temperature/duration data (NIST), state safe-storage law status (dated). No product recommendations by brand — classes and ratings only; that's the anti-affiliate stance made structural.

## Visual design

**Rating-plate industrial aesthetic** — the page styled like the machined spec plates riveted to commercial safes: everything is a rated, stamped number.

- Palette: gunmetal/graphite grays with brushed-steel texture accents (subtle CSS gradients, no image files), safety-orange as the single accent, off-white text panels for body copy.
- **Section headers as stamped spec plates:** bordered metal-plate styling with rivet dots in the corners and engraved-look type ("SECTION 4 — GUN SAFES: MARKETING DECODED").
- **The ratings decoder (section 1) as a wall of UL-style labels:** each rating class rendered as its own label-plate card (the visual language of the actual UL sticker), with the minutes figure huge — 5 / 15 / 30 — because minutes are the entire point.
- **A "minutes" motif throughout:** every product class carries a stopwatch badge (⏱ 5 MIN vs. ⏱ 30 MIN); the threat-model section pits attacker time budgets against container ratings on a simple horizontal time-bar chart (burglar's 8 min vs. RSC's 5 vs. TL-15's 15 — the whole argument in one graphic). **This time-bar chart is the 1200×630 og:image**; the what-goes-where matrix is the "screenshot this" (and print) artifact.
- The bypass catalog as **tool-tag cards**: each attack labeled like an evidence tag (attack / tool cost / defeats which class).
- The what-goes-where matrix as a bordered steel-grid table with verdict glyphs (✓ / △ / ✗) and one-word verdicts.
- Weight rendered honestly: a scale-bar comparing RSC vs. TL-15 vs. "your floor joists' polite limit" — a dry-humor touch consistent with the page's voice.
- Print stylesheet for the matrix + anchoring checklist.
