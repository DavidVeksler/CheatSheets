# Spec: Contract Red Flags for Freelancers & Consultants — Built to Feed to Your AI

**Target file:** `contract-red-flags.html`

**Why this topic:** Lawyers charge $400–600 to review freelance contracts that always turn on the same ~10 clauses. The 2026 twist: people now paste contracts into an LLM for review, but generic "review this contract" prompts produce generic output. This page's dual design — human-readable clause guide AND a copy-paste review rubric that turns any AI into a specialized contract reviewer — is a genuinely novel format no competitor has. The freelancer/consultant audience overlaps the site's dev-heavy readership exactly.

## Content approach

Dual-mode structure, stated in the intro: read it yourself for the top 5, or copy the **AI Review Rubric** block and paste it with your contract into any capable model. Every clause section feeds both modes — the human explanation and a machine-checkable criterion.

Sections:

1. **Quick reference: the 60-second scan.** Before deep review, the five instant deal-breakers to Ctrl-F for: "work made for hire" + "all intellectual property" combos with no carve-out, "indemnify... arising from or related to" (unbounded), unlimited liability (no cap clause at all), "non-compete", payment terms >45 days or "pay when paid." Each with the Ctrl-F search terms literally given.
2. **The clause-by-clause guide.** Density target: 12 full clause blocks plus the 5 compact "quiet ones" — 17 clause treatments total, every full block carrying all four anatomy parts (no abbreviated blocks). One block per clause, in identical four-part anatomy — *What it says (real-world sample language, verbatim style) / What it actually means / Red-flag versions vs. acceptable versions / Your counter-language (copy-paste ready)*:
   - **IP assignment** — work-for-hire vs. assignment vs. license; the carve-outs that matter: pre-existing IP/background technology schedule, generalized skills & know-how, portfolio rights, open-source tooling. The dev-specific trap: assigning "all inventions during the engagement" (moonlight projects captured). Counter: assignment upon full payment, background-IP exhibit.
   - **Indemnification** — the asymmetry test (mutual or one-way?), scope ("arising from the Services" ok; "related to this Agreement" unbounded), third-party-claims-only limitation, the defense-costs trap (duty to defend = paying their lawyers from day one). Counter-language for mutual, capped, third-party-only indemnity.
   - **Limitation of liability** — the two-part standard: cap at fees paid (or 12 months of fees) + mutual waiver of consequential damages; the carve-out negotiation (what legitimately sits outside the cap: confidentiality breach, IP infringement, gross negligence). Red flag: their cap exists, yours doesn't.
   - **Payment terms** — Net-15/30 vs. Net-60/90 (real cost: Net-90 ≈ a 2–4% price cut in financing terms — show the math), deposit norms (25–50%), late-payment interest (state max rates exist), kill fees, "pay when paid" (their client's default becomes yours), milestone acceptance deadlines (silence = acceptance after N days, or invoices float forever).
   - **Scope & change control** — "as reasonably requested" = unlimited revisions; the fix: enumerated deliverables + written change-order requirement + revision counts.
   - **Non-compete / non-solicit** — non-competes for contractors (often unenforceable but chilling — note the FTC rule's litigation status, dated, and state splits: CA void, others reasonable-scope); client non-solicits (reasonable) vs. industry non-competes (walk); the placement-agency conversion-fee variant.
   - **Termination** — for-convenience asymmetry (they can, you can't), notice periods, payment-through-termination for WIP, IP status of unpaid work on termination.
   - **Warranties** — "error-free/defect-free" software warranties (impossible; counter: material-conformance-to-spec for N days), disclaim implied warranties.
   - **Insurance requirements** — when a $2M E&O demand on a $5k gig means walk vs. negotiate; typical E&O/GL costs so freelancers can price it in.
   - **Confidentiality/NDA overreach** — perpetual terms on everything (norm: 3–5 yrs except trade secrets), residuals clauses (ask for one — it's the pro move), one-way NDAs for two-way conversations.
   - **Governing law & disputes** — their-home-state courts + fee-shifting against you; arbitration pros/cons at freelance scale (cross-link small-claims-court.html: the small-claims exemption).
   - **The quiet ones:** assignment of the contract itself (your client sells; you now work for whoever), audit rights, publicity/logo rights (get portfolio use back), force majeure asymmetry, "time is of the essence."
3. **THE AI REVIEW RUBRIC — the page's centerpiece.** A single self-contained prompt block (~600–900 words) the reader copies verbatim and pastes along with their contract. Structure of the rubric: role framing ("you are reviewing on behalf of the independent contractor..."), the full checklist distilled to testable criteria ("For each clause below, quote the relevant contract text, classify as GREEN/AMBER/RED per these definitions, and propose replacement language..."), the specific standards from section 2 encoded as pass/fail thresholds (liability cap present and ≥ mutual? payment ≤ Net-30? IP assignment conditioned on payment?), an output format spec (table: clause / verdict / quoted text / proposed redline), and instructions to flag *missing* protective clauses, not just bad present ones (the absence checklist: no liability cap, no kill fee, no background-IP carve-out, no late-payment interest). Explicitly instruct the model to answer "not present in contract" honestly rather than inventing quotes — a hallucination guard written into the rubric.
4. **Negotiation reality.** Four plays, each concrete enough to execute: what's actually negotiable at each client size (enterprise procurement won't move indemnity but will move payment terms and IP carve-outs; startups the reverse), the "3 asks" strategy (pick your three; everything red-flagged isn't equally deadly), the walk-away short list (unlimited personal liability, industry non-compete, IP with no payment condition), and how to send redlines without seeming difficult (the cover-email script).
5. **Common mistakes** (expect 6+ entries): signing the client's MSA "to be easy" instead of sending your own SOW template first, negotiating rate but not terms (Net-90 + unlimited revisions erase the rate win), assuming unenforceable = harmless (defense costs are the punishment), reusing a US template for foreign clients, letting the AI review replace reading the quoted text yourself (the rubric forces quotes precisely so you can verify).

Research: standard freelance MSA norms (AIGA/Freelancers Union contract standards), current FTC non-compete rule status (volatile — date it), state non-compete splits. Sample clause language must be realistic but original (drafted for the page, not copied from a proprietary contract). Legal-information-not-advice disclaimer.

## Visual design

**Redline/markup aesthetic** — the page looks like a contract under tracked-changes review, because that's what it teaches.

- Base: white "document" column on a neutral desk-gray background, contract-serif body for sample clauses vs. clean sans for commentary — the two voices visually distinct.
- **Sample clause language rendered as document excerpts** with margin ruling and paragraph numbers; red-flag phrases shown with **literal redline styling** (red strikethrough) and counter-language as **green inserted-text styling** (underlined green), exactly like Word tracked changes. Margin comments as yellow comment-bubbles with connector lines for the "what it actually means" notes.
- GREEN/AMBER/RED verdict chips on every clause variant, consistent with the rubric's output vocabulary.
- **The AI Rubric block styled as a terminal/code block** — dark panel, monospace, prominent COPY button, token-count badge — visually signaling "this is the machine-readable artifact." Sticky "Copy the rubric" button follows scroll.
- The 60-second scan as a literal Ctrl-F card: each deal-breaker phrase in a bordered `kbd`-styled search box.
- One interaction beyond copy buttons: clause-anatomy blocks collapse to their verdict summary for scanning, expand for the full four-part anatomy.
- **og:image / shareable artifact:** the 1200×630 social card is the **60-second-scan Ctrl-F card** rendered in the redline aesthetic — bordered `kbd` search boxes plus RED chips read at thumbnail size, where the rubric (a wall of monospace) doesn't. The "screenshot this" artifact is the **AI Rubric terminal block** — the thing readers share, save, and re-paste.
