# Spec: The Glue Chart — Which Adhesive for Which Two Materials

**Target file:** `the-glue-chart.html`

**Why this topic:** "What glue for X to Y" is one of the internet's eternal questions, and the answer space is a genuine matrix — the right adhesive depends on *both* materials plus load, gap, flex, and water exposure — which is exactly why no one maintains the full table: it's tedious cross-referencing across manufacturer TDS sheets, woodworking forums, and materials-science common knowledge. The famous existing resource (thistothat.com) proves the demand and is decades stale and sparse. This is the purest, most staleness-proof cross-source-normalization play in the collection: chemistry doesn't drift, and the page can be *the* reference.

## Content approach

Two-layer architecture: **the matrix answers the question; the chemistry section explains it** — so the reader in a hurry gets the cell, and the reader who lingers learns why (and stops needing the chart). The page's honesty edge over every glue-brand site: it recommends *chemistry classes*, not SKUs, it says "mechanical fastener" when glue is the wrong answer, and it treats the unglueables honestly.

Sections:

1. **Quick reference: THE MATRIX.** Materials × materials, ~14×14: wood, metal, rigid plastic–easy (ABS/PVC/acrylic/PS), rigid plastic–hard (PE/PP — polyolefins get their own row because the answer changes completely), flexible plastic/vinyl, rubber/silicone (silicone also split out — the other unglueable), glass, ceramic/porcelain, stone/concrete/masonry, fabric/leather, paper/cardboard, foam (EPS gets the solvent warning), drywall/plaster. Each cell: the primary adhesive class + a fallback + a flag glyph where prep is critical or a fastener is honestly better. Symmetric matrix — build the data as one triangle, render both. Density: ~100 unique pairings populated; no cell ships as "varies" (Rule: if a cell truly depends, the cell says on *what* in four words and points to its section).
2. **The adhesive roster — one block per chemistry.** Density: 14–16 entries, identical anatomy (what it is / bonds well / fails on / gap-filling? / water/heat rating / cure time to handle & full / shelf life after opening / the gotcha): CA "super glue" (+ the accelerator and the brittleness truth; the O-ring/rubber niche where it's king), 2-part epoxy (5-min vs. slow-cure strength difference — the folklore that 5-minute epoxy is the weakest common choice), PVA wood glues (Type I/II/III water ratings; "stronger than the wood" is true and what it implies), polyurethane (Gorilla-class: the foaming, the moisture-cure, where it's actually best — dissimilar porous pairs — vs. its overuse), construction adhesive (subfloor/panel class), contact cement (laminates/leather — the both-sides-dry method people get wrong), silicone sealant-as-adhesive (glass/aquarium niche; it's a gasket, not a structural glue), MS-polymer hybrids (the modern outdoor do-most), hot melt (speed vs. strength honesty; the crafts-vs-repair line), solvent cements (PVC/ABS — welding, not gluing; why primer purple matters for pressure pipe), plastic-model cement (polystyrene welding), UV-cure (glass repair niche), thread lockers (the metal-to-metal answer that isn't glue-shaped), spray adhesive, wood-specific specialty (hide glue one line for the furniture-repair reversibility folklore), acrylic structural (MMA — the metal bonder pros use).
3. **Know your plastic — the identification section.** The matrix's hardest column made usable: recycling-code lookup table (1–7 → glueability verdict), the folklore tests (polyolefin feel/float test — PE/PP float in water; the scratch/flex character notes), and **the polyolefin protocol**: PE/PP/HDPE bond only with primer systems (polyolefin primer + CA, or specialty two-part acrylics) or plastic welding — plus the honest "design for mechanical retention instead" advice. Silicone's equivalent paragraph (nothing sticks to cured silicone except more silicone — plan accordingly). This section is the page's expertise signal and the answer to its best snippet query.
4. **Joint prep — why glue "doesn't work."** The section that rescues every failed-bond reader: clean/degrease (IPA, not WD-40-contaminated rags), abrade for tooth (grit guidance), the clamp-pressure and open-time table per chemistry, gap-filling vs. gap-hating adhesives (CA hates gaps; epoxy loves them — the single most useful pairing rule), temperature/humidity effects (CA loves humidity, PU needs it, epoxy hates cold), end-grain starvation (the woodworking classic), and release surfaces (what glue *won't* stick to, used deliberately: tape dams, wax paper).
5. **The decision layer.** When glue is the wrong answer (structural loads, safety parts, high heat — fasteners/welding/replacement, one honest paragraph); repair triage for the classic broken objects, each a two-line recipe arriving at a specific answer: ceramic mug handle (epoxy or CA-with-caveat; food-safety note honestly handled), eyeglass hinge (don't — the metal fatigue truth, spring-hinge reality; link eyeglasses page), shoe sole (urethane shoe adhesive class, not CA), car trim clip (fastener, not glue), plastic toy (identify the plastic first — §3), headphone band, garden hose (replace the fitting). Density: 8–10 triage recipes.
6. **Storage & shelf-life folklore:** CA in the freezer (works), epoxy pump-tops, the opened-tube half-life table (most opened CA is dead in weeks — buy small tubes), nozzle-clog prevention tricks, and the safety block (CA + cotton exotherm, solvent cement ventilation, epoxy sensitization with bare-hands habits, EPS + solvent melt) — one tight block, not scattered warnings.
7. **Common mistakes:** using CA on a gap it can't fill, gluing an unidentified plastic and blaming the glue, skipping degrease on "clean-looking" parts, 5-minute epoxy for a load joint because it's fast, hot glue on anything that lives in a car (summer), re-gluing over old failed adhesive, treating construction adhesive as a precision glue, expecting any adhesive to fix a part that flexes (the flex-kills-rigid-glue rule), and buying the big bottle (shelf life beats unit price for occasional users).

Research: manufacturer technical data sheets for representative products per class (primary for cure/strength/compatibility claims), materials-science references for the polyolefin/silicone surface-energy explanations, woodworking-authority sources for PVA/joint claims. Rule 1: strength and cure figures are anchors; verify against TDS sheets, and where TDS values conflict across brands in a class, give the honest range.
