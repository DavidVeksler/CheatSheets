# Spec: Flight Cancelled? The Disruption Playbook

**Target file:** `flight-disruption-playbook.html`

**Why this topic:** Gate-agent and FlyerTalk folklore — the parallel-rebooking moves, the interline rules, the phone-desk tricks — exists only in scattered forum threads, while airlines actively obscure the rights side (cash refunds owed vs. vouchers offered, EU261's €250–600, DOT's automatic-refund rule). Aggregation + folklore + adversarial-information in one page.

## Content approach

Two halves, in this order because that's crisis order: **Act** (the next 60 minutes decide your outcome) then **Extract** (what they owe you, claimed later, calmly).

Sections:

1. **Quick reference: the first 15 minutes.** The parallel-processing drill the moment the cancellation hits: (1) open the airline app and grab any decent rebooking hold IMMEDIATELY (inventory drains in minutes — you can refine later), (2) simultaneously get in the gate line, (3) simultaneously call — and call the **international desk** (Canada/UK/Australia numbers listed for major US carriers: same rebooking power, no hold queue), (4) check the airline's own interline/partner options so you can *propose* a specific flight ("can you put me on the United 1043?" beats "what do you have?"). The core folklore rule: **agents can do more for passengers who bring them a specific solution.** Plus: same-day alternatives checklist — nearby airports, train (Northeast corridor / Europe), driving break-even (~4–5 hrs). Density: the drill is 4 numbered steps; international-desk numbers for the 5–6 major US carriers; alternatives checklist 5–7 entries.
2. **Know your leverage: what kind of disruption is this?** The table that determines everything: controllable (mechanical, crew, IT) vs. uncontrollable (weather, ATC) × cancellation vs. delay length × where you are (US / EU-UK / elsewhere) → what you're owed. This is the page's mental model: your rights depend on the *cause* and the *jurisdiction*, and the airline will not volunteer either. Density: the full static matrix runs ~15–20 rows (3 jurisdictions × 2 causes × 3 delay/cancel buckets, collapsed where outcomes merge).
3. **The US rules (post-2024 DOT automatic-refund rule).** Cancellations and "significant changes" (defined: 3 hr domestic / 6 hr international, downgrades, added connections) → **automatic cash refund** if you don't accept the alternative — refund of the ticket, not a voucher; agents will offer credit first, the script to decline it. Controllable-delay commitments per the DOT dashboard (meals after 3 hrs, hotels for overnight — airline-by-airline commitments table: all 10 dashboard carriers, one row each). What US law does NOT give you: EU-style cash compensation. Checked-bag fee refunds on delayed bags (the timeline thresholds). Involuntary denied boarding is different and better: 200–400% of one-way fare in cash (current caps, dated) — and the folklore on volunteering well (negotiate: dollars not vouchers, hotel, confirmed seat, ask for more when the ask escalates).
4. **EU261 / UK261 — the money section.** Applies to: any flight departing EU/UK, or EU/UK carriers flying in — the coverage matrix (US carrier departing US → EU is NOT covered; the return leg is). The compensation table: €250/400/600 by distance (3 distance bands × delay/notice conditions), delay >3 hrs at *arrival*, cancellation <14 days notice, the extraordinary-circumstances exemption (weather yes, mechanical NO — airlines claim it anyway; deny-and-appeal folklore), duty of care (meals/hotel) applies even when compensation doesn't. How to claim: direct to airline first (template letter link/inline), national enforcement bodies, when the 30%-cut claim firms (AirHelp et al.) are actually worth it (never for clear-cut cases, maybe for litigation-required ones). Canada's APPR and Brazil/Turkey/Israel regimes in one summary row each.
5. **Folklore toolkit — the moves.** Rapid-fire entries (10–12; the list below is the floor, not the ceiling): the 24-hr rule for booking fresh tickets as backup; **original routing credit / "flat tire rule"** informal policies; why booking the first flight of the morning is the single best prevention (aircraft already overnighted at the gate); rolling delays = start researching at delay #2, not #4; Twitter/X DM desks still staffed and fast at several carriers; airport lounge agents rebook too (day passes as queue-jumping); when a "schedule change" email months out is a free-cancellation golden ticket; credit card trip-delay insurance (the underused one: 6–12 hr thresholds, $500/day-class benefits — pay with the right card); hotel-walking rights when the airline's hotel desk shrugs (keep receipts, reasonable-cost reimbursement); PNR basics — check your record for the rebooking before the agent finishes typing.
6. **When checked bags go missing.** Delayed vs. lost timelines, Montreal Convention liability (~1,300 SDR ≈ $1,700-ish, dated), interim-expense rights (buy the toiletries, keep receipts), the 21-day lost threshold, why AirTags changed the game (and airlines' evolving policy on them).
7. **Common mistakes** (the 7 below, minimum): accepting the auto-rebooking without checking alternatives (the app's first offer is rarely the best), accepting a voucher when owed cash, leaving the airport without a written cancellation-reason statement (needed for EU261/insurance), booking a new ticket yourself *before* letting the airline fail to rebook (kills reimbursement claims — sequence matters), waiting in line doing nothing else, claiming via a 30%-cut firm for a slam-dunk case, not knowing the disruption cause classification is contestable.

Research: DOT automatic-refund rule text + the airline commitments dashboard, EU261 regulation + CJEU mechanical-defect rulings, Montreal Convention limits, current IDB compensation caps, carrier international phone numbers. Rot speeds and re-verification for all of these live in the volatile-facts register above — date each on the page per that register.

## Visual design

**Departure-board aesthetic** — split-flap/airport-signage language, instantly readable as "airport" and unlike anything else on the site.

- Dark charcoal background with the classic **amber monospace departure-board type** for section headers (CSS letter-tiles with subtle flap-gap lines; a restrained one-time flap animation on the hero only). Body text in a clean light-on-dark sans for readability — the board styling is for headers/status elements, not paragraphs.
- Status vocabulary as the design system: entries tagged with board-style status chips — **DELAYED / CANCELLED / REFUND DUE / COMP €600** — amber for actions, green for money owed, red for mistakes.
- **The first-15-minutes drill as a boarding-pass-shaped card:** perforated edge, big step numbers, designed to screenshot to your phone before every trip (include a "save this card" hint + print-to-wallet-size stylesheet). This card is also the **og:image**: render the 1200×630 social preview from it, so the "screenshot this" artifact and the share card are the same object.
- **The leverage table (section 2) as an interactive router:** three toggles (Where? / Cause? / Delay length?) → the owed-to-you panel updates, board-style. Static full matrix as the no-JS fallback.
- EU261 compensation table styled as a fare board: distance bands × delay → € amounts in big amber numerals.
- Airport-pictogram-style icons (baggage, hotel, meal) drawn as simple SVG glyphs consistent with ISO wayfinding style.
- Mobile-first, per the reading-conditions statement in Targeting: single column, huge tap targets, phone numbers as tel: links — the gate-B37-at-11-PM reader is the design's acceptance test.
