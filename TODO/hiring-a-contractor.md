# Spec: Hiring a Contractor Without Getting Burned

**Target file:** `hiring-a-contractor.html`

**Why this topic:** General contractors know exactly which of their competitors are flaky and why the deposit-then-vanish pattern works — but that folklore circulates only inside the trades. The consumer-facing "content" is lead-gen (Angi, Thumbtack) with a financial interest in NOT teaching skepticism. Home renovation disputes are a top consumer-complaint category every year, the failure modes are stereotyped and preventable, and the defense is structural (payment schedules, lien waivers), not vigilance. Pairs with home-maintenance-guide.html and feeds small-claims-court.html when it goes wrong anyway.

## Content approach

Organizing principle: **you don't prevent contractor disasters by judging character — you prevent them with structure.** The page teaches the payment-schedule-and-paperwork machine that makes honesty the contractor's easiest option. Second recurring device: folklore from the GC's side of the table ("what good contractors wish you knew" — including how NOT to be a nightmare client, which is also how you get the good ones to take your job).

Sections:

1. **Quick reference: the deal-structure card.** The non-negotiables in one block: deposit ≤10% or state cap (CA: 10% or $1,000, whichever is less — state cap table), payments tied to completed milestones never dates, final payment 10–15% held until punch-list completion + lien waivers, everything in writing including changes, verify license + insurance certificates directly (not photocopies — call the insurer for a COI naming you as certificate holder), never pay cash, never pay for materials the contractor hasn't delivered.
2. **Finding candidates — where the good ones actually are.** Folklore section: the good ones don't advertise (booked via word-of-mouth; a contractor available to start tomorrow is a signal, not a convenience), ask tradespeople you already trust (your plumber knows which GCs pay their subs — **subs are the best referees in the industry**), supply-house counter staff know who pays their accounts, recent-permit records at the building department show who does work like yours legally (public records — a genuinely unknown move), lead-gen platforms decoded (contractors pay per lead; reviews are survivorship-biased; use them to generate a long list, never as vetting).
3. **Vetting — the checks that predict outcomes.** Six checks, ranked by predictive value:
   - License check (state lookup links pattern; what license class covers your job; unlicensed = no lien rights against you in some states but also no recourse fund access — the tradeoffs stated honestly).
   - Insurance: GL + workers' comp (the horror scenario that makes comp non-negotiable: uninsured worker injured on your property claims against YOUR homeowner's policy).
   - The reference call script — but call the *subs and suppliers*, not just clients ("does he pay you on time?" predicts your outcome better than "was the tile nice?").
   - Visit a job in progress (a clean, safe site at 2 PM Tuesday tells you more than a portfolio).
   - Court-records search (small claims + liens filed by/against them — free at the county site).
   - State recovery funds: several states maintain victim-compensation funds for licensed-contractor failures (list — a reason licensing matters beyond competence).
4. **Bids — reading the spread.** Get 3+ comparable bids on an identical written scope. The folklore rules: **the too-low bid is the most expensive one** (it's a door-opener; change orders are the business model — the mechanism explained), a vague bid is a blank check (line-item allowances for finishes, brands/model numbers specified, "allowance" games decoded), red-flag phrases — expect 6+ quoted phrases, each with the mechanism decoded ("we'll work out the details as we go," "permit's really not needed for this," "price is good today only," "I need the deposit to buy materials" from an established contractor = cash-flow trouble = your deposit is paying for the *last* job). When the high bid is the bargain.
5. **The contract — clause checklist.** 10–12 clauses; what must be in it: fixed price vs. cost-plus (and when cost-plus with a GMP is legitimate), scope + drawings referenced, start AND substantial-completion dates with a per-day delay figure if you can get it, the payment schedule (worked example for a $60k kitchen: 10/25/25/25/15 tied to named milestones), **change orders in writing with price before work proceeds** (the single most disaster-preventing sentence), permit responsibility on the contractor in their name (owner-pulled permits shift liability to you — never agree), warranty terms (1 yr workmanship floor, manufacturer warranties passed through), cleanup/dumpster/site protection, dispute resolution (cross-link small-claims-court.html; note many state contractor boards run free/cheap arbitration).
6. **Mechanic's liens — the ambush nobody warns you about.** The mental model: **subs and suppliers you've never met can lien YOUR house if the GC doesn't pay them — even though you paid the GC in full.** Defenses by escalating job size: conditional + unconditional lien waivers collected with every payment (define the four waiver types briefly), preliminary-notice tracking (know who's on your job), joint checks (GC + supplier) for big material buys, title-company disbursement for major renovations. This section justifies the page by itself.
7. **Managing the job.** The weekly-standing-meeting habit, the OHIO rule for decisions (contractors' #1 client complaint: slow decisions stall jobs — pick finishes BEFORE demo day), the change-order log, what "punch list" means and how to run one (blue-tape walkthrough, written list, one round), substantial completion vs. final completion and why the distinction controls the last payment, when to bring in an independent inspector mid-job (framing/rough-in for major work — a few hundred dollars against five figures of drywall-covered mistakes).
8. **When it goes wrong anyway — the escalation ladder.** Seven rungs, in order: the documented-cure letter (certified mail, specific defects, deadline), state contractor licensing board complaint (real teeth against license holders: discipline, bond claims), the contractor's bond claim (bond amounts by state — often small, first-come), recovery fund claim, insurance claims where applicable, small claims (cross-link) vs. attorney thresholds, firing a contractor mid-job without creating YOUR breach (the trap: termination clauses, document everything, pay for work actually done).
9. **Common mistakes:** biggest deposit = biggest red flag ignored, choosing on price alone, verbal change orders ("while you're at it…" = the budget killer), paying ahead of work ("never let the money get ahead of the job" — the one-line rule of the whole page), owner-pulled permits, no lien waivers on a five-figure job, being unreachable then blaming delays, firing by text in anger before documenting.

Research: state deposit-cap and licensing-lookup tables (dated) — expect a 50-state licensing-lookup table (50 rows: board + lookup URL + deposit cap where one exists; most states have no statutory cap, say so per row rather than omitting), lien-waiver forms by state (CA's statutory forms as the example), state recovery funds list (~10–12 states maintain one), NARI/NAHB consumer guidance for cross-checking norms.

## Visual design

**Blueprint aesthetic** — full cyanotype treatment: the page as the project's drawing set. (Distinct from nuclear-preparedness, which uses blueprint only as a diagram accent on a light page — here it's the whole identity.)

- Deep blueprint-blue background, white/light-cyan line work and text, title-block styling: each section headed by a **drawing title block** (section name, "SHEET 3 OF 9", revision triangle) exactly like construction documents. A subtle grid/graph texture behind.
- Body text panels in white "paper" cards pinned onto the blueprint (readability first — long text never sits directly on blue).
- **The payment schedule as a Gantt-style milestone bar** (SVG): the $60k kitchen example drawn as milestone blocks with payment releases hanging beneath each — the "money never ahead of work" rule made visual.
- **The lien section gets a hazard treatment:** diagonal caution striping on its title block — the one section styled as a warning within the drawing set.
- Red-flag phrases rendered as **red REJECTED stamps** over quoted contractor lines (rotated stamp styling — the site's third stamp usage, so make it distinct: hollow outline stamp vs. insurance's solid rubber stamp).
- The deal-structure card (section 1) designed as a **permit-poster**: bold bordered notice styling, printable — "post it on the fridge for the duration of the job."
- Checklists (vetting, contract clauses, punch list) as real checkboxes with localStorage, styled as drawing-set revision checkmarks.
- **og:image / shareable artifact:** the 1200×630 social card is the **$60k payment-schedule Gantt** — the "money never ahead of work" visual, unmistakable at thumbnail size on the blueprint ground. The "screenshot this" (and print-this) artifact is the **permit-poster deal-structure card** from section 1 — the block that ends up on fridges.
