# Spec: Stoicism — Practice, Texts, Misreadings

**Target file:** `stoicism-practice-texts-misreadings.html`

**Why this topic:** Stoicism is popular but frequently reduced to productivity slogans, emotional suppression, or "broicism." This cheatsheet should anchor Stoicism in the classical texts while making it practical: dichotomy of control, virtue, impressions, negative visualization, journaling, role ethics, death, anger, grief, and the major misreadings.

## Content Approach

1. **Quick reference:** core concepts, one-line definition, classical source, modern practice, common misuse.
2. **Historical map:** Zeno, Chrysippus, Epictetus, Seneca, Marcus Aurelius; Greek/Roman context; what texts survive.
3. **Core doctrine:** virtue as the good, externals/indifferents, dichotomy/control, impressions/prohairesis, nature/reason, cosmopolitanism, role ethics.
4. **Practices:** morning/evening reflection, premeditatio malorum, view from above, voluntary discomfort, pause before assent, memento mori, journaling prompts.
5. **Emotions:** anger, fear, grief, desire, shame; not suppression but disciplined judgment. Compare with Buddhist pages carefully.
6. **Texts guide:** Enchiridion, Discourses, Meditations, Letters, On Anger, On the Shortness of Life. Include how to read each.
7. **Modern use cases:** workplace conflict, parenting, illness, money, politics, death, online outrage.
8. **Common misreadings:** emotionless robot, passivity, fatalism, hustle culture, individualism without duty, quote-mining, confusing "control" with control-freak behavior.

## Research Sources

Use primary texts in public-domain or reputable translations, Stanford Encyclopedia/Internet Encyclopedia where helpful, academic/classical references for historical claims. Verify quotes exactly and keep verbatim quotation short.
