# Spec: Debt Collection Defense — Your Rights, Their Scripts, the Paper Trail

**Target file:** `debt-collection-defense.html`

**Why this topic:** The most shame-loaded consumer-finance topic, which is why the honest version barely exists — the SERP is debt-settlement lead-gen and credit-repair upsells, both of which monetize the desperation. The actual defense (FDCPA rights, validation demands, the statute-of-limitations shield, the re-aging trap where a $50 goodwill payment can restart the clock on time-barred debt) is legal folklore that consumer attorneys know and nobody compiles, because the people who hold it bill hours and the people who need it are too ashamed to ask. Collectors count on ignorance; this page is the counter-script. Feeds small-claims (consumers can sue collectors — FDCPA statutory damages up to $1,000 + fees) and pairs with scam-defense (debt-collection scams are a top imposter category).

## Content approach

The tone rule, stated to the builder: zero moralizing about how the debt happened — the reader gets a defense manual, not a lecture; one line up front normalizes ("collection is a volume business built on scripts; this page gives you yours"). Organizing spine: **say nothing, verify everything, put it all in writing.** The reader's three assets are the validation demand, the statute of limitations, and the collector's own legal exposure.

Sections:

1. **Quick reference: the first-call protocol.** The do-this-now card: do NOT confirm the debt is yours, do NOT make a "good faith" payment, do NOT give bank details; DO get the collector's name/company/address, DO say the magic sentence ("I dispute this debt and request written validation"), DO log date/time/what was said, DO check the 30-day clock (the validation-notice window starts at first written contact). The one-line warnings: a small payment or written acknowledgment can restart the SOL in many states (the re-aging trap — this page's headline fact), and admitting "yes that's mine" on a recorded line is evidence.
2. **Know your enemy: who's actually calling.** The debt food chain in one diagram: original creditor (not covered by FDCPA — but by state analogs) → assigned collector → debt buyer (bought a spreadsheet row for 4–8¢ on the dollar — why their documentation is often garbage and why validation demands work) → collection law firm (the escalation tier). Plus the fourth caller: **the imposter** — fake-debt scams with real personal data; the tells (pressure for same-day payment via gift card/wire/app = scam, always — cross-link scam-defense) and how to verify a collector is real (state licensing lookups, the mailed notice requirement).
3. **The validation demand — your best weapon.** Reg F's required itemization notice explained (what they must send: original creditor, itemized amount, your dispute rights); the 30-day window mechanics (dispute in writing within 30 days of the validation notice → collection must pause until they verify); **the complete template letter** (certified mail, return receipt; the exact elements: dispute, demand for validation, original-creditor chain of title, cease phone contact if desired) — with a copy button and the folklore note that debt buyers frequently can't produce chain-of-title documentation and drop the account. Second template: the **cease-communication letter** (FDCPA §805(c) — they must stop contact except to confirm cessation or announce specific action; the honest tradeoff: it can accelerate a lawsuit decision, when to use vs. hold).
4. **The statute of limitations shield.** Time-barred ≠ erased (the debt exists; the *lawsuit* dies); the 50-state SOL table (3–10 yr spread, by debt type — written contract/open account/promissory note columns; ~50 rows, dated); **the re-aging section in full**: which acts restart the clock by state class (partial payment, written acknowledgment, new payment plan), the collector script designed to trigger it ("just $5 today to show good faith"), and the counter-script. The FDCPA/Reg F rule that suing or threatening to sue on known time-barred debt is a violation — and that some states require time-barred disclosures. What to say when a time-barred debt is collected: nothing about payment; the SOL letter variant.
5. **What they legally cannot do — the violations list.** The FDCPA catalog with the collector-script examples: call before 8 AM/after 9 PM, call your workplace after being told not to, discuss the debt with third parties (family/neighbors/employers — the humiliation lever, explicitly illegal), threaten arrest or jail ("debtors' prison" doesn't exist for consumer debt — with the honest garnishment/judgment distinction so the reassurance is accurate), misrepresent amounts or legal status, Reg F's 7-calls-in-7-days presumption, social-media contact rules. Each entry: violation → what it sounds like on the phone → what to do (log it). Then **suing them back**: FDCPA statutory damages up to $1,000 + actual damages + attorney fees (fee-shifting is why consumer attorneys take these free — NACA referral), small-claims as the DIY route (cross-link), and the CFPB + state AG complaint as the free pressure move (companies must respond).
6. **If you actually owe it — the honest strategy section.** The page doesn't pretend every debt is beatable: validation confirmed, SOL alive — now what. The settlement playbook: collectors' margins mean 30–60% settlements are routine on bought debt; **everything in writing before paying a cent** ("pay for delete" reality: less common post-Reg F but askable; at minimum "settled in full" language agreed in writing); never give ACH access (pay by check/money order); the tax note (forgiven debt >$600 → 1099-C income, with the insolvency exception in one line); lump sum beats payment plans (plans restart clocks and often lapse into default-rate terms). When debt-management plans (nonprofit NFCC counselors) beat debt-settlement companies (fee-harvesting, credit destruction — the honest warning). The judgment-already-happened annex in brief: exemptions exist (wages/benefits floors, protected federal benefits like SS are exempt from garnishment for consumer debt), vacating default judgments you never knew about (sewer service), link small-claims' collections section from the debtor's side.
7. **Credit-report cleanup.** Disputing collection tradelines (FCRA 30-day investigation clock), the medical-debt rules as currently in force (dated paragraph — paid medical collections removal, reporting thresholds; VOLATILE flag), why paying an old collection doesn't always help the score (scoring-model differences, one honest paragraph), goodwill letters, and the free-tools reflex (AnnualCreditReport weekly pulls, freezes — cross-link the-free-version-exists).
8. **Common mistakes:** admitting the debt on the first call, the $5 good-faith payment on time-barred debt, ignoring a court summons because the debt is old (default judgment revives everything — answer it, SOL is an affirmative defense YOU must raise), paying a "collector" who cold-called with gift-card urgency, hiring debt-settlement firms for what a letter does free, negotiating without written terms, letting shame keep the letters unopened (the unopened-envelope tax: every deadline in this ecosystem favors whoever acts inside the window).

Research: FDCPA text + CFPB Reg F pages (primary), CFPB consumer guides and complaint data, state SOL statutes for the table (verify each — Rule 1 in force), NACA/NCLC consumer-law references, current CFPB medical-debt rulemaking status (dated). Legal-information-not-advice disclaimer once, per Rule 4.

## Visual design

**Certified-mail aesthetic** — the page's thesis is "put it in writing," so the design language is the US certified-mail ritual: envelopes, green return-receipt cards, date stamps, and a paper trail that visibly accumulates.

- Palette: envelope kraft-white and postal gray-blue, with **certified-mail green** (the PS Form 3811 green card) as the primary accent for everything that is a reader action, and a date-stamp red-violet for deadlines and violations. Typewriter-adjacent monospace for the letters, clean sans for guidance.
- **Signature element & og:image: the paper-trail timeline** — the dispute rendered as a horizontal chain of postal artifacts: [their notice] → [your validation demand, green-tagged, day 12 of 30] → [the 30-day clock] → [their proof-or-drop fork]. Envelope and receipt-card iconography as flat SVG; the og:image crop is the chain with the headline "the 30-day window."
- **The two letter templates as physical letters:** typewriter-set documents on white with fold-crease rules, fill-in blanks highlighted green, COPY button, and a "send certified, keep the green card" footer strip on each.
- **The violations list as date-stamp entries:** each violation gets a rubber-date-stamp-styled tag (VIOLATION — §805(b)) in the red-violet ink; the collector-script quote in gray italics beside the counter-move in green.
- The SOL table with a year-bar per state (3–10 yr bars) and a small "clock-restart" icon column flagging which acts re-age in that state class.
- The first-call protocol as a phone-side card (bordered, numbered, big type) designed for one-handed reading mid-call; the debt food chain as a simple four-node flow diagram with the ¢-on-the-dollar annotations.
- Discretion as a design value: no debt-themed clip art, no red alarm styling beyond the stamp accents, neutral title in the browser tab — the page should read calmly over a shoulder.
