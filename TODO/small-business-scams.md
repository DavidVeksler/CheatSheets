# Spec: The Small Business Scam Catalog — What Arrives After You File the LLC

**Target file:** `small-business-scams.html`

**Why this topic:** Forming a business entity puts your name and address into a public record, and an entire predatory mail industry is built on scraping it — the "Certificate of Existence" letters, the $150 "annual report filing service" for a $20 state filing, the labor-law-poster invoices, the fake USPTO renewal notices. These arrive dressed as government mail, timed to the exact week the owner is new, busy, and compliance-anxious. No one aggregates the full catalog because the honest version has no product to sell ("the poster is free from the DOL" earns nothing), and the state-by-state consumer alerts that document each scam are scattered across fifty Secretary of State websites nobody reads. Pure no-byline + cross-source-normalization leverage, and the site's scam-defense-for-parents architecture is proven and reusable — this is the same page for a different vulnerable moment.

## Content approach

Fixed entry anatomy throughout the catalog — every scam runs five fields: **What arrives** (the physical/verbal artifact, described with the actual letterhead phrases used) / **The tell** (the 2–3 giveaways) / **What's real underneath** (the genuine obligation it impersonates, if any — this is the page's value edge: most of these scams work because there IS a real annual report, a real trademark renewal) / **The price vs. the real price** ("$87.50 for a certificate you don't need; the state sells it for $10 if you ever do") / **The response** (toss / verify via the agency's own site / the two that need action). Tone rule inherited from scam-defense-for-parents: zero shame — these letters are designed by professionals to fool busy people, and half the readers arrive having already paid one.

Sections:

1. **Quick reference: the 60-second authenticity test.** The universal checks, in order: (1) find the fine print — solicitation mailers are legally required to carry a "THIS IS NOT A GOVERNMENT AGENCY" disclaimer, and it's always there, small (teach the reader to hunt it — the single highest-value skill on the page); (2) the payee — real state fees go to the Secretary of State via a .gov, never to "Compliance Filings Division, LLC" at a PO box; (3) the deadline pressure vs. your actual filing calendar (link the entry on finding your real due dates); (4) the return address vs. your state capital; (5) when in doubt: go to the agency's website yourself — never the letter's URL or phone number. Plus **the trigger timeline** graphic: file LLC → public record → the predictable mail wave (weeks 1–8: certificate letters, poster invoices, EIN "services"; year 1 anniversary: fake annual-report notices; trademark filing → fake renewal invoices years later).
2. **The mail wave — filing-triggered paper scams.** The core catalog, ~10 entries: Certificate of Existence/Status/Good Standing solicitations (and the honest note on when you genuinely need one — a lender asks — and what the state actually charges); annual report "filing services" (the real report, the real fee, the real deadline — per-state variance noted once with the "check your SoS site" pointer); labor-law poster invoices (posters are free from DOL and your state agency — link the-free-version-exists; the compliance-fear mechanics); EIN filing services charging $200+ for the free 10-minute IRS process; fake USPTO trademark renewal/monitoring invoices (the WIPO-lookalike register scams — real renewal windows explained so the fake urgency loses its power); domain slamming letters ("Domain Registry" transfer-disguised-as-renewal); business directory/Yellow Pages invoice scams (the walking-fingers logo isn't trademark-protected — the classic); DUNS/credit-listing solicitations (what D-U-N-S actually is and the free path); fake business grant/award letters ("you've been selected" + processing fee); workers-comp/insurance "audit" solicitations.
3. **The phone and inbox wave.** ~8 entries: Google Business Profile robocalls ("your listing will be suspended" — Google doesn't call); SEO/web-presence cold calls with fake authority claims; utility disconnect-today calls (the gift-card tell); the toner/office-supply scam (phantom orders + aggressive invoicing — the decades-old classic that still works on anyone with an AP inbox); Business Email Compromise for the smallest teams (the fake-CEO gift-card ask, the vendor bank-detail-change email — and the one-rule fix: **verify any payment-detail change by phone at a number you already had**); payroll-diversion phishing (HR-targeted "update my direct deposit"); fake OSHA/health-inspector visits and calls; invoice fraud generally (PO discipline in one paragraph — the smallest-business version of AP controls).
4. **Legal-shaped threats — the ones you can't just toss.** The section that earns the page its authority, because the right answer isn't "ignore it": **ADA website demand letters** (how the mill model works, what the actual legal exposure is, the wrong responses — panic-paying the "settlement" and ignoring it entirely are both wrong — and the right one: real letter vs. mass-mailer triage, when to involve a lawyer, the accessibility-fix path that moots the claim); **copyright troll image letters** (PicRights/Getty-class — often technically real claims with inflated demands; the response protocol: verify the image use, take it down, negotiate in writing, the license-history defense); **patent troll demand letters** (one honest block: what they are, why small operations are targeted, state anti-troll laws exist, don't respond without counsel); **fake business-debt collection** (link debt-collection-defense's validation machinery — it applies); the triage rule across all four: *legal-shaped mail gets 24 hours and a verification, never a same-day payment.*
5. **Response protocols.** If you're unsure: the verify-independently flow (agency site via your own search, call the number YOU find). If you paid: card chargeback windows, mail-fraud reporting (USPS Postal Inspection Service — mail scams are federal crimes and reports matter), FTC ReportFraud, state AG consumer division, and the honest recovery odds (low for wire/check; the page doesn't oversell). If it keeps coming: the mail-preference realities and why the wave subsides. For teams: the two-line AP policy that stops 90% of it (no payment without a PO or known vendor; all bank-detail changes verified by phone).
6. **Common mistakes:** paying a small fake invoice "because it's only $90" (it marks you as a payer — the sucker-list mechanics), using the letter's own phone number to "verify," ignoring ADA/copyright letters like junk (the two that escalate), assuming email from a known vendor's real address is safe (compromised-account BEC), letting the intern handle the mail unbriefed (the page IS the briefing — closing line), and shame-silence after paying one (report it; the FTC action that killed the biggest certificate-mailer started with reports).

Research: FTC actions and consumer alerts on corporate-compliance mailers, USPS Postal Inspection Service scam catalogs, state Secretary of State scam-alert pages (harvest 4–6 states' alerts to normalize the letterhead-phrase list), USPTO's official warning page on solicitation scams (it maintains a named list — use it), DOL poster pages, ADA demand-letter reporting (state bar and legal-aid summaries). Rule 1: real fee anchors and named operations verified at build; letterhead phrasings drawn from documented specimens, not invented.

## Visual design

**Document-examination aesthetic** — the page as a forensic light table: scam letters laid out as specimens under examination, with annotation callouts marking the tells. The visual promise: *we put the letter under the loupe so you can too.*

- Palette: cool light-table gray-white canvas, specimen documents in warm paper cream with realistic letter typography (serif "official-ish" headers — pastiche, clearly mocked-up, no real operation's trade dress reproduced), and a two-ink annotation system: **examiner red-orange** for scam tells (circles, underlines, callout lines) and **verify teal** for the real-version-exists notes. Clean sans for the page's own voice — the examiner's voice and the specimen's voice must never blur.
- **Signature element & og:image: the annotated specimen letter** — one full mocked-up "Certificate of Existence" solicitation with 5 numbered examiner callouts (the fine-print disclaimer circled, the LLC payee, the fake deadline, the non-.gov address, the inflated fee vs. the real one in teal). Built as one SVG/HTML composite; og:image is this letter with callouts at 1200×630 — it should read as "spot the fake" at thumbnail size.
- **The 60-second test as an examiner's checklist card** — five checks with loupe iconography, sized for screenshot; the print stylesheet renders it plus the trigger timeline on one page (the tape-it-by-the-mail-pile artifact).
- **The trigger timeline** as a horizontal mail-conveyor graphic: filing date at left, envelope icons arriving along the belt with week/year markers — each envelope links to its catalog entry.
- Catalog entries as **specimen cards**: a small envelope/letterhead thumbnail glyph per scam (drawn generic), the five anatomy fields in labeled rows, the price-vs-real-price pair rendered as two tags (red-orange strikethrough price / teal real price) — the page's recurring visual verdict, distinct from the site's stamp and chip systems.
- Legal-shaped threats section visually escalated: amber caution border and a "DO NOT TOSS — 24-HOUR PROTOCOL" header plate, separating them from the tossable catalog at a glance.
- Dark mode: light table goes dark-slate, specimens stay cream (documents keep their paper identity — same rule as the certified-mail page but different document language: examination vs. correspondence). 375 px: specimen letter scrolls in an overflow container with callouts stacked beneath as a numbered list; anatomy cards stack; nothing depends on hover.
