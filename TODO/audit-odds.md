# Spec: Audit Odds — What Actually Triggers an IRS Audit, and the Deductions Fear Is Costing You

**Target file:** `audit-odds.html`

**Why this topic:** Small-business tax behavior is governed by a folklore fear — "the home office deduction is an audit flag" — that is decades stale, while the actual audit-selection mechanics (information-return mismatch, DIF outliers, hobby-loss patterns) go unnamed. The result is a systematic wealth transfer from the fearful to the IRS: legitimate deductions (self-employed health insurance, retirement contributions, home office) skipped to avoid an audit whose real probability is under 1%, while the behaviors that actually draw letters go merrily on. Every CPA article soft-pedals this because "you're over-worrying, here's the EV math" doesn't sell engagements, and TurboTax content can't say it either. This is base-rate correction applied to the reader's own money — actual-risk-dashboard's method pointed at the 1040 — and the IRS Data Book publishes the exact odds table nobody assembles.

## Content approach

The page is one argument in the site's base-rate voice: **the expected cost of an audit is small and insurable by good records; the cost of skipped deductions is large and certain — so fix the two real exposures and claim everything you're entitled to.** Tone rules: zero moralizing in either direction (not "everyone cheats" and not "you must maximize"); the page never advises aggressive positions — its entire thesis is that the *ordinary, clearly-legal* deductions are being skipped out of miscalibrated fear. The EV math is shown once, explicitly, and it's the spine.

Sections:

1. **Quick reference: the odds table.** Audit rates per 1,000 returns from the IRS Data Book (cited by year in the header): overall individual rate, by income band (the U-shape: elevated at EITC-range and above ~$500k, minimal in the middle — shown honestly, including the uncomfortable EITC fact: low-income credit-claimants are audited at outsized rates via cheap correspondence audits, a documented resource-allocation reality the page states plainly and without editorializing beyond one sourced line), Schedule C bands, corporate/partnership context rows, and the **correspondence-vs-field split** — the reframe that defuses most fear: the large majority of "audits" are letters asking about one line item, not agents in your kitchen. Companion strip: **the EV math worked** — (audit probability × plausible adjustment on a defensible return) vs. (the certain annual value of the skipped-deduction stack from §3) — the page's one equation, with honest inputs.
2. **What actually triggers audits.** ~10 entries, each with the mechanism named: unreported information returns (the real #1 — 1099-NEC/K/INT matching is automated; the computer already knows, and the mismatch letter writes itself), DIF scoring explained in one honest paragraph (outlier-vs-peers, exact formula secret, ratios matter more than amounts), losses year after year (hobby-loss: the 3-of-5-years profit safe harbor, what "profit motive" evidence looks like), cash-intensive business codes, round numbers everywhere (the $5,000-even tell), refundable-credit claims (EITC/fuel credits), very high deduction-to-income ratios, ERC claims (the enforcement wave, dated), digital-asset question answered inconsistently, and amended returns claiming large refunds. Then **the retired fears**, parents-threat-model style — each fear / why it's stale / the receipt: the home-office flag (its 1990s folklore origin, the 2013 simplified option as the IRS institutionalizing the deduction, current reality), "e-filing increases audits" (inverse), "extensions flag you" (no evidence, arguably the opposite), "claiming a refund flags you."
3. **The deductions you're skipping — the redirect.** The page's payoff; 12–15 entries, fixed anatomy: **worth** (typical annual $ for a solo operator, dated) / **the actual rule** (plain language, the real boundaries) / **the record you need** (the audit-proofing habit — this field is what converts fear into procedure) / **the fear vs. the reality** (one line each). Entries: home office (simplified $5/sq-ft-up-to-300 vs. actual-expense method compared honestly, the exclusive-use rule stated straight — the kitchen table genuinely doesn't qualify; what does), **self-employed health insurance** (above-the-line, the single most-skipped large deduction — worth thousands), **retirement contributions** (Solo 401(k) vs. SEP-IRA limits, dated — the largest legal reduction available and the least claimed; link cliff-map for the MAGI interaction), QBI/199A 20% (exists until it doesn't — verify status at build, thresholds and SSTB note in two lines, link cliff-map for phase-outs), standard mileage (the rate dated; the contemporaneous-log requirement as the make-or-break — app or notebook, but *during the year*, not reconstructed in March), business meals at 50% (the who/why documentation habit), startup costs ($5k first-year election), Section 179/bonus depreciation (one honest paragraph, not a treatise — Rule 4), phone/internet business-use percentage (the reasonable-method rule), self-employment tax's employer-half deduction (automatic but worth explaining — people don't know it exists), continuing education (the maintains-vs-qualifies-for-new-trade line), HSA (the triple-advantage one-liner, link cliff-map).
4. **What actually gets people — the real exposure list.** The counterweight that keeps the page honest: unreported income (again, because it's most of the ballgame), **payroll trust-fund taxes** (the TFRP block shared with first-employee-checklist — personal liability, no entity shield), personal expenses mislabeled (the bright lines stated plainly: commuting isn't deductible, clothing isn't unless it's a costume/uniform, the family cell plan needs a real allocation), perpetual hobby losses, ERC-mill claims, and "my preparer handled it" (the taxpayer signs; preparer fraud cases land on clients too — the choose-your-preparer line).
5. **When the letter comes.** The crisis section, CP2000 first: **a CP2000 is a proposed adjustment from document matching, not an audit** — the 30-day response protocol (agree/partially-agree/disagree paths, respond in writing, never ignore), then correspondence-audit protocol (respond by mail with copies never originals, stay inside the question asked — the over-sharing warning), field audit = get representation (EA/CPA/attorney, what representation costs vs. what's at stake — the honest threshold), audit reconsideration exists, the statute of limitations table (3 years standard / 6 years for >25% understatement / unlimited for fraud or no return) driving the **records-retention table** (what to keep, how long, the scan-everything default), and the scam cross-check: the IRS initiates by mail, not phone — link small-business-scams.
6. **Common mistakes:** skipping deductions as "audit insurance" (run the EV math — the page's thesis restated once as the closer), round-numbering everything, reconstructing mileage in March, mixing personal and business accounts (the single cheapest audit-proofing move is a separate business checking account — one hour, transformative), ignoring a CP2000 into a Notice of Deficiency, panic-hiring representation for a one-line correspondence letter, DIY-ing a field audit, and buying "audit defense" products without reading the odds table you're now holding.

Research: IRS Data Book (current year — the odds table's spine; TRAC-IRS and GAO reports for the correspondence/field split and EITC-rate documentation), IRS pubs (587 home office, 463 travel/meals, 560 retirement plans, Schedule C instructions), current-year limit announcements (mileage, retirement, HSA), TFRP guidance, CP2000 official page. Rule 1 at maximum force plus the reassurance-tables standard: **every rate, limit, and probability on this page must carry its source and year inline, or the number doesn't ship** — a stale limit on a tax page is the site's accuracy protocol failing at the exact spot readers act on it.

## Visual design

**Green-bar printout aesthetic** — the page as a continuous-feed dot-matrix accounting run: alternating mint/white bar stripes, tractor-feed perforation edges, monospace figures. The nostalgia of the ledger printout carries the page's authority claim (this is what the data actually says) with a wink, and it's visually unclaimed on the site — the insurance page's Berkshire ledger is typeset print; this is machine output.

- Palette: pale mint green-bar stripes on paper white, near-black dot-matrix ink (a subtly stippled/bitmap-flavored display face for headers, clean monospace for all figures), **printer-ribbon red** reserved for the real-trigger entries and the two exposure blocks, and a **highlighter amber** for the skipped-deduction "worth" figures — the marker a human dragged across the printout. Tractor-feed hole strips as thin decorative rails on both page edges (pure CSS, suppressed at 375 px and in print).
- **Signature element & og:image: the audit-odds strip** — the odds table rendered as a dot-matrix density graphic: each income band a row of 1,000 pixel-dots with the audited returns filled in red (most rows: a handful of red dots in a field of gray — the base rate made visceral, NNT's icon-array argument at 10× scale and in printout form, visually distinct from the 100-person grid). Data Book year printed in the strip's header like a job stamp. The og:image is this strip with the title strap; the near-empty rows ARE the message at thumbnail size.
- **Deduction entries as pay-out voucher stubs:** each skipped deduction as a perforated voucher — the amber-highlighted worth figure big at left, the four anatomy fields as printed lines, the record-you-need field boxed (the procedural payload). The running total of the voucher stack prints at the section's end — the free-version-exists tally move, reused in printout idiom: "the stack fear leaves on the table: $X,XXX/yr."
- Retired fears as **voided printout lines**: struck through with a dot-matrix "VOID" overprint and the receipt line beneath — visually rhyming with parents-threat-model's deflated fears without repeating its fog-blue treatment.
- The CP2000/letter section as a **job-separator page**: the burst-page asterisk-art divider dot-matrix printers used between jobs — signaling "different mode: you have a letter, here's the protocol," with the 30-day clock as a printed countdown field.
- The EV equation set in a bordered calculation block, monospace, shown with real worked numbers — the one place the page does math at the reader.
- Dark mode: deep green-black terminal-adjacent inversion (the printout becomes the phosphor screen the data came from — same era, second surface); print stylesheet drops the stripes to keep toner sane and renders the odds table + voucher stack as the handout. 375 px: bars and vouchers stack full-width, the 1,000-dot rows wrap to fixed-width blocks with counts in real text (a11y: every graphic's numbers exist as text — non-negotiable on this page).
