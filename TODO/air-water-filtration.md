# Spec: Air Purifiers & Water Filters — The Spec Math vs. the Marketing

**Target file:** `air-water-filtration.html`

**Why this topic:** Both categories are commodity physics sold as proprietary magic. HEPA is a standard, not a brand; CADR math is public; NSF certifications are searchable — yet the SERP is affiliate listicles ranking $600 units that a $70 box-fan build matches, and water-filter marketing sells reverse osmosis to people whose actual problem is chlorine taste (or nothing). The honest page leads with "test first, then buy for the contaminant you actually have" — advice no seller can give because the answer is often "you don't need one."

## Content approach

One method stated up front and applied to both halves: **name the contaminant → measure it → buy the certified spec that removes it → ignore everything else on the box.** The page is two parallel cheatsheets (AIR / WATER) sharing that spine, each opening with its own quick-reference decision table.

### Air half

1. **Quick reference: what's your air problem?** Decision table: wildfire smoke / allergens / dust / pet dander / odors-VOCs / mold worry / virus season / "general wellness" — each row → what actually helps (HEPA CADR sizing / source control / ventilation / humidity control / **nothing, save your money** for the wellness row) + the spec to look for. Include the two non-purifier answers that beat purifiers: CO2 as a ventilation proxy (a $150 monitor tells you when to ventilate) and range-hood use while cooking (the #1 indoor PM source people ignore).
2. **The only three numbers on the box that matter.** CADR (smoke/dust/pollen, in CFM), room size math worked: ACH = CADR × 60 ÷ room volume; targets ~4–5 ACH for smoke/allergy relief; the worked example (300 ft², 8 ft ceiling → CADR needed) arriving at a real number per Rule 3. Noise at the speed you'll actually run (the dirty secret: units are quoted at turbo, lived-with at whisper — size UP so low speed suffices). Filter replacement cost/yr — the razor-blade model; a $150 unit with $80/yr filters vs. a $300 unit with $40/yr filters, 5-yr TCO table.
3. **HEPA is a commodity — and the CR box.** "True HEPA" (H13 99.95%+) is a standard any honest unit meets; brand premiums buy sensors and aesthetics, not filtration. Then the **Corsi-Rosenthal box**: box fan + 4× MERV-13 furnace filters + tape, ~$70–100, CADR in published testing competitive with units 4–6× the price; the build card (parts list, 15-minute assembly, filter-replacement cadence, the fan-safety note about newer fans). Cite the actual studies (UC Davis, EPA evaluations). Honest cons: ugly, louder per CFM at top speed, no auto mode.
4. **The theater shelf.** Verdicts per the theater-audit method: ionizers/"plasma"/PECO/photocatalytic (weak evidence at consumer scale; some produce **ozone — actively harmful**; the CARB certified-list check), UV wands/in-unit UV (dwell time physics — air moves too fast), "filterless" units, essential-oil "purification," houseplants-as-purifiers (the NASA-study misreading — you'd need hundreds), negative-ion wellness claims. Each: two lines + verdict chip.
5. **Air common mistakes:** buying one unit for a whole floor (per-room physics), running it on low in a huge room and concluding purifiers don't work, paying for smart features instead of CADR, never replacing filters (or replacing "washable" ones' function with hope), purifier as mold *treatment* (fix the water problem), closing up the house forever instead of ventilating when outdoor air is clean (the CO2 monitor closes this loop).

### Water half

6. **Quick reference: what's your water problem?** Decision table: municipal chlorine taste / lead risk (pre-1986 plumbing, service-line uncertainty) / PFAS headline worry / hard water scale / well water / "just want it to be safe" — each row → the test to run first, the filter class certified for it (NSF/ANSI number), and the typical price band. The spine rule stated bold: **your utility's Consumer Confidence Report is free and legally required; read it before spending a dollar** (how to find it; what to look for) — then a $30–150 mail-in test only if you have pre-1986 plumbing, a well, or CCR flags.
7. **The NSF decoder.** The certification numbers as a table — NSF/ANSI 42 (aesthetics: chlorine, taste — what basic carbon does), 53 (health claims: lead, cysts, some PFAS — *per-contaminant*: check the listing, not the logo), 58 (reverse osmosis), 401 (emerging/pharma traces), P473/PFAS certifications — and the critical folklore: certification is **per contaminant**, manufacturers print the logo for a 42 while implying 53; how to search the NSF listing database directly (the 2-minute verification nobody does). Density: 5–6 standards, each with what-it-covers + the gotcha.
8. **Filter formats — the decision table.** Pitcher / faucet-mount / under-sink carbon / under-sink RO / whole-house / softener, columns: what it removes (mapped to NSF classes), cost + cartridge cost/yr, flow tradeoffs, when it's the right answer, when it's overkill. The RO honesty block: 3–4:1 waste-water ratios (better with permeate pumps), removes minerals (the remineralization upsell — taste preference, not health need), slow tank refill; RO is the right answer for *specific* contaminants (arsenic, nitrates, PFAS breadth), not a default upgrade. Softeners ≠ filters (hardness is scale/soap economics, not safety — and the sodium note).
9. **The theater aisle, water edition.** Alkaline/ionizer water (THEATER — stomach acid exists; the evidence review in two lines), "structured"/hydrogen water, **the TDS-meter trick** (TDS measures dissolved minerals, NOT contamination — mineral-rich safe water reads "worse" than distilled; sellers use the meter as a scare prop — this entry is a folklore killer worth its own H2 for the query), bottled water reality (often municipal source; weaker disclosure rules than tap; the microplastics irony; legitimate uses: boil-water advisories, some wells), distillers (work but slow/energy-heavy; fine for CPAP niche).
10. **Water common mistakes:** buying RO for chlorine taste, trusting the logo without the listing, never replacing cartridges (a saturated carbon filter can shed), testing nothing and buying everything, whole-house carbon expecting lead protection at the tap (point-of-use for drinking-water contaminants), softening drinking water for "purity," panic-buying after a headline instead of running the $30 test that answers the actual question.

Research: AHAM CADR directory (primary for air), the CR-box studies (UC Davis/EPA), CARB ozone device list, EPA CCR rules + current PFAS MCL status (dated), NSF listing database (primary for water), CDC/EPA lead guidance. Every performance claim traced to a certification or study, not a product page — per Rule 1 all figures above are anchors to verify.
