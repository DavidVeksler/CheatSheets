# Spec: Auto Repair Decoded — Upsells, Noises, and What Your Car Actually Needs

**Target file:** `auto-repair-decoder.html`

**Why this topic:** The service advisor is a commissioned salesperson and the flat-rate labor system pays technicians by the billed hour, not the clock hour — upselling is structural, not moral. The defensive knowledge (which flushes are theater, which noises are urgent, what the owner's manual actually schedules) is mechanic folklore that never gets compiled because everyone who holds it bills hours. Merges the earlier "mechanic's ear" candidate into one page: the upsell decoder is the offense-defense table, the noise table is the folklore crown jewel, and the warranty-rights section ("dealer service NOT required") kills the single most profitable myth in the industry.

## Content approach

Two anchor principles, stated up front: **(1) the owner's manual is the contract** — the manufacturer that built the engine publishes exactly what it needs, and anything the service menu adds beyond it is a margin product until proven otherwise; **(2) noises are data** — most drivetrain and brake failures announce themselves in a describable vocabulary, and knowing the word for what you hear changes the phone call. The page's credibility strategy is deliberate: it says YES to the unsexy legitimate services (brake fluid, timing belts, transmission drain-and-fill) as loudly as it says no to flushes — a page that only debunks gets dismissed as cynical.

Sections:

1. **Quick reference: the service-menu verdict table.** The spine; density 18–22 rows. Columns: menu item, typical price, verdict (MANUAL SAYS / SOMETIMES / THEATER), the honest interval, one-line reason. The roster: engine oil flush (THEATER — modern oils + no OEM endorsement), transmission **flush** vs. **drain-and-fill** (the distinction row: drain-and-fill per manual = real; pressure flush = controversial, and the high-mileage-never-serviced folklore caveat explained honestly), coolant exchange (MANUAL SAYS — real interval, right spec matters), **brake fluid exchange (YES — hygroscopic, ~2–3 yr / per manual; the row that buys the page its credibility)**, power-steering flush (SOMETIMES — dying category, EPS has no fluid), fuel-injection/induction cleaning (THEATER for port injection with Top Tier gas; SOMETIMES for direct-injection carbon — walnut blasting is a real thing at real mileage), engine air filter (real but 5-minute DIY — the $60-installed markup called out, hold it to the light test), cabin air filter (same, with the glovebox-drop how-to in two lines), wiper blades (DIY minute, 3× markup), battery service/terminal cleaning (mostly padding), "fuel system treatment" bottles (additive theater), nitrogen tire fill (THEATER, one line), alignment (SOMETIMES — after impacts/tire wear patterns, not calendar), tire rotation (real, cheap, often free with tires), differential/transfer-case fluid (MANUAL SAYS at real intervals — the forgotten legitimate one), spark plugs (MANUAL SAYS — iridium interval truth vs. early replacement), serpentine belt (inspect-then-replace, cracking rubric), "3,000-mile oil change" (the founding myth — oil-life monitors and 7.5–10k modern intervals, with the severe-service honesty check: short trips ARE severe service).
2. **Why the menu exists — the two business models.** Brief and unhysterical: flat-rate labor (the tech is paid book hours; a 0.3-hr job billed at 1.0 gives the system its incentives), advisor commissions and menu-sales targets, why dealership service is the profit center that survives thin new-car margins, the multi-point-inspection sheet decoded (green/yellow/red theater — yellow is the sales zone; what "recommended" vs. "required" means on the sheet). The takeaway is calibration: your advisor isn't a villain, but "the inspection found some concerns" is a sales sentence and you can treat it that way. The scare-word calibration ("unsafe to drive" — legitimate for brake-line/steering findings, sales-y for filters and wipers).
3. **The owner's manual is the contract.** The maintenance schedule decoded: normal vs. severe schedules (and the honest note that typical American short-trip driving IS severe), "lifetime fluid" translated (lifetime = warranty lifetime — the transmission-fluid controversy handled with both readings), where to get any manual free (manufacturer sites/NHTSA — link the-free-version-exists), and the one-page **interval card**: the ~10 intervals that matter for a modern car (oil per monitor, brake fluid 2–3 yr, coolant ~5 yr/100k then per spec, transmission drain ~30–60k severe, plugs per manual, **timing belt — THE deadline: the one interval that bricks an interference engine, with the water-pump-while-in-there logic**, filters by inspection, tires by tread/date code with the 6-yr age note). This card is the page's printable.
4. **The noise table — the mechanic's ear.** The folklore crown jewel; density 14–18 rows. Columns: the sound (in plain onomatopoeia + when it happens), probable causes ranked, urgency tier (**STOP NOW / THIS WEEK / SCHEDULE / LIVE WITH IT**), typical repair cost band. Rows: grinding when braking (pads gone → rotors dying — the cost of waiting quantified: $250 job becomes $600), squeal that stops when braking (wear indicators — by design), clunk over bumps (sway-bar links/bushings — cheap, common, oversold as ball joints), single clunk on shift (mounts/driveline lash), click-click on turns (CV axle — the boot-history note), speed-proportional hum that changes with lane-lean (wheel bearing), roar that follows tire rotation (cupped tires), belt squeal at cold start (belt/tensioner), tick at idle that fades warm (often benign lifter/exhaust-manifold expansion — the "aluminum engines tick" folklore), **knock under load (STOP — rod knock, the one catastrophic sound)**, hiss + rough idle (vacuum leak), rattle under car at low speed (heat shield — the gloriously cheap fix that sounds like death), hum/whine from transmission (fluid level first), groan when steering at full lock (normal for some, pump cry for others), rhythmic thump matching wheel speed (tire flat-spot/separation — inspect today), buzz under braking (ABS self-test vs. warped-rotor pulsation distinction). Framing note: probable ≠ certain — the table's job is calibrating urgency and the phone call, not replacing diagnosis; say it once.
5. **Your warranty rights — the myth-killer section.** **Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act: the dealer cannot require dealer service (or OEM parts) to keep your warranty** — independent shops and DIY with receipts preserve it; the tie-in-sales rule, what documentation to keep (receipts + intervals), the one real exception (they can deny a claim if they prove YOUR maintenance caused THE failure — specific, not blanket). Plus the adjacent money: **TSBs and "secret warranties"/goodwill** (known defects fixed free past warranty if you ask right — how to search NHTSA for your VIN's TSBs and recalls, the goodwill-request script), extended warranties = service contracts (verdict lives at insurance-worth-it — link, one line), state repair-shop rights (written estimates, authorization before work, old parts back on request — "verify your state" once).
6. **Buying repairs like a pro.** The decision layer: dealer vs. independent vs. chain decision table (warranty/recall/TSB work → dealer; out-of-warranty diagnosis and most repairs → independent with marque experience; tires/exhaust/alignment → competent chains; the folklore: the independent shop that specializes in your brand is the best value in the industry), parts tiers decoded (OE / OEM-supplier / quality aftermarket / white-box — where each is fine and where it isn't: brakes yes, crash sensors no), the diagnostic-fee norm ($100–200, legitimate, often credited toward repair — and why "free diagnosis" is a sales funnel), second-opinion etiquette (get the finding in writing, decline the repair politely, the estimate is yours), **read your own codes first**: a $20 OBD2 dongle + free app turns "check engine" into a named code before anyone quotes you — flashing CEL = stop driving, steady = schedule (the two-tier rule), and the parts-store free-scan folklore.
7. **Repair vs. replace — the math on an old car.** The framework for the $2,500-repair-on-a-$4,000-car moment: compare the repair not to the car's resale value but to **the cost of replacing the transportation** — any car payment (~$500–700/mo used-average, dated) exceeds almost any repair amortized over months kept; the real thresholds: repeated repairs trending up, rust/structural, safety systems, the one-hoss-shay end state; the "$1,000/yr in repairs is still cheaper than $7,000/yr in payments" arithmetic as a worked example arriving at real numbers per Rule 3; when replacement genuinely wins (reliability need, the repair exceeds 50%+ of a *reliable* replacement's cost, life-stage change). Sub-note: the timing-belt/major-service decision on a car you're about to sell (don't) vs. keep (do).
8. **Common mistakes:** approving the yellow column wholesale, declining brake fluid because this page said flushes are theater (read the verdict column — that's why it exists), the "while we're in there" spiral without written change pricing (the contractor-page logic on wheels), ignoring the flashing CEL, paying diagnosis twice by shop-hopping without the written finding, dealer-servicing a 12-year-old car out of warranty fear, skipping the timing belt on an interference engine you plan to keep, buying the extended warranty at the finance desk instead of reading insurance-worth-it first, and never opening the owner's manual that answers 80% of this page for your exact car.

Research: Magnuson-Moss text + FTC's auto-warranty consumer guidance (primary), NHTSA TSB/recall lookup mechanics, OEM maintenance schedules for 2–3 representative popular models as worked examples (verify against actual manuals), Top Tier gasoline program facts, CPSC/industry data where cited, current used-car payment averages (dated). Rule 1 in force: every price band and interval above is an anchor to verify; the noise table's cause rankings should be cross-checked against reputable technician references, not content mills.

## Visual design

**Repair-order aesthetic** — the page as an annotated shop invoice: carbon-copy triplicate coloring, dot-matrix line items, and approval checkboxes the customer finally controls.

- Palette: invoice white with the carbonless-copy tint trio as section accents (canary yellow, pink, goldenrod — used as subtle panel tints, one per major table zone), near-black dot-matrix-flavored monospace for line items and prices, a rubber-stamp red reserved for verdicts and the STOP NOW tier. Perforation-edge rules between sections.
- **Signature element & og:image: the annotated estimate** — a rendered repair-order invoice (shop-form ruling, line items, $ column) with the page's verdicts stamped per line: APPROVED in green on the legitimate items, DECLINED in red over the flushes, a margin note in handwriting-style annotation on each ("manual says 100k — you're at 42k"). Built to crop at 1200×630; it is the entire thesis in one image. (Fourth stamp usage on the site — differentiate: ink-pad texture, straight not rotated, invoice-context only.)
- **The service-menu table styled as the shop's own menu board** flipped: verdict chips leftmost (MANUAL SAYS / SOMETIMES / THEATER), the THEATER rows tinted like voided lines.
- **The noise table as a diagnostic chart:** each row led by the onomatopoeia in large quotes ("GRRRIND", "click-click-click") with a small waveform-doodle glyph varying per sound type, urgency tier as a color-coded tag (red/amber/blue/gray) — designed so the driveway reader can scan sounds without reading causes.
- The interval card (§3) as a **glovebox card**: bordered, two columns, print stylesheet at 4×6; the page's physical artifact.
- The repair-vs-replace worked example as a two-column tally (repair path vs. payment path) with the monthly totals big at the bottom — the receipt-comparison device consistent with the batch's money pages.
- OBD2 section gets a small code-readout motif (dot-matrix "P0420" in a display panel) — one decorative touch, not a theme takeover.
- All tables 375-px safe with the verdict/urgency column pinned leftmost; anchor ids per row for the waiting-room deep link.
